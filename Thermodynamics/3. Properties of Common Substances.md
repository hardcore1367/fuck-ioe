# 3. Properties of Common Substances

## 3.1 Pure Substance

A system is said to be composed of a pure substance if it satisfies all three of the following conditions:

- **Homogeneous in composition** — The chemical composition is identical in every part of the system. Water and steam together are still homogeneous in composition because both are H₂O regardless of phase.
- **Homogeneous in chemical aggregation** — The chemical elements are combined or bonded in the same way throughout. This means no region of the system has a different molecular structure or a different compound from any other region.
- **Invariable in chemical aggregation** — The chemical composition does not change over time, even as thermodynamic processes (heating, compression, phase change) occur within the system.

## 3.2 State Postulate

Two independent intensive thermodynamic properties are sufficient to completely establish the thermodynamic state of a pure substance. Once two independent properties (such as temperature and pressure) are known, every other property — specific volume, enthalpy, entropy — is fully determined.

## 3.3 Ideal Gas

An **ideal gas** is a gas in which molecules have no intermolecular forces and occupy negligible volume. Real gases behave like ideal gases at **low pressures** and **high temperatures**.

### 3.3.1 Boyle's Law

At constant temperature, pressure is inversely proportional to volume.

$$P \propto \frac{1}{V} \qquad (\text{at constant } T)$$

If pressure increases, volume decreases.

### 3.3.2 Charles's Law

At constant pressure, volume is directly proportional to absolute temperature.

$$V \propto T \qquad (\text{at constant } P)$$

Higher temperature means the gas expands to occupy more volume.

### 3.3.3 Equation of State (Ideal Gas Law)

Combining both laws gives the **ideal gas equation of state**:

$$PV = mRT \qquad \text{(for mass } m \text{)}$$

Or in specific form (per unit mass), which is more common in engineering:

$$Pv = RT$$

Where:
- $P$ = pressure (Pa or kPa)
- $V$ = total volume (m³), $v$ = specific volume (m³/kg)
- $m$ = mass of gas (kg)
- $R$ = specific gas constant (J/kg·K) — different for each gas
- $T$ = absolute temperature (always in **Kelvin**, never Celsius)

## Two-Phase Liquid and Vapor Mixture System

When a substance exists as both liquid and vapor simultaneously, it's called a **two-phase mixture**. This happens during phase change — for example, when water boils, it doesn't all turn to steam instantly. For a period, liquid and vapor coexist.

- The dome-shaped curve is called the **saturation dome** or **vapor dome**.
- The **left boundary** of the dome is the **saturated liquid line** (quality x = 0) — this is where liquid is just about to start boiling.
- The **right boundary** is the **saturated vapor line** (quality x = 1) — this is where vapor is just about to start condensing.
- **Inside the dome** is the two-phase (wet mixture) region where both liquid and vapor exist together.
- **Left of the dome** is the compressed/subcooled liquid region.
- **Right of the dome** is the superheated vapor region.
- The **top of the dome** is the critical point.
- On a constant-pressure line (isobar), as you add heat: temperature rises → flattens out inside the dome (phase change at constant T and P) → rises again in the superheated region.

- The **P-v diagram** plots pressure on the y-axis and specific volume on the x-axis.
- The dome shape is similar to the T-v diagram.
- Inside the two-phase region, **isotherms (constant temperature lines) are perfectly horizontal** — this means pressure stays constant during phase change at a fixed temperature.
- At lower pressures, the dome is wider (phase change spans a larger range of specific volumes).
- Above the critical point, isotherms no longer have a flat section — there is no distinct phase change.

## Terms Used in Two-Phase Mixture

### Saturation Temperature

**Saturation temperature** ($T_{sat}$) is the temperature at which a substance boils or condenses at a given pressure. For water at atmospheric pressure (101.325 kPa), $T_{sat}$ = 100°C. Increasing pressure raises the saturation temperature — this is why a pressure cooker cooks faster.

### Saturation Pressure

**Saturation pressure** ($P_{sat}$) is the pressure at which a substance undergoes phase change at a given temperature. Saturation temperature and saturation pressure always come in pairs — for every $T_{sat}$, there is exactly one $P_{sat}$, and vice versa. You can find these values in the **saturated water tables** (Table A-4 and A-5 in most textbooks).

### Saturated Liquid

A **saturated liquid** is a liquid that is on the verge of vaporizing — add even a tiny amount of heat and it starts to boil. It sits exactly on the left boundary of the dome (x = 0). Its specific volume is denoted **$v_f$** (subscript f = fluid = liquid). Properties at this state are found in the "sat. liquid" column of steam tables.

### Saturated Vapor

A **saturated vapor** (also called dry saturated vapor) is a vapor that is on the verge of condensing — remove any heat and liquid droplets start to form. It sits exactly on the right boundary of the dome (x = 1). Its specific volume is denoted **$v_g$** (subscript g = gas = vapor). Properties at this state are in the "sat. vapor" column of steam tables.

### Compressed Liquid (Subcooled Liquid)

A **compressed liquid** (or **subcooled liquid**) is a liquid whose temperature is below the saturation temperature at that pressure — it's not yet ready to boil. It exists to the left of the dome. For example, water at 20°C and 1 atm is a compressed liquid because its temperature (20°C) is below $T_{sat}$ at 1 atm (100°C).

Since compressed liquid data is often unavailable, we use the approximation:

$$v \approx v_f(T), \quad u \approx u_f(T), \quad h \approx h_f(T)$$

This approximation works because the properties of compressed liquid don't change much with pressure.

### Superheated Vapor

A **superheated vapor** is a vapor whose temperature is above the saturation temperature at that pressure — it has gone beyond the point of complete vaporization. It exists to the right of the dome. Steam in turbines is almost always superheated. Its properties are found in the **superheated vapor tables** (Table A-6).

### Degree of Superheat

The **degree of superheat** is simply how far above the saturation temperature a superheated vapor is:

$$\text{Degree of Superheat} = T_{\text{actual}} - T_{sat} \text{ (at that pressure)}$$

For example, if steam is at 250°C and $T_{sat}$ at that pressure is 180°C, the degree of superheat is 70°C. A higher degree of superheat means the steam is further from the two-phase region.

### Critical Point

The **critical point** is the state at which the saturated liquid and saturated vapor lines merge — the very top of the dome. Above the critical point, there is no clear distinction between liquid and vapor; the substance exists as a **supercritical fluid** and no phase change can be observed no matter how much heat is added.

For water, the critical point values are:
- $T_c = 374.14°C$
- $P_c = 22.09 \text{ MPa}$
- $v_c = 0.003155 \text{ m}^3/\text{kg}$

### Phase Change

**Phase change** is the conversion of a substance from one phase to another — liquid to vapor (vaporization) or vapor to liquid (condensation). During phase change at constant pressure, the **temperature stays constant** even as heat is continuously added. The heat absorbed during this process is called the **latent heat of vaporization**:

$$h_{fg} = h_g - h_f$$

where $h_{fg}$ is the enthalpy difference between saturated vapor and saturated liquid. On the T-v diagram, the flat horizontal line inside the dome at a given pressure represents this phase change process.
