# Natural Language Processing (NLP)
NLP is a field of AI that deals with enabling computers to understand, interpret, and generate human language (natural language like English, Nepali) in a meaningful way.

## 1. Steps Involved in NLP

Consider this sentence throughout all steps: **"The dogs are running fast in the park."**

### 1. Lexical Analysis
The input text is broken down into individual tokens — words, punctuation, and symbols. This is called **tokenization**. Numbers, spaces, and special characters are also handled here.

```
"The dogs are running fast in the park."
→ ["The", "dogs", "are", "running", "fast", "in", "the", "park"]
```

### 2. Morphological Analysis
Each token is analyzed for its **internal word structure** — roots, prefixes, and suffixes are identified and words are reduced to their base form (lemmatization/stemming).

```
"dogs"    → root: "dog"  (plural removed)
"running" → root: "run"  (suffix -ing removed)
```

### 3. Syntactic Analysis (Parsing)
The grammatical structure of the sentence is analyzed — words are arranged into a **parse tree** showing subject, verb, object relationships and checking if the sentence follows grammar rules.

```
Sentence
├── Subject: "The dogs"
├── Verb: "are running"
├── Adverb: "fast"
└── Location: "in the park"
```

### 4. Semantic Analysis
The **actual meaning** of the sentence is extracted. Word meanings are verified in context and ambiguous words are resolved. Ensures the sentence makes logical sense.

```
"dogs" → living animals
"running" → physical movement (not running a program)
"park" → outdoor area (not parking lot)
```

### 5. Discourse Integration
The meaning of the current sentence is interpreted **in relation to previous sentences**. Pronouns and references are resolved using earlier context.

```
Previous: "I took my dogs out."
Current:  "They are running fast in the park."
→ "They" is resolved to "dogs" from previous sentence.
```

### 6. Pragmatic Analysis
The **real-world intent** behind the sentence is understood based on the situation and context — what the speaker actually means, not just what the words say.

```
"The dogs are running fast in the park."
→ Said to a friend: sharing information
→ Said to a dog owner near traffic: a warning
```

### Summary Flow

```
Input Text
    → [Lexical Analysis]      — tokenize words
    → [Morphological]         — find root forms
    → [Syntactic]             — check grammar/structure
    → [Semantic]              — extract meaning
    → [Discourse Integration] — resolve references
    → [Pragmatic]             — understand intent
    → Final Understanding
```


## 2. NLP Problems in Nepali Language and Their Solutions

Nepali language presents unique and significant challenges for NLP due to its complex structure, limited digital resources, and rich morphology.

### 1. Ambiguity
Nepali words frequently change meaning based on context, tone, and sentence position, making it very hard for machines to interpret correctly.
*Example: "काम" can mean "work" or "desire" depending on context.*

**Solution:** Use **context-aware models** like transformers that analyze surrounding words before assigning meaning. Train models on large Nepali corpora to learn contextual patterns.

### 2. Rich Morphology
Nepali is a highly inflected language — a single root word can take dozens of forms based on tense, gender, number, and case, making tokenization and root extraction very complex.
*Example: "जानु" (to go) → जान्छु, जान्छौ, गएँ, गइन् — all different forms of the same root.*

**Solution:** Build a **Nepali morphological analyzer** that identifies root words and their suffixes/prefixes. Rule-based stemming tools specific to Nepali grammar are needed.

### 3. Lack of Digital Resources
Nepali has very limited labeled datasets, corpora, dictionaries, and annotated text compared to English — making it hard to train accurate NLP models.

**Solution:** Develop and publicly share **Nepali NLP datasets** — tagged corpora, wordnets, treebanks, and lexicons. Organizations like Nepali NLP Group and Madan Puraskar Pustakalaya have started this work.

### 4. Code Mixing and Transliteration
Nepali speakers frequently mix Nepali and English in the same sentence (code mixing) and write Nepali in Roman script (transliteration) rather than Devanagari, confusing NLP systems.
*Example: "Malai yo movie ekdam ramro lagyo" — mixed Nepali-English in Roman script.*

**Solution:** Build **code-mixed NLP models** trained on both Devanagari and Roman script Nepali. Use transliteration converters to normalize input before processing.

### 5. Anaphora Resolution
Resolving pronouns and references in Nepali is difficult because Nepali pronouns vary by respect level (तँ, तिमी, तपाईं all mean "you" but differ in formality).
*Example: "रामले श्यामलाई भन्यो कि ऊ ढिलो भयो" — "ऊ" refers to whom?*

**Solution:** Use **discourse-level models** that track entities throughout the conversation and resolve references using grammatical agreement rules specific to Nepali.

### 6. Lack of Standardization
Nepali has no strict standardized spelling rules — the same word can be spelled multiple ways, and dialects vary significantly across regions.
*Example: "विद्यालय" and "बिद्यालय" mean the same thing but are spelled differently.*

**Solution:** Develop a **Nepali spell checker and standardization dictionary** that maps variant spellings to a canonical form before NLP processing begins.

### Summary

| Problem | Solution |
|---|---|
| Ambiguity | Context-aware models |
| Rich Morphology | Morphological analyzer |
| Lack of Resources | Build Nepali datasets |
| Code Mixing | Code-mixed NLP models |
| Anaphora Resolution | Discourse-level tracking |
| Lack of Standardization | Spell checker and normalization |

## 3. Applications of NLP

**1. Machine Translation**
Automatically translates text from one language to another.
*Example: Google Translate converting English to Nepali.*

**2. Speech Recognition**
Converts spoken language into text by analyzing sound patterns.
*Example: Siri, Google Assistant understanding voice commands.*

**3. Sentiment Analysis**
Identifies the emotional tone behind text — positive, negative, or neutral.
*Example: Analyzing customer reviews to determine product satisfaction.*

**4. Chatbots and Virtual Assistants**
Enables machines to understand and respond to human queries in natural language.
*Example: Customer support chatbots handling complaints automatically.*

**5. Text Summarization**
Automatically generates a short summary of a long document.
*Example: News apps summarizing long articles into key points.*

**6. Spam Detection**
Analyzes email content and classifies it as spam or not spam based on language patterns.
*Example: Gmail filtering unwanted promotional emails automatically.*


## 4. NLU vs NLG

| Natural Language Understanding (NLU) | Natural Language Generation (NLG) |
|---|---|
| Machine **reads and understands** human language | Machine **produces** human language as output |
| Input is human language, output is structured data | Input is structured data, output is human language |
| Focuses on **interpretation** | Focuses on **expression** |
| Example: Detecting that "Book me a flight" is a booking request | Example: Generating "Your flight has been booked for Monday" |

### In Short
- **NLU** = Human → Machine *(understanding)*
- **NLG** = Machine → Human *(speaking)*

## 5. Pragmatic vs Phonetic Analysis

| Phonetic Analysis | Pragmatic Analysis |
|---|---|
| Deals with the **sounds** of language | Deals with the **intent and context** behind language |
| First level of NLP analysis | Last level of NLP analysis |
| Converts speech sounds (phonemes) into recognizable words | Interprets what the speaker actually means in a real-world situation |
| Works at the **sound/pronunciation** level | Works at the **situational/contextual** level |
| Does not consider meaning or intent | Goes beyond literal meaning to understand purpose |
| Output is a sequence of recognized words | Output is the true intent of the utterance |
| Example: Converting spoken "dog" into the text "dog" by recognizing its phonemes | Example: "Can you pass the salt?" — understood as a request, not a yes/no question |

### Key Difference in One Line
Phonetic analysis asks **"what sound was made?"** — Pragmatic analysis asks **"what did the speaker mean by it?"**


## 6. Machine Translation in NLP

Machine translation (MT) is the process of **automatically converting text or speech from one language to another** using a computer without human involvement. It is one of the oldest and most important applications of NLP.


### Challenges in Machine Translation

**1. Ambiguity**
Words and sentences in natural language often have multiple meanings. The system must correctly identify the intended meaning from context, which is very difficult.
*Example: "Bank" — financial institution or river bank? Wrong choice produces wrong translation.*

**2. Idiomatic Expressions**
Idioms cannot be translated literally — their meaning is completely different from the individual words. MT systems often fail here.
*Example: "Kick the bucket" translated literally to Nepali makes no sense — it means "to die."*

**3. Grammatical Structure Differences**
Different languages have completely different sentence structures — word order, verb placement, and agreement rules vary greatly, making direct translation difficult.
*Example: English follows Subject-Verb-Object. Nepali follows Subject-Object-Verb — simple word substitution produces incorrect output.*

**4. Low Resource Languages**
Neural and statistical models require massive amounts of parallel training data. Languages like Nepali, Maithili, and Newari have very limited digital text available, leading to poor translation quality.

**5. Cultural and Contextual Nuance**
Some concepts, expressions, and cultural references exist in one language but have no direct equivalent in another, making accurate translation impossible without cultural knowledge.
*Example: The Nepali word "आफ्नो मान्छे" (one's own people) has no direct single-word English translation.*

**6. Handling Pronouns and Formality**
Some languages like Nepali have multiple levels of formality in pronouns (तँ, तिमी, तपाईं all mean "you"). MT systems often fail to choose the correct level based on context.

**7. Evaluation Difficulty**
Measuring translation quality is itself a challenge — automated metrics like BLEU score do not always reflect actual human-perceived quality of translation.
