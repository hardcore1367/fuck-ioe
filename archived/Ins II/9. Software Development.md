# Software Development
Software development is the process of designing, writing, testing and maintaining programs that control and monitor instrumentation systems. It involves defining requirements, choosing appropriate programming models, writing reliable code and testing thoroughly to ensure the system behaves correctly under all conditions.

## 1. Selection and Purchase

## Selection of Software
Software selection is the process of evaluating and choosing the most suitable software that meets the requirements of the organization. The following criteria should be considered when selecting EMI software for the college:

- **Functionality** — software must meet all required measurement and analysis needs
- **Ease of use** — should be simple enough for students and faculty to learn and operate
- **Compatibility** — must work with existing lab hardware, instruments and operating system
- **Reliability** — software should be well tested, stable and from a reputable vendor
- **Support and documentation** — vendor should provide training, technical support and regular updates
- **Cost** — should fit within the available budget including licensing and maintenance fees
- **Scalability** — should be expandable as future needs of the college grow


### Purchase of Software
Software purchase is the formal process of acquiring the selected software through proper channels. The following steps should be followed:

- **Get approval** — obtain approval from college management and finance department
- **Request quotation** — contact multiple vendors and collect price quotes for comparison
- **Evaluate trial** — request demo or trial version and test with actual lab instruments before finalizing
- **Negotiate terms** — negotiate license type (single user, lab license or site license), support terms and update policy
- **Place order** — finalize vendor, sign agreement and complete purchase formalities
- **Installation and training** — arrange installation on lab computers and conduct training for staff and students
- **Maintenance** — keep software updated and maintain license documentation for future reference

## 2. Software Models

Software models define the process and structure followed during software development. Different models suit different types of projects based on complexity, requirements clarity and risk involved. Choosing the right model is important because it affects cost, time, flexibility and reliability of the final software.


### 1. Waterfall Model
The waterfall model is the oldest and simplest software development model. It follows a strict linear and sequential approach where each phase must be fully completed before the next phase begins. The phases are Requirements, Design, Implementation, Testing and Maintenance. It is called waterfall because progress flows in one direction like a waterfall — you cannot go back to a previous phase. This model works best when requirements are well understood and unlikely to change.

Advantages:
- Simple and easy to understand and manage
- Well defined phases with clear milestones
- Good documentation produced at each phase
- Works well when requirements are clear and fixed from the beginning

Disadvantages:
- No flexibility — changes are very difficult and costly once a phase is complete
- Testing happens only at the end, so bugs are discovered very late
- Customer sees the final product only at the end, leaving no room for feedback
- Not suitable for projects where requirements change frequently


### 2. Prototype Model
The prototype model builds a working model of the software called a prototype before the actual system is developed. The prototype is shown to the customer, feedback is collected and the prototype is refined repeatedly until the customer is satisfied. Once requirements are fully understood the actual system is developed. This model is useful when customer requirements are unclear or uncertain at the beginning.

Advantages:
- Customer can see and interact with the system early and give feedback
- Unclear requirements are identified and clarified through the prototype
- Reduces risk of building the wrong system
- Customer satisfaction is higher as the system is built based on actual feedback

Disadvantages:
- Prototype may create unrealistic expectations in the customer
- Developers may try to use the prototype as the final product which leads to poor quality
- Time and cost of building prototype adds to overall development time
- Frequent changes based on feedback can make the project difficult to manage


### 3. Spiral Model
The spiral model combines the structured approach of waterfall with the flexibility of iterative development and adds a strong focus on risk analysis. Each loop of the spiral consists of four phases: `Planning -> Risk Analysis -> Development -> Evaluation`. After each loop the software grows and risks are identified and handled before proceeding. It is best suited for large, complex and high risk projects where requirements may evolve over time.

Advantages:
- Best model for large and high risk projects
- Risk is identified and handled at every stage of development
- Highly flexible — changes can be accommodated at any loop
- Customer is involved throughout the entire development process

Disadvantages:
- Complex and expensive to manage compared to other models
- Requires skilled team with expertise in risk analysis
- Not suitable for small or simple projects due to overhead
- Total development time is longer than other models


**Comparison Table**

| Waterfall | Prototype | Spiral |
|---|---|---|
| Linear and sequential | Build and refine prototype | Repeated cycles with risk analysis |
| Requirements must be fixed upfront | Requirements clarified through prototype | Requirements can evolve |
| No working software until end | Working prototype available early | Working software available early |
| Low flexibility | Medium flexibility | High flexibility |
| Low cost and simple | Medium cost | High cost and complex |
| Testing at end | Testing after prototype approval | Testing in each iteration |
| Suitable for small simple projects | Suitable when requirements are unclear | Suitable for large complex projects |
| Low risk management | Medium risk management | Best risk management |



## 3. Software Reliability

Software reliability is the ability of a software system to perform its required functions correctly and consistently under specified conditions for a given period of time. In instrumentation and control systems, reliability is critical because software failure can cause equipment damage, incorrect measurements or safety hazards.


## Factors Affecting Software Reliability

- **Complexity** — more complex code has more chances of containing bugs and errors
- **Poor requirements** — unclear or incomplete requirements lead to wrong implementation
- **Insufficient testing** — software not tested thoroughly will have undetected bugs in operation
- **Human error** — mistakes made by developers during coding and design
- **Hardware dependency** — software reliability is also affected by the reliability of underlying hardware
- **Environmental conditions** — unexpected inputs, power failures or memory overflow can cause software to fail
- **Poor documentation** — lack of documentation makes maintenance difficult and introduces new bugs


### How to Improve Software Reliability

- **Thorough testing** — test software under all possible conditions including boundary and abnormal inputs
- **Modular design** — divide software into small independent modules so each can be tested and verified separately
- **Code reviews** — have other developers review the code to catch errors early
- **Use proven algorithms** — use well tested and established algorithms instead of writing new untested ones
- **Error handling** — add proper error detection and handling so software recovers gracefully from failures
- **Version control** — track all changes to code so errors introduced by changes can be identified and reversed
- **Good documentation** — maintain clear documentation so maintenance does not introduce new errors
- **Redundancy** — use backup systems or duplicate processing to ensure system continues working even if one part fails


## 4. Fault Tolerance
Fault tolerance is the ability of a software system to continue operating correctly even when a fault or error occurs, without complete system shutdown. In instrumentation and control systems this is critical because even a small failure can cause incorrect measurements, equipment damage or safety hazards.

### How to Achieve Fault Tolerance

- **Redundancy** — duplicate critical components so if one fails the other takes over
- **Checkpointing** — save system state periodically so recovery is possible after failure
- **Error detection and correction** — use checksums, parity bits and watchdog timers to detect and correct errors
- **Exception handling** — write code to handle unexpected errors gracefully without crashing
- **Failsafe design** — system moves to a safe state when fault is detected rather than continuing incorrectly
- **Watchdog timer** — hardware timer that resets the system if software stops responding within expected time


### Rollback Recovery
When a fault occurs, the system goes back to the last saved state called a checkpoint and restarts from there.

- System saves its state periodically at checkpoints
- On fault, faulty state is discarded and last good checkpoint is restored
- Simple to implement but work done after last checkpoint is lost
- Used in databases and critical control systems


### Roll Forward Recovery
When a fault occurs, the system uses a log of all operations to move forward past the fault and reach a correct state.

- System maintains a log of all operations performed
- On fault, logged operations are replayed to reconstruct correct state
- No work is lost but requires maintaining detailed operation log
- Used in database and file systems

| Rollback | Roll Forward |
|---|---|
| Goes back to last checkpoint | Moves forward using operation log |
| Some work is lost | No work is lost |
| Simpler to implement | More complex |

## 5. Software Bugs
A software bug is an error, flaw or unintended behavior in a program that causes it to produce incorrect or unexpected results. Bugs can range from minor display errors to critical failures in control systems.


### Types of Bugs

- **Syntax error** — incorrect use of programming language rules, detected at compile time
- **Logic error** — program runs but produces wrong output due to incorrect logic
- **Runtime error** — program crashes during execution due to unexpected conditions like division by zero
- **Semantic error** — code is syntactically correct but meaning is wrong
- **Performance bug** — program works correctly but is too slow or uses too much memory


### Phases of Bugs

**1. Intent**
The developer misunderstands the requirement and designs the wrong solution. The bug exists in the design before any code is written.
- Root cause is unclear or wrong requirements
- Most expensive bug to fix as it affects entire design

**2. Translation**
Developer understood the requirement correctly but made a mistake while converting design into code.
- Coding mistakes, wrong variable used, wrong formula implemented
- Common human error during programming

**3. Execution**
Code is written correctly but produces wrong results during runtime due to unexpected inputs, memory issues or hardware interaction.
- Examples: overflow, null pointer, race condition
- Difficult to detect as it only appears under specific conditions

**4. Operation**
System works correctly in development but fails in real operational environment due to wrong usage, unexpected environment or incorrect configuration by user.
- Not a coding error but a system integration or user error


### Debugging
Debugging is the process of finding, analyzing and fixing bugs in software. Steps are:

- **Reproduce** the bug consistently
- **Identify** the location of the bug using logs, breakpoints or print statements
- **Analyze** the root cause
- **Fix** the bug
- **Test** to verify the fix did not introduce new bugs


### Software Testing

**1. White Box Testing**
Tester has full knowledge of internal code structure and tests based on the code logic.
- Tests internal paths, branches and conditions
- Done by developers
- Finds logic errors and coverage gaps
- Also called structural or glass box testing

**2. Black Box Testing**
Tester has no knowledge of internal code. Tests based only on inputs and expected outputs.
- Tests functionality from user perspective
- Done by dedicated testers
- Finds missing functions and wrong outputs
- Also called functional testing

**3. Grey Box Testing**
Tester has partial knowledge of internal structure. Combines both white box and black box approaches.
- Tests both functionality and internal paths
- More effective than pure black box testing
- Used for integration and penetration testing

**4. Alpha Testing**
Testing done by internal developers and testers within the organization before releasing to users.
- Done in controlled development environment
- All major bugs are expected to be fixed at this stage
- First phase of user acceptance testing

**5. Beta Testing**
Testing done by real end users in their actual environment before final release.
- Done outside the development organization
- Users report bugs and give feedback
- Final phase before official product release

| Alpha Testing | Beta Testing |
|---|---|
| Done by internal team | Done by real end users |
| Controlled environment | Real user environment |
| Before beta release | Before final release |
| All bugs expected to be caught | Final bugs and feedback collected |
| Developers present during testing | Developers not present |


## 6. Good Programming Practice

Good programming practice refers to a set of guidelines and habits that developers follow to write code that is reliable, readable, maintainable and error free.


**1. Use Meaningful Names**
- Use descriptive names for variables, functions and constants
- Bad: `x = 5` Good: `temperature = 5`
- Makes code self explanatory and easy to understand

**2. Write Modular Code**
- Divide program into small independent functions or modules
- Each module does one specific task
- Easy to test, debug and reuse

**3. Comment and Document Code**
- Write comments explaining what each section of code does
- Document functions with their purpose, inputs and outputs
- Helps other developers and future maintenance

**4. Use Constants Instead of Magic Numbers**
- Bad: `if temp > 100` Good: `if temp > MAX_TEMP`
- Makes code readable and easy to change values in one place

**5. Handle Errors Properly**
- Always check for error conditions and handle them gracefully
- Never let program crash without a meaningful error message
- Use exception handling wherever necessary

**6. Keep Code Simple**
- Write simple and straightforward code
- Avoid unnecessary complexity and clever tricks
- Simple code is easier to read, test and maintain

**7. Use Version Control**
- Track all changes to code using version control like Git
- Allows reverting to previous working version if new changes break the system

**8. Test Thoroughly**
- Test every module individually before integrating
- Test with normal, boundary and invalid inputs
- Never assume code is correct without testing

**9. Avoid Global Variables**
- Global variables can be modified from anywhere causing unexpected behavior
- Use local variables and pass data through function parameters

**10. Follow Coding Standards**
- Follow consistent indentation, naming conventions and formatting
- Makes code uniform and readable across the entire team

## 7. Cohesion and Coupling

### Cohesion
Cohesion refers to how closely related and focused the responsibilities of a single module are. A highly cohesive module performs one specific and well defined task and all elements inside it work together toward that single purpose. High cohesion is always desirable as it makes modules easier to understand, test, debug and maintain. For example a module that only handles temperature reading has high cohesion while a module that reads temperature, controls motor and prints report has low cohesion. High cohesion also improves reusability because a focused module can be used in other systems without carrying unnecessary functionality.

Types range from worst to best: coincidental, logical, temporal, procedural, communicational, sequential and functional. Functional cohesion where a module does exactly one well defined task is the most desirable.


### Coupling
Coupling refers to the degree of dependency between two modules in a software system. Loosely coupled modules are independent of each other meaning changes in one module do not affect others. Low coupling is always desirable as it makes the system flexible, reusable and easy to modify. For example if module A directly reads and modifies internal variables of module B they are tightly coupled and any change in B will break A. On the other hand if modules communicate only through well defined parameters they are loosely coupled and can be modified independently.

Types range from worst to best: content, common, control, stamp and data coupling. Data coupling where modules communicate only through simple parameters is the most desirable.

**Golden Rule**
Always aim for **high cohesion and low coupling** — each module should do one thing well and know as little as possible about other modules. This produces software that is modular, maintainable, testable and reliable.


## 8. Embedded Software
Embedded software is software written specifically to control a dedicated hardware device. It runs on microcontrollers or microprocessors embedded inside the device itself and is designed to perform one specific function repeatedly and reliably. Unlike general purpose software, embedded software is tightly coupled with the hardware it controls and often cannot be easily updated after deployment. Examples include software inside washing machines, digital thermometers, traffic light controllers and industrial sensors.

**Characteristics:**
- Designed for one specific dedicated function
- Runs continuously without user intervention
- Limited memory and processing resources
- Directly controls hardware components like sensors and actuators
- Highly reliable and must work without failure for long periods
- Usually written in C or assembly language for efficiency


## 9. Real Time Software
Real time software is software that must respond to inputs and produce outputs within a guaranteed time limit called a deadline. Missing a deadline in a real time system can cause system failure or safety hazard. Real time software is commonly used in safety critical systems where timing is as important as correctness. Examples include aircraft control systems, antilock braking systems and industrial process controllers.

**Types:**
- **Hard real time** — deadline must be met strictly, missing deadline causes complete system failure. Example: airbag controller, pacemaker
- **Soft real time** — missing deadline occasionally is acceptable but degrades performance. Example: video streaming, audio processing

**Characteristics:**
- Must respond within strict and guaranteed time constraints
- Uses Real Time Operating System (RTOS) for task scheduling
- Priority based scheduling ensures critical tasks run first
- Handles multiple concurrent tasks simultaneously
- Deterministic behavior — response time is predictable and guaranteed
- Examples of RTOS: FreeRTOS, VxWorks, RTLinux
