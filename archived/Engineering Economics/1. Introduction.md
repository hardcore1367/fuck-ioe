## Engineering Economics

Engineering economics, previously known as engineering economy, is a subset of economics concerned with the use and application of economic principles in the analysis of engineering decisions.


## Principles of Engineering Economics

1. **A nearby penny is worth a distant dollar** — money now is worth more than the same money later (time value of money)
2. **All that counts is differences among alternatives** — only compare costs/benefits that differ between options; ignore common ones
3. **Marginal revenue must exceed marginal cost** — only take an action if the extra gain > extra cost
4. **Additional risk is not taken without expected additional return** — higher risk must offer higher reward to be acceptable


## Role of Engineers in Decision Making

- Engineers don't just design — they also **evaluate costs, time, and resources**
- They select the **most economical alternative** among technically valid options
- They prepare **cash flow estimates** for projects
- They use economic tools (PW, AW, IRR) to **justify investments** to management
- Bridge between **technical feasibility** and **financial viability**

## Scope of Engineering Economics

Covers all economic analysis done in engineering projects:

- **Cost estimation** — predict project costs before execution
- **Feasibility analysis** — is a project worth doing? (e.g., building a new road)
- **Comparison of alternatives** — e.g., buy machine A vs machine B
- **Depreciation & taxes** — accounting for asset loss over time
- **Replacement analysis** — when to replace old equipment
- **Break-even & risk analysis** — at what point does a project become profitable

*Example:* Choosing between two pumps — one cheap but high maintenance vs one expensive but low maintenance → EE finds which costs less over lifetime.


## Opportunity Cost

**The benefit lost** by not choosing the next best alternative.

> If you invest ₹1L in machine A, the opportunity cost = return you would have earned in machine B.

- It's never shown in accounting books — it's an **implicit cost**
- Always considered in engineering decisions when comparing alternatives
