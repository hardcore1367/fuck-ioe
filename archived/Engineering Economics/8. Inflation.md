# Inflation — Exam Ready Notes

---

## 1. Define Inflation + Causes

**Inflation** is the sustained increase in the general price level of goods and services over time, resulting in the **decrease of purchasing power of money.**

> Rs. 100 today buys less than Rs. 100 did 5 years ago.

**Causes:**
- **Excess money supply** — government prints more money than economy needs
- **Demand-pull** — demand for goods exceeds supply, prices rise
- **Cost-push** — rise in production costs (wages, raw materials) passed to consumers
- **Imported inflation** — rising prices of imported goods
- **Supply chain disruptions** — shortage of goods pushes prices up
- **Government deficits** — borrowing and spending beyond revenue

---

## 2. Actual Dollar vs Constant Dollar

| Feature | Actual Dollar (A$) | Constant Dollar (C$) |
|---|---|---|
| Also called | Current dollar / Nominal | Real dollar |
| Definition | Amount of money **actually paid** at that point in time | Amount expressed in terms of **purchasing power of a base year** |
| Includes inflation? | ✅ Yes | ❌ No (inflation removed) |
| Interest rate used | Market rate (inflated) | Real interest rate |
| Example | Salary of Rs. 50,000 in 2024 | Rs. 50,000 expressed in 2020 prices |

**Converting between them:**

$$C\$ = \frac{A\$}{(1+f)^n}$$

$$A\$ = C\$ \times (1+f)^n$$

Where **f** = inflation rate, **n** = number of years from base year

**Interest rate relationship:**
$$1 + i_m = (1 + i_r)(1 + f)$$

Where $i_m$ = market rate, $i_r$ = real rate, $f$ = inflation rate

---

## 3. Effects / Impact of Inflation on Economy

**Negative Effects:**
- **Reduces purchasing power** — same money buys fewer goods
- **Discourages saving** — money loses value over time, people spend instead
- **Increases cost of borrowing** — lenders charge higher interest to compensate
- **Creates uncertainty** — businesses struggle to plan long-term investments
- **Hurts fixed income earners** — salaries don't rise as fast as prices

**Positive Effects (mild inflation):**
- Encourages spending and investment (money loses value if held)
- Reduces real burden of debt for borrowers

---

## 4. Demand-Pull vs Cost-Push Inflation

| | Demand-Pull | Cost-Push |
|---|---|---|
| **Cause** | Excess demand over supply | Rising production costs |
| **Triggered by** | High consumer spending, government spending | Rising wages, oil prices, raw materials |
| **Direction** | Demand pulls prices up | Costs push prices up |
| **Example** | Post-COVID spending boom | Oil price shock raising transport costs |

> **Simple way to remember:** Demand-pull = *"too much money chasing too few goods."* Cost-push = *"it costs more to make, so it costs more to buy."*

## 5. Deflation Method of Analysis

Used to evaluate projects **under inflation** by converting all **Actual dollars → Constant dollars** first, then using the **real interest rate.**

### Steps:

**Step 1:** Identify all cash flows in Actual dollars (A$)

**Step 2:** Convert to Constant dollars using:
$$C\$_n = \frac{A\$_n}{(1+f)^n}$$

**Step 3:** Calculate real interest rate:
$$i_r = \frac{1 + i_m}{1 + f} - 1$$

**Step 4:** Compute PW/AW using **real rate $i_r$** on constant dollar cash flows

---

### Example:

- Cash flow at year 2 = Rs. 10,000 (Actual)
- Inflation f = 5%, Market rate $i_m$ = 15%

**Convert to Constant:**
$$C\$_2 = \frac{10,000}{(1.05)^2} = Rs.\ 9,070$$

**Real rate:**
$$i_r = \frac{1.15}{1.05} - 1 = 0.0952 = 9.52\%$$

**Then use Rs. 9,070 discounted at 9.52%** for PW calculation.

---

### Why Use Deflation Method?

> When cash flows are given in **actual dollars** but you want to **remove the effect of inflation** to see the true real return. Both deflation method and regular method (using market rate on actual dollars) give the **same PW answer** — just different paths.
