# Introduction

## 1. Noise
**Noise** is any unwanted electrical signal that gets added to the original signal during transmission or processing, causing distortion and errors in the system.

### Major Sources of Noise
- Power lines, power supplies and voltage regulators
- Motors, relays and solenoids
- High voltage equipment such as welding machines and transformers
- High current equipment such as heaters and industrial drives
- Lightning and electrostatic discharge

### Noise/Energy Coupling Mechanisms
Noise coupling refers to the process by which unwanted electrical noise transfers from one circuit to another. The four main mechanisms of noise coupling are capacitive, inductive, electromagnetic and conductive coupling.

**Principle of Energy Coupling**

Energy coupling is based on the principle that **any changing electric or magnetic field in one circuit will induce a corresponding voltage or current in a nearby circuit**, even without direct physical connection, as described by Faraday's law of electromagnetic induction and Coulomb's law of electric fields.

- A changing electric field induces displacement current in nearby conductors
- A changing magnetic field induces voltage in nearby conductors (Faraday's law)
- The amount of energy transferred depends on the strength of the field, frequency and physical proximity of the circuits

**1. Capacitive Coupling**

Capacitive coupling happens when two conductors are placed close to each other. The electric field between them acts like a capacitor and transfers noise from one conductor to another.

- Higher the frequency, more the noise transfer (because capacitor passes high frequency easily)
- Common in parallel wires
- Usually > 1 KHz
- Remedies:
  - Increase separation between conductors
  - Place a grounded shield between the two conductors
  - Keep signal cables short
  - Reduce source impedance


**2. Inductive (Magnetic) Coupling**

Inductive coupling happens when current flowing through one wire creates a magnetic field around it. This magnetic field induces an unwanted voltage in a nearby wire.

- Higher the current and faster it changes, more the noise induced
- Common when power cables run parallel to signal cables
- Usually > 3 KHz
- Transformers and motors are major sources of this type of noise
- Remedies:
  - Use twisted pair cables — twisting cancels out the induced voltages
  - Keep power and signal cables separate and perpendicular
  - Use shielded cables

**3. Electromagnetic Coupling (EMI)**

Electromagnetic coupling happens when high frequency circuits radiate electromagnetic waves into the air. These waves travel through space and disturb nearby sensitive circuits like sensors and microcontrollers.

- Involves both electric and magnetic fields combined
- Usually > 15 MHz
- Common sources are motors, relays, switching power supplies and RF devices
- Can cause malfunction or wrong readings in microcontroller based systems
- Remedies:
  - Enclose equipment in a metal box called Faraday cage which blocks EM waves
  - Use shielded cables for signal transmission
  - Use EMI filters at input and output lines


**4. Conductive Coupling**

Conductive coupling happens when noise travels directly through a shared wire such as a common ground or common power supply line connecting multiple circuits.

- Also called common impedance coupling
- DC to 10 MHz
- When one circuit draws sudden heavy current, voltage drops on the shared wire and disturbs all other circuits connected to it
- Very common in systems where digital and analog circuits share same ground
- Remedies:
  - Use separate power supplies for analog and digital sections
  - Place decoupling capacitors close to each IC to filter supply noise
  - Use star grounding — each circuit gets its own separate ground path connecting to one central point
 

# Grounding
**Grounding** is the process of connecting an electrical circuit or equipment to the earth in order to provide a common reference voltage point of 0V and to create a safe path for fault currents to flow.

**1. Single Point Grounding**

All ground connections from every circuit meet at one single central point.
- Prevents noise from one circuit travelling through shared ground wire to another circuit
- Used for low frequency and DC circuits
- **Disadvantages:**
  - At high frequencies, long ground wires act as inductors and antennas, picking up noise
  - Impractical for large complex circuits as too many wires meet at one point
  - Ground wire length increases impedance at high frequency

**2. Ground Plane / Grid**

A solid copper layer covering the entire PCB acts as the common ground for all components.
- Provides very low impedance ground path for all circuits
- Reduces EMI radiation from PCB
- Best solution for high frequency and digital circuits
- Grid is used when full plane is not possible — copper grid still provides low impedance return path

**3. Ground Loop**

**Mechanism:**
- A ground loop forms when two or more ground connections exist between two points, creating a closed loop
- This loop acts as an antenna and picks up changing magnetic fields, inducing noise voltage in the ground path
- This noise voltage appears as interference in the signal

**Elimination:**
- Use single point grounding — only one ground path between any two points
- Ground shield of signal cable at one end only
- Use isolation transformers or optocouplers to break the loop while still passing the signal
- Use differential signal transmission which rejects common mode noise caused by ground loops
## 2. Filtering

Filtering is the process of removing unwanted noise from a signal by blocking certain frequencies, modes or voltage levels. Three main approaches are frequency based, mode based and amplitude based filtering.

### Types (Block/Pass)

**1. Frequency Based Filtering**

Filters that allow only certain frequencies to pass and block the rest.

**Low Pass Filter (LPF)**
- Allows low frequency signals to pass and blocks high frequency noise
- Used to remove high frequency noise from sensor signals before feeding to ADC
- Example: RC filter at ADC input

**High Pass Filter (HPF)**
- Allows high frequency signals to pass and blocks low frequency noise
- Used to remove slow varying DC offset or low frequency interference

**Band Pass Filter (BPF)**
- Allows only a specific range of frequencies to pass and blocks all others
- Used in communication systems where only a specific frequency band is needed


**2. Mode Based Filtering**

**Common Mode Filter**
- Common mode noise appears equally on both signal lines with respect to ground
- Common mode choke (inductor on both lines) blocks this noise
- Used in power supply lines and data cables

**Differential Mode Filter**
- Differential mode noise appears between the two signal lines
- Capacitor placed between the two lines filters this noise
- Used to clean signal between transmitter and receiver


**3. Amplitude Based Filtering (Surge Suppression)**

Protects circuits from sudden high voltage spikes caused by lightning, switching or electrostatic discharge.

- **TVS Diode (Transient Voltage Suppressor)** — clamps voltage spike instantly, fastest response
- **MOV (Metal Oxide Varistor)** — absorbs energy of voltage spike, used in power lines
- **Zener Diode** — clamps voltage at a fixed level, used for low energy spikes
- All these devices are connected in parallel with the circuit to divert excess voltage to ground

### Types

**1. Minimize Bandwidth**

Reducing the bandwidth of a system to only what is needed so that high frequency noise outside the signal range is automatically rejected.
- Narrower bandwidth = less noise enters the system
- Achieved using LPF at ADC input or limiting amplifier bandwidth
- Rule: bandwidth should be just enough to pass the signal, nothing more


**2. Ferrite Bead**

A ferrite bead is a passive component placed in series on a power or signal line that passes low frequency signals normally but absorbs and dissipates high frequency noise as heat.
- Acts as a frequency dependent resistor
- No effect on DC or low frequency signals
- Effective against high frequency EMI on supply lines and signal cables
- Commonly used on USB lines, power pins of ICs and motor drive lines


**3. Decoupling Capacitors**

A decoupling capacitor is a capacitor placed between the power supply pin (VCC) and ground (GND) of an IC or circuit, as close as possible to the IC. It acts as a local energy reservoir and filters out high frequency noise on the supply line.


**How it works**
- When an IC switches, it demands sudden burst of current from supply
- This sudden demand causes voltage dip on supply line which appears as noise to other ICs
- Decoupling capacitor stores charge locally and supplies this sudden current instantly
- This prevents the voltage dip from propagating to other parts of the circuit

**Uses**
- Filters high frequency noise and glitches on power supply lines
- Stabilizes supply voltage during sudden current demands of digital ICs
- Prevents one IC from disturbing another through shared supply line
- Essential in every microcontroller, ADC and high speed digital circuit


**Placement Rule**

- Must be placed as close as possible to the VCC and GND pins of the IC
- Long traces between capacitor and IC reduce its effectiveness due to parasitic inductance


**Effect of Too Large Capacitor**

Using a very large capacitor as decoupling capacitor causes the following problems:
- **Slow response** — large capacitor takes more time to charge and discharge, making it unable to respond to fast high frequency noise
- **Resonance problem** — combination of large capacitance and PCB trace inductance creates a resonant circuit which can amplify noise at resonant frequency instead of filtering it
- **Ineffective at high frequency** — large capacitors have high parasitic inductance (ESL) which makes them behave as inductors at high frequency, blocking instead of filtering
- **Inrush current** — at power on, large capacitor draws heavy inrush current which can stress the power supply


**4. Line filters, isolators, transient suppressors**


**Line Filters**

A line filter is a filter connected at the power entry point of equipment to block conducted noise from entering or leaving through the power line.

- Blocks both common mode and differential mode noise on AC/DC supply lines
- Consists of combination of inductors and capacitors (LC filter / Pi filter)
- Prevents noise generated inside equipment from going back to mains and disturbing other equipment
- Also prevents external mains noise from entering sensitive circuits
- Commonly used at power supply input of industrial equipment and computers


**Isolators**

An isolator is a device that provides electrical isolation between two circuits while still allowing signal or power to pass between them.

- Breaks the direct electrical connection, eliminating ground loops and common mode noise
- **Optocoupler** — uses light (LED + phototransistor) to transfer signal without electrical connection, most common
- **Isolation transformer** — transfers power/signal magnetically, blocks DC and common mode noise
- **Digital isolator** — uses capacitive or magnetic coupling for high speed digital signal isolation
- Used between microcontroller and high voltage circuits, industrial sensors and control systems


**Transient Suppressors**

Transient suppressors are devices that protect circuits from sudden short duration high voltage spikes caused by lightning, switching or electrostatic discharge.

- **TVS Diode** — fastest response, clamps voltage spike to safe level instantly, used for sensitive ICs
- **MOV (Metal Oxide Varistor)** — absorbs large energy spikes, used at power line entry, degrades over time with repeated use
- **Zener Diode** — clamps at fixed voltage, used for small low energy spikes
- **Gas Discharge Tube (GDT)** — handles very high energy spikes like lightning surges, slowest response
- All connected in parallel with the circuit to divert excess voltage safely to ground


# Shielding

Shielding is the process of enclosing a circuit, cable or equipment inside a conducting material to prevent external noise from entering or internal noise from escaping. It works by blocking or absorbing electric and magnetic fields.


**Shielding Mechanisms**
Shielding is the process of enclosing a circuit, cable or equipment inside a conducting or magnetic material to prevent external noise from entering or internal noise from escaping.

---

**1. Electrostatic Shielding**
Electrostatic shielding protects a circuit from external electric fields and capacitive (electrostatic) noise coupling.

- A grounded conducting enclosure surrounds the circuit and absorbs the electric field
- The electric field terminates on the outer surface of the shield and does not penetrate inside
- Shield must be properly grounded — without grounding it has no effect
- Used in coaxial cables where outer braid acts as electrostatic shield
- Also used in shielded signal cables to prevent capacitive pickup from nearby wires

---

**2. Electromagnetic Shielding**
Electromagnetic shielding protects a circuit from high frequency electromagnetic radiation (EMI) that travels through air.

- A completely enclosed metal enclosure blocks both electric and magnetic field components of EM wave
- This enclosure is called a **Faraday cage**
- Works by reflecting and absorbing the EM wave at the surface of the metal
- Effectiveness depends on conductivity of material, thickness and frequency of noise
- Higher frequency noise is easier to shield than lower frequency
- Used for sensitive instruments, RF circuits and microcontroller enclosures
- Aluminum and copper are commonly used materials due to high conductivity

---

**3. Magnetic Shielding**
Magnetic shielding protects against low frequency magnetic fields and inductive coupling.

- Harder to achieve than electric or EM shielding
- Uses high permeability materials such as mu-metal or silicon steel to redirect magnetic field lines around the protected circuit
- The magnetic field follows the low reluctance path through the shield material instead of passing through the circuit
- Used around transformers, inductors and CRT displays
- Twisted pair cables provide practical magnetic shielding for signal cables by cancelling induced voltages

---

**General Rules for Effective Shielding**
- Shield must always be properly grounded to be effective
- For signal cables, ground the shield at one end only to avoid ground loops
- No gaps or holes in the shield — even small openings allow high frequency noise to enter
- Use higher conductivity material (copper, aluminum) for better electrostatic and EM shielding
- Use high permeability material (mu-metal) for magnetic shielding
- Shielded enclosure must have filtered openings for cables entering or leaving

**ESD (Electrostatic Discharge)**
ESD is the sudden flow of static electricity between two objects at different electric potentials when they come into contact or come close enough for a spark to jump. Even a small ESD event invisible to the human eye can permanently damage sensitive electronic components like ICs and microcontrollers.

---

**Why it is dangerous**
- Human body can accumulate thousands of volts of static charge just by walking on carpet
- A discharge of even 100V can damage a CMOS IC permanently
- Damage may not be immediately visible but causes latent failure later during operation

---

**Protection Measures**
- **TVS Diode** — placed at input/output pins to clamp ESD spike to safe voltage
- **Series resistor** — placed at input pins to limit current during ESD event
- **ESD protection diodes** — built into most modern ICs at every pin
- **Decoupling capacitors** — help absorb small ESD transients on supply lines
- **Proper grounding** — ensures static charge is safely discharged to ground

---

**Workplace Prevention**
- **Anti-static wrist strap** — worn by technician and connected to ground to continuously drain static charge from body
- **ESD mat** — placed on workbench and grounded to drain static charge from components placed on it
- **ESD safe packaging** — components stored and transported in anti-static bags (metallic pink/silver bags)
- **Humidity control** — maintaining humidity above 40% reduces static charge buildup
- **ESD safe clothing** — wear anti-static lab coat, avoid synthetic fabrics which generate static
- **No unnecessary touching** — always handle PCBs and ICs by edges, never touch pins or traces directly

**General Rules for Grounding and Shielding in Circuit Design**

---

**Grounding Rules**

- **Star grounding** — all ground connections meet at a single central point, preventing noise from one circuit from affecting another through shared ground path
- **Separate analog and digital grounds** — analog and digital circuits should have separate ground planes joined at only one point to prevent digital switching noise from corrupting analog signals
- **Keep ground traces wide and short** — wide short traces have low impedance, reducing voltage drops and noise on ground line
- **Avoid ground loops** — do not create multiple ground paths between two points as this forms a loop which acts as antenna and picks up magnetic noise
- **Ground the shield at one end only** — for signal cables, grounding shield at both ends creates a ground loop
- **Single point ground for low frequency** — use star topology for DC and low frequency circuits
- **Ground plane for high frequency** — use solid copper ground plane on PCB for high frequency and digital circuits as it provides lowest impedance path

---

**Shielding Rules**

- **Always ground the shield** — ungrounded shield has no effect on electric field noise
- **No gaps or holes** — even small openings allow high frequency EMI to enter the enclosure
- **Use correct material** — copper or aluminum for electric and EM shielding, mu-metal for magnetic shielding
- **Shield signal cables** — use shielded cables for all sensitive analog signal connections
- **Filter cable entries** — all cables entering a shielded enclosure should pass through EMI filters to prevent noise entering along the cable
- **Keep sensitive circuits away from noise sources** — physical separation is the first and simplest form of shielding
