# Chapter 8 — Circuit Layout — Exam Notes

## 1. Routing Signal Traces on PCB

Routing means drawing the copper paths (traces) on a PCB that connect components together. It is one of the most important steps in PCB design. Poor routing causes noise, signal loss, and interference, so several factors must be carefully considered.

**Key Points — Trace Density:**
- Trace density = how many traces are packed in a given area
- Higher density = more chances of crosstalk and interference
- Keep traces as spread out as possible
- High density may require multi-layer PCB

**Key Points — Common Impedance:**
- When two signals share the same return path, their currents mix — this is common impedance problem
- Causes noise and interference between signals
- Solution: give each signal its own dedicated return path
- Use separate ground planes to avoid sharing

**Key Points — Transmission Line Concerns:**
- At high frequencies, PCB traces behave like transmission lines
- Impedance must be controlled (usually 50Ω)
- Mismatched impedance causes reflections and signal distortion
- Keep traces short and use proper termination

**Key Points — Return Paths:**
- Every signal current needs a return path back to source
- Return path should be directly below the signal trace (on ground plane)
- Broken or long return paths cause noise and EMI
- Never cut slots in ground plane under signal traces

## 2. Crosstalk

Crosstalk is unwanted interference between two nearby signal traces on a PCB. When current flows through one trace, it creates an electromagnetic field that induces a small unwanted signal in the neighboring trace. This corrupts the signal in the neighboring trace.

**Key Points — Definition:**
- Unwanted signal coupling between two adjacent traces
- Caused by electromagnetic fields from one trace affecting another
- More severe at high frequencies and with longer parallel traces

**Key Points — Reduction Guidelines:**
- Increase spacing between parallel traces (most effective method)
- Keep parallel trace lengths as short as possible
- Route traces on adjacent layers perpendicular to each other (not parallel)
- Use ground traces/planes between signal traces as shields
- Use differential signaling for sensitive signals
- Reduce trace length running parallel to each other
- Use proper termination to reduce signal reflections that worsen crosstalk

## 3. PCB Types

A PCB (Printed Circuit Board) is a board that holds and connects electronic components using copper traces. There are different types based on how many layers of copper they have.

**Key Points — Single-Sided PCB:**
- Copper traces on one side only
- Simple and cheap to manufacture
- Used in simple, low-cost electronics (toys, simple gadgets)
- Limited routing space, not suitable for complex circuits

**Key Points — Double-Sided PCB:**
- Copper traces on both sides (top and bottom)
- Components can be placed on both sides
- More routing space than single-sided
- Connected between layers using vias (small holes with copper)
- Used in moderate complexity circuits

**Key Points — Multi-Layer PCB:**
- Three or more copper layers (e.g., 4, 6, 8 layers)
- Inner layers used for power and ground planes
- Very high routing density
- Better signal integrity and lower noise
- Used in complex, high-speed electronics (computers, phones)
- More expensive to manufacture
- Advantages: dedicated ground planes, less EMI, better power distribution

## 4. Component Placement Rules and Guidelines

Component placement means deciding where to physically place each component on the PCB before routing. Good placement makes routing easier, improves performance, and reduces noise.

**Key Points — Rules and Guidelines:**
- Place components in functional groups (keep related components together)
- Place decoupling capacitors as close as possible to IC power pins
- Keep high-speed components away from sensitive analog components
- Place connectors at edges of the board for easy access
- Keep heat-generating components away from heat-sensitive ones
- Orient components consistently for easier soldering and inspection
- Place components on the same side where possible (reduces manufacturing cost)
- Consider mechanical constraints — keep away from mounting holes and edges
- Place crystal/oscillator close to the IC it connects to
- Keep analog and digital sections separated on the board

## 5. Ground, Returns and Shields in PCB

Grounding in PCB means providing a common reference point and return path for all signals and power. Poor grounding is one of the most common causes of noise and interference in PCB circuits.

**Key Points — Ground and Returns:**
- Ground plane = a full copper layer dedicated to ground
- Provides low impedance return path for all signals
- Reduces EMI and noise significantly
- Signal return current flows directly below the signal trace on the ground plane
- Never split or cut the ground plane unnecessarily

**Key Points — Shields:**
- Shielding means surrounding sensitive traces or components with ground
- Guard traces (ground traces placed beside signal traces) act as shields
- Metal enclosures around PCB also provide shielding from external interference

**Key Points — Grounding in Multi-Layer PCB:**
- Dedicated inner layer used purely as ground plane
- Every signal trace has ground plane directly above or below it
- Provides very low impedance return path
- Reduces crosstalk between layers
- Power and ground layers are placed adjacent to each other for good decoupling
- Multiple ground vias used to connect ground plane to all layers properly

## 6. Impedance Mismatch Problems in PCB Trace Routing

Impedance mismatch occurs when the impedance of a PCB trace does not match the impedance of the source or load connected to it. At high frequencies this causes serious signal problems.

**Key Points — Problems Caused:**
- Signal reflections — part of signal bounces back toward source
- Ringing — unwanted oscillations on the signal
- Signal distortion and incorrect logic levels
- Reduced signal amplitude at receiver
- Increased EMI radiation

**Key Points — Causes of Mismatch:**
- Trace width changes along the route
- Vias add impedance discontinuities
- Stubs (unterminated branches) on traces
- Layer changes in multi-layer PCB
- Components with wrong termination

**Key Points — Solutions:**
- Maintain consistent trace width throughout routing
- Use controlled impedance traces (calculate correct width for 50Ω)
- Add termination resistors at end of traces
- Minimize use of vias and stubs
- Avoid sharp bends in traces (use 45° angles)

## 7. PCB — Definition and Advantages

A PCB (Printed Circuit Board) is a flat board made of insulating material (like fiberglass) with copper traces printed/etched on it to electrically connect electronic components. Components are soldered onto the board.

**Key Points — Advantages of PCB over Point-to-Point Wiring:**
- More compact and organized layout
- Consistent and repeatable connections — no human wiring errors
- Easier to mass produce at low cost
- Easier to test and troubleshoot
- Mechanically stronger and more reliable connections
- Better signal integrity due to controlled trace geometry
- Supports complex circuits with many connections
- Easier to replace/repair components
- Professional appearance and smaller size

## 8. Clock Jitter in PCB

Clock jitter means the clock signal does not arrive at exactly the same time every cycle — it varies slightly. Ideally a clock should be perfectly periodic, but in reality small timing variations (jitter) occur. This causes timing errors in digital circuits, especially at high speeds.

**Key Points — Sources of Clock Jitter:**
- Power supply noise affecting the clock oscillator
- Crosstalk from nearby signal traces coupling into clock line
- Thermal noise in components
- Poor PCB layout of clock traces (long traces, vias, sharp bends)
- Phase-locked loop (PLL) noise
- Electromagnetic interference (EMI) picked up by clock trace

**Key Points — Remedies:**
- Route clock traces as short and direct as possible
- Keep clock traces away from noisy signal traces
- Use ground plane directly under clock trace for shielding
- Use series termination resistor on clock trace
- Use a clean, well-decoupled power supply for clock circuit
- Avoid vias in clock trace path where possible
- Use differential clock signals for better noise immunity
- Shield clock trace with ground guard traces on both sides
