# Data Acquisition And Transmission

## 1. Analog Signals

An **analog signal** is a continuous signal whose value can take any real value within a range at any point in time. It directly represents a physical quantity — for example, a microphone converts sound pressure into a continuously varying voltage.

## 2. Digital Signals

A **digital signal** is a discrete signal that takes only a finite set of values — most commonly two levels: **HIGH (1)** and **LOW (0)**. These correspond to voltage ranges rather than exact voltages (e.g., 0–0.8 V = LOW, 2–5 V = HIGH in TTL logic).

## 3. Analog vs Digital 

| **Analog** | **Digital** |
|---|---|
| Continuous in time and amplitude | Discrete in time and amplitude |
| Infinite possible values | Finite values (typically 2: 0 and 1) |
| Susceptible to noise accumulation | Highly noise-immune |
| No conversion needed for natural signals | Requires ADC/DAC for real-world interfacing |
| Degrades over distance | Can be regenerated perfectly |
| Hard to store and process accurately | Easy to store, copy, and process |
| Example: audio waveform, thermocouple | Example: UART data, digital audio, Ethernet |

## 4. Analog Communication System

An **analog communication system** transmits information in analog form from a source to a destination over a communication channel. It consists of several functional blocks, each performing a specific role in the transmission process.

### Components

**1. Source**
The origin of the information to be transmitted — for example, a person speaking, a sensor reading, or a music player.

**2. Input Transducer**
Converts the source information (a non-electrical signal like sound or light) into an **electrical analog signal**. Example: a microphone converts sound into voltage variations.

**3. Transmitter**
Processes the electrical signal for transmission. This includes **modulation** — combining the message signal with a high-frequency **carrier wave** so it can travel long distances efficiently.

**4. Communication Channel**
The physical medium through which the modulated signal travels. Examples: copper wire, coaxial cable, optical fiber, or free space (air). It is the path connecting transmitter to receiver.

**5. Noise**
Unwanted disturbances that get added to the signal while it travels through the channel. Noise is unavoidable and degrades signal quality — it is the primary enemy of analog communication.

**6. Receiver**
Picks up the signal from the channel and reverses the transmitter's work — primarily **demodulation** — to extract the original message signal from the carrier.

**7. Output Transducer**
Converts the recovered electrical signal back into its original non-electrical form. Example: a speaker converts voltage variations back into sound.

**8. Destination**
The final recipient of the transmitted information — a person, a device, or a system.


## 5. Digital Communication System

A **digital communication system** transmits information in digital (binary) form from source to destination. Compared to analog, it adds encoding and decoding stages to improve efficiency and error resistance.


<img width="500" height="fit-content" style="object-fit: contain;"  alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/014d7ab1-5126-4421-bba2-8b9633f8a5dd" />


## Components

**1. Source**
The origin of information — voice, video, text, sensor data, etc.

**2. Source Encoder**
Converts the source output into a binary bit stream and **removes redundancy** to compress the data. This improves transmission efficiency. Example: converting audio into compressed digital samples.

**3. Channel Encoder**
Adds **controlled redundancy** (extra bits) back into the bit stream for **error detection and correction**. This protects the data against channel noise. Example: adding parity bits or using Hamming code.

**4. Channel Modulator**
Converts the digital bit stream into an analog signal suitable for transmission over the channel. Maps bits to signal waveforms using schemes like **ASK, FSK, or PSK**.

**5. Channel (+ Noise)**
The physical medium carrying the signal — wire, fiber, or free space. **Noise** is added here, corrupting the signal during transit.

**6. Channel Demodulator**
Receives the noisy analog signal and recovers the digital bit stream by reversing the modulation process.

**7. Channel Decoder**
Uses the redundant bits added by the channel encoder to **detect and correct errors** introduced by the channel noise.

**8. Source Decoder**
Reverses the source encoding — reconstructs the original information from the decoded bit stream.

**9. Destination**
The final recipient of the transmitted information.


## 6. Optical Fibre

An **optical fibre** is a thin, flexible strand of glass or plastic that transmits data as pulses of light using the principle of **total internal reflection**. It is the backbone of modern high-speed communication networks due to its extremely high bandwidth and low signal loss.

### Structure

An optical fibre has three layers:
- **Core** — the innermost part where light travels.
- **Cladding** — surrounds the core; has a lower refractive index to cause total internal reflection.
- **Jacket** — outer protective coating.

### Types Based on Number of Modes

| **Single-Mode Fibre (SMF)** | **Multi-Mode Fibre (MMF)** |
|---|---|
| Very narrow core (~8–10 µm) | Wider core (~50–62.5 µm) |
| Only one light path (mode) travels | Multiple light paths travel simultaneously |
| Less **modal dispersion**, higher bandwidth | More modal dispersion, lower bandwidth |
| Used for long-distance communication | Used for short-distance communication |
| More expensive | Less expensive |

### Types Based on Refractive Index Profile

| **Step-Index Fibre** | **Graded-Index Fibre** |
|---|---|
| Refractive index changes abruptly at core-cladding boundary | Refractive index decreases gradually from core centre outward |
| Higher dispersion | Lower dispersion |
| Simpler to manufacture | More complex but better performance |

### Characteristics
- Operates on the principle of **total internal reflection** — light reflects entirely within the core when the angle of incidence exceeds the **critical angle**.
- Extremely high **bandwidth** — can carry terabits per second.
- Very low **attenuation** (signal loss) compared to copper — signal travels much farther without repeaters.
- Immune to **electromagnetic interference (EMI)**.
- Lightweight and thin despite high capacity.

### Advantages over Copper Wire

| **Optical Fibre** | **Copper Wire** |
|---|---|
| Much higher bandwidth | Limited bandwidth |
| Very low signal attenuation | Higher attenuation, needs more repeaters |
| Immune to EMI and crosstalk | Susceptible to EMI and crosstalk |
| More secure — does not radiate signal | Signal can be tapped more easily |
| Lighter and thinner | Heavier and bulkier |
| No risk of electrical sparks | Risk of sparks in hazardous environments |

### Why Optical Fibre is in High Demand
- Explosion of internet traffic, video streaming, and cloud services requires massive bandwidth that copper cannot provide.
- 5G backhaul and data centre interconnects depend heavily on fibre.
- Governments and ISPs are deploying **FTTH (Fibre to the Home)** to replace copper last-mile connections.

### Applications
- **Internet backbone** and long-haul telecommunication networks.
- **Cable TV (CATV)** distribution systems.
- **LAN (Local Area Networks)** in campuses and data centres.
- **Medical** — endoscopes and surgical imaging equipment.
- **Military and aerospace** — secure, EMI-immune communication links.
- **Sensor systems** — temperature, pressure, and strain sensing.

## 7. Satellite Communication

A **satellite communication system** uses an artificial satellite orbiting the Earth as a relay station to transmit signals between ground stations. It enables long-distance communication over areas where laying cables is impractical — oceans, mountains, remote regions.

<img width="500" height="fit-content" style="object-fit: contain;"  alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/95f9d7e3-5db8-4421-a23e-92897f54e9a7" />


### Components

**1. Space Segment (Satellite)**
The satellite itself, orbiting in space. Contains a **transponder** that receives the uplink signal, amplifies it, shifts its frequency, and retransmits it as a downlink signal.

**2. Ground Segment (Earth Station)**
The ground-based equipment that communicates with the satellite. Consists of a **dish antenna**, transmitter, receiver, and signal processing equipment. There are two types — **uplink station** (transmits to satellite) and **downlink station** (receives from satellite).

**3. Control Station**
Monitors and controls the satellite's position, orientation, and health. Sends **telemetry, tracking, and command (TT&C)** signals to keep the satellite on its correct orbital path.

## Uplink and Downlink Frequencies

- **Uplink** is the transmission from Earth station **to** the satellite.
- **Downlink** is the transmission from satellite **to** the Earth station.
- Uplink frequency is always **higher** than downlink frequency to compensate for greater transmission power available on the ground.


### Advantages

- Covers very large geographic areas including oceans and remote regions with a single satellite.
- Independent of ground infrastructure — useful in disaster recovery and military operations.
- Supports **broadcasting** to a huge number of receivers simultaneously (TV, radio).
- High bandwidth capacity for data, voice, and video transmission.

### Disadvantages

- **Propagation delay** is significant — for GEO satellites (~36,000 km altitude), the round-trip delay is approximately **270 ms**, which affects real-time communication.
- High **setup and launch cost** — satellites are expensive to build, launch, and maintain.
- **Signal attenuation** due to rain, atmospheric absorption, and interference (**rain fade**).
- Limited satellite **lifespan** (typically 10–15 years) and replacement is costly.
- **Spectrum congestion** — limited frequency bands are shared among many users.


## 8. Bluetooth

**Bluetooth** is a short-range wireless communication technology that operates in the **2.4 GHz ISM band** and uses **frequency hopping spread spectrum (FHSS)** to transmit data. It is designed for low-power, low-cost communication between devices over short distances.

### Characteristics

- Operates in the **2.4 – 2.485 GHz** ISM band.
- Uses **FHSS** — hops across 79 channels at 1600 hops/second to avoid interference.
- Range: typically **10 metres** (Class 2); up to 100 m (Class 1).
- Data rate: **1 Mbps** (Bluetooth 1.0), up to **3 Mbps** (EDR), and much higher in BLE 5.x.
- Low power consumption — designed for battery-operated devices.
- Supports both **voice** (SCO links) and **data** (ACL links) transmission.

### Bluetooth Connection Mechanism

### 1. Inquiry
Before connecting, a device needs to discover others nearby. The **initiating device** broadcasts an inquiry message and listens for responses. Devices that respond send back their **device address and clock information**. This phase builds a list of discoverable devices in range.

### 2. Paging
Once a target device is identified, the initiating device performs **paging** — it sends a train of page messages using the target's device address to establish synchronization. The target responds, and both devices synchronize their clocks and hopping sequences. After paging, a **connection is established**.

### 3. Connection Modes
Once connected, a device can be in one of the following modes:

- **Active mode** — device is actively transmitting or receiving data.
- **Sniff mode** — device listens at reduced intervals to save power.
- **Hold mode** — device temporarily stops ACL traffic but stays connected; used for low-priority links.
- **Park mode** — device gives up its active address and enters a very low-power state while staying loosely synchronized with the piconet.

### Bluetooth Network Topology

#### Piconet
A **piconet** is the basic Bluetooth network unit. It consists of one **master** device and up to **7 active slave** devices (plus up to 255 parked slaves). The master controls the hopping sequence and timing. All devices share the same channel and synchronize to the master's clock.

#### Scatternet
A **scatternet** is formed when two or more piconets **overlap and interconnect**. A device can belong to multiple piconets simultaneously — acting as a slave in one and a master in another. This bridging device is called a **bridge node**. Scatternets extend the range and capacity of Bluetooth networks beyond a single piconet.

### Bluetooth Protocol Stack

- **Radio layer** — defines frequency band, modulation, and power levels.
- **Baseband layer** — manages physical links, hopping, addressing, and error correction.
- **LMP (Link Manager Protocol)** — handles link setup, authentication, and power control between devices.
- **L2CAP (Logical Link Control and Adaptation Protocol)** — multiplexes higher-layer protocols and handles segmentation and reassembly of packets.
- **RFCOMM** — emulates serial port communication (RS-232) over Bluetooth for legacy applications.
- **SDP (Service Discovery Protocol)** — allows devices to discover what services other Bluetooth devices offer.
- **Audio layer** — handles voice transmission via SCO links directly from baseband.

### Applications

- Wireless **headsets, earbuds, and speakers**.
- **File transfer** between phones and computers.
- **Input devices** — wireless keyboards, mice, game controllers.
- **Healthcare** — heart rate monitors, glucose meters.
- **IoT** — smart home devices, beacons, wearables.
- **Hands-free car kits** and in-vehicle infotainment.

### Advantages

- Low power consumption — ideal for battery-operated devices.
- No line-of-sight required; works through walls and obstacles.
- Automatic connection via pairing — easy to use.
- Supports both voice and data simultaneously.
- FHSS makes it resistant to interference and eavesdropping.

### Disadvantages

- Short range — typically limited to 10 metres.
- Lower data rate compared to Wi-Fi.
- Can suffer interference from other 2.4 GHz devices (Wi-Fi, microwave ovens).
- Security vulnerabilities — susceptible to **bluejacking, bluesnarfing, and bluebugging** attacks.
- A piconet supports only 7 active devices — limited scalability.

### Frequency Hopping Spread Spectrum (FHSS)

Instead of transmitting on a single fixed frequency, Bluetooth rapidly **hops across 79 channels** (each 1 MHz wide) within the 2.4 GHz band in a pseudo-random sequence at **1600 hops/second**. Each hop lasts **625 µs** (one time slot).

The hopping sequence is not truly random — it is **determined by the master's BD_ADDR and clock**, so all slaves in the piconet can compute the same sequence and stay synchronized.
