# 3. Portfolio and Project Management Institute (PMI) Framework

A **portfolio** is a collection of projects, programs, and processes that are managed together to meet the objectives of an organization, and it sits above individual projects in how an organization plans its work strategically. This chapter looks at how organizations manage groups of projects through portfolio management, how the PMI framework organizes project management knowledge into related categories, and what factors tend to drive or block a project's success.

## 3.1 Project, Program, and Portfolio

A **project** is a temporary effort undertaken to create a unique product or service and has a definite end goal. A **program** is a group of related projects, subsidiary programs, and program activities that are managed together in a coordinated way to gain benefits that would not be available if each project were managed on its own. A **portfolio** is a collection of projects, programs, and other work that are grouped together to help meet an organization's strategic business objectives. The key difference across the three is one of scope and purpose: a project defines specific end goals and objectives, a program coordinates multiple related projects to gain combined benefits, and a portfolio represents the organization's overall strategic planning commitment across all of its projects and programs, related or not.

## 3.2 Project Portfolio Management (PPM)

**Project Portfolio Management (PPM)** is the analysis and optimization of the costs, resources, technologies, and processes for all the projects and programs within a portfolio. It is carried out by portfolio managers or by a Project Management Office (PMO), and its goal is to balance risk and reward while making sure that every outcome in the portfolio supports the strategic goals and business objectives of the organization. PPM is carried out through five broad steps: defining the business objectives, collecting project ideas for the portfolio, selecting the best projects through a cost-benefit analysis, validating the feasibility of the chosen portfolio, and finally executing and managing the portfolio by coordinating work across project and program managers.

### 3.2.1 Key Benefits of Project Portfolio Management

- It helps predict the outcome of projects and plan for the ones that will offer the best results.
- It helps identify which projects carry the most risk and which can offer the most reward.
- It gives managers a global view of each project, allowing them to anticipate future problems.
- It improves the accuracy of estimating and managing the financial resources of a group of projects.
- It allows managers to make business-based decisions, reducing cost and increasing productivity.
- It helps monitor project progress in real time by providing detailed, consolidated data.

### 3.2.2 Project Management vs Project Portfolio Management

| S.N. | Project Management | Project Portfolio Management |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Focuses on doing projects right. | Focuses on doing the right projects. |
| 2 | Applies knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques to project activities to meet a single project's goals. | Manages a collection of projects to meet the organization's overall strategy. |
| 3 | Directs a single project successfully. | Selects and successfully executes the right projects for the organization. |
| 4 | Is a temporary but unique process. | Is an ongoing process performed continuously. |
| 5 | Does not make strategic plans or prioritize projects. | Makes strategic plans and prioritizes projects. |
| 6 | Aims to complete a single project and deliver a service. | Aims to look across all projects to improve return on investment and reduce costs. |
| 7 | Typically used for smaller-scale, more detailed projects. | Typically used for large-scale as well as more diffused, organization-wide projects. |

## 3.3 PMI Framework — Knowledge Area Classification

The **PMI framework** organizes the nine knowledge areas of project management (covered in Chapter 2) into related categories that show how they connect to one another and to the people involved in a project.

- **Stakeholder needs and expectations** sit at the center of the framework, since a stakeholder is any individual, group, or party with an interest in the organization and the outcome of its actions, such as employees, customers, shareholders, suppliers, communities, and governments; every knowledge area ultimately exists to satisfy these needs and expectations.
- The **core knowledge areas** are scope, time, cost, and quality management, and they directly define what the project will deliver, by when, at what cost, and to what standard.
- The **facilitating knowledge areas** are human resource, communication, risk, and procurement management, and they support the core areas by managing the people, information, uncertainty, and external contracts involved in delivering the project.
- The **integrative knowledge area** is project integration management, which ties the core and facilitating areas together into a single, coordinated project management plan.
- **Tools and techniques** are applied across all of the above knowledge areas to increase understanding and consistency, and while project management software makes these tools easier to use, the same techniques have long been applied using simple paper and pencil methods.

In this framework, stakeholder needs drive the core knowledge areas, the facilitating knowledge areas support those core areas, and the integrative knowledge area binds everything together so that tools and techniques can be applied consistently across the whole project.

## 3.4 Project Management Institute (PMI)

The **Project Management Institute (PMI)** is a U.S.-based, not-for-profit professional organization for project management, and it is the body responsible for publishing and maintaining the PMBOK guide. PMI considers the Project Management Office (PMO) to be a major catalyst for achieving organizational excellence in how projects are run.

## 3.5 Drivers of Project Success

Certain factors consistently push a project toward success.

- Strong top management support.
- Clear goals and objectives.
- Client support throughout the project.
- A realistic plan.
- Appropriate resources, covering the 4 Ms: Man, Machine, Material, and Money.
- A clear sense of ownership.
- A formal methodology.
- A hardworking, focused staff.
- Standard and structured ICT support infrastructure.
- Effective communication.
- An experienced project manager.

## 3.6 Inhibitors of Project Success

Just as certain factors drive success, others consistently work against it.

- Poor communication.
- Lack of leadership.
- Unclear expectations.
- Poor upfront planning.
- A built-in negative attitude toward IT.
- Frequently changing business strategies.
- Poor top management support.
- Conflicts of objectives among stakeholders.
- Inadequate resources.
- Financial limitations.
- Lack of historical data to plan against.

These drivers and inhibitors connect closely to the **balanced scorecard**, since a balanced scorecard tracks project and organizational performance across financial, customer, internal process, and learning/growth perspectives; drivers of success such as top management support, client support, and effective communication strengthen these same perspectives, while inhibitors such as poor communication, financial limitations, and unclear expectations weaken them. The balanced scorecard is covered in full detail in Chapter 15.

## 3.7 Project Management Maturity

A **maturity model** is a framework used to help an organization measure and improve its project management processes and systems over time. The **Project Management Maturity Model (PMMM)** is a formal tool used to measure how mature an organization's project management practice is, and once its current level and areas for improvement are identified, it provides a roadmap for advancing to higher maturity and better performance. Maturity is generally measured across five levels: **Initial**, where processes are ad hoc and chaotic; **Managed**, where requirements and processes are planned, performed, and controlled; **Defined**, where processes are well characterized and documented in standards and procedures; **Quantitatively Managed**, where key subprocesses are controlled using statistical and quantitative techniques; and **Optimizing**, where processes are continually improved based on a quantitative understanding of their common causes of variation.
