# 6. Project Management Process Groups

A **process** is a sequence of steps taken to achieve a particular goal, and every process in project management is described in terms of its inputs, tools and techniques, and outputs. Project management process groups organize the work of a project into logical stages, and understanding how these groups overlap and map onto the nine knowledge areas is essential to seeing how a project is actually managed from start to finish.

## 6.1 Process

A process takes documents, plans, designs, and other artifacts as **inputs**, applies certain mechanisms as **tools and techniques**, and produces documents, products, services, knowledge, or artifacts as **outputs**. For a project to succeed, the appropriate process must be selected to meet the project's objectives, the chosen method must be well-defined yet flexible enough to satisfy requirements, communication and engagement with stakeholders must be established and maintained, stakeholder needs and expectations must be followed, and the competing constraints of scope, schedule, budget, quality, resources, and risk must be balanced to produce the specified product, service, or result.

## 6.2 The Five Project Management Process Groups

Project management processes are concerned with describing and organizing the work of a project, and they are grouped into five process groups that a project moves through, sometimes with overlap between them.

![The Five Project Management Process Groups](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/ea9d3e85-4edd-4547-96d5-7d1f09f23323)


- The **initiating processes** recognize when a project or a new phase of a project should begin.
- The **planning processes** design and maintain a plan that leads to the successful completion of the project.
- The **executing processes** coordinate people and resources to carry out the plan.
- The **monitoring and controlling processes** monitor and measure progress, taking corrective action when necessary.
- The **closing processes** analyze the completion of the project or phase and confirm its acceptability.

## 6.3 Percentage of Time Spent on Each Process Group

Across a typical project, time is not spent equally on all five process groups. The **executing** process group consumes the largest share of project time and effort, since this is where the actual project work is carried out. The **planning** process group takes up the next largest share, reflecting how much upfront design and scheduling a project needs. The **monitoring and controlling** process group runs throughout the project, in parallel with both planning and executing, since progress must be tracked continuously rather than only at fixed points. The **initiating** and **closing** process groups take up comparatively little time, since they mark the short, defined start and end points of the project.

## 6.4 Overlap of Process Groups in a Phase

Process groups are not strictly sequential; they overlap with one another within a single project phase. Initiating processes lead into planning processes, but planning continues to be refined even after executing processes have started, since new information from execution often requires adjustments to the plan. Monitoring and controlling processes run alongside both planning and executing for the full duration of the phase, catching deviations and feeding corrective action back into planning or execution as needed. Closing processes only begin once the executing and monitoring/controlling processes for that phase are substantially complete, marking the transition point into the next phase or into project closure.

## 6.5 Mapping of Process Groups to Knowledge Areas

The nine knowledge areas (introduced in Chapter 2) provide a detailed description of the actual processes carried out within a project, and each knowledge area can contain processes that belong to more than one process group at the same time. For example, Project Integration Management contains processes across almost all five process groups, from developing the project charter (initiating) to closing the project or phase (closing), while a knowledge area like Project Time Management is concentrated mainly within the planning, executing, and monitoring/controlling groups. This is why the relationship between process groups and knowledge areas is usually represented as a grid or matrix, with the five process groups along one axis and the nine knowledge areas along the other, and each cell in the matrix showing which specific process belongs to that combination.
