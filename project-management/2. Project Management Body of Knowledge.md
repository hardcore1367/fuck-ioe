# 2. Project Management Body of Knowledge

The **Project Management Body of Knowledge (PMBOK)** is the standard collection of processes and knowledge areas that describe how projects are managed in practice. It brings together proven traditional practices along with newer, less widely used practices into a single set of guidelines that project managers can follow across most projects, most of the time. This chapter also covers the environment a project operates in and the personal skills a project manager needs to lead a team through that environment successfully.

## 2.1 PMBOK

**PMBOK** is a collection of processes and knowledge areas that are generally accepted as best practice within project management. It represents the sum of knowledge within the profession of project management, combining widely used proven traditional practices with a smaller set of innovative and advanced practices. PMBOK provides guidelines for managing individual projects, and following it is considered good practice because it applies to most projects most of the time. PMBOK recognizes **five process groups** and **nine knowledge areas** for every type of project.

### 2.1.1 Five Process Groups

Each process under PMBOK is described in terms of its inputs, tools and techniques, and outputs. Inputs are documents, plans, designs, or artifacts; tools and techniques are the mechanisms applied to those inputs; and outputs are the resulting documents, products, services, or knowledge.

- The **initiating processes** formally recognize that a new project exists or that an existing project should continue into its next phase, and this group produces the project charter, which answers the question "What are we trying to do?"
- The **planning processes** take the project to an appropriate level of detail once it has been initiated, with the major goal of managing risk throughout execution by planning time, cost, and resources efficiently.
- The **executing processes** carry out the procedures needed to complete the tasks defined in the project management plan, coordinating people and resources to achieve the project's goals.
- The **monitoring and controlling processes** track, review, and monitor the progress and performance of the project so that potential problems can be identified quickly and corrective action taken.
- The **closing processes** formally close the project once all tasks are completed, the project team is disbanded, and the project is approved by the client.

### 2.1.2 Nine Knowledge Areas

Each knowledge area contains some or all of the project management processes, and together they cover every dimension of managing a project.

1. Project Integration Management
2. Project Scope Management
3. Project Time Management
4. Project Cost Management
5. Project Quality Management
6. Project Human Resource Management
7. Project Communications Management
8. Project Risk Management
9. Project Procurement Management

Much of PMBOK is unique to project management, such as CPM, PERT, and WBS, while some areas overlap with other management disciplines like general management, financial forecasting, and organizational behavior.

## 2.2 Project Environment

A **project environment** consists of the forces that influence a project's ability to achieve its objectives, and because every project is environment-specific, it must continually adapt to environmental changes. These environmental influences act on a project through complexity, uncertainty, competition for resources, flexibility requirements, and rapid technological change. The project environment is classified into three types.

- The **internal environment** refers to events, factors, people, systems, and conditions inside the organization that are generally under its direct control. It includes the project's objectives, its constraints of time, cost, and quality, its temporary structure, and its human and non-human resources. Staff, budget, and corporate culture are the main factors through which the internal environment affects a project.
- The **task environment** affects the organization's ability to reach its goals and includes stakeholders such as clients, contractors, consultants, suppliers, government bodies, competitors, and financiers, whose interests and impacts on the project are closely interrelated.
- The **external environment** is composed of factors that occur outside the organization and are beyond its control, such as the economy, competition, politics, and customers and suppliers. Even though these factors originate outside the organization, they can significantly influence its current operations, growth, and long-term sustainability, so managers must continuously monitor and adapt to them.

### 2.2.1 Obstacles to Project Success

A number of common obstacles can prevent a project from succeeding even when it is well planned on paper.

- Poor project planning and direction.
- Insufficient communication among the team and stakeholders.
- Lack of change, risk, financial, and performance management.
- Failure to coordinate with stakeholders.
- Ineffective involvement in management.
- Lack of skilled team members.
- Poor or missing technology and tools.

## 2.3 General Management Skills

**General management** covers the broad skills needed to run any organization, and it is closely related to project management because a project manager must apply many of the same skills within the boundary of a single project. General management includes planning, organizing, staffing, directing, motivating, controlling, coordinating, and communicating. **Planning**, in particular, involves determining objectives, establishing planning premises and constraints, deciding the planning period, collecting and processing information, deciding between alternative courses of action, evaluating those alternatives, and selecting the best plan. Project management can be seen as an application of these same general management skills to a temporary, unique piece of work rather than to the organization's ongoing operations, which is why a good general manager can usually transition into project management, but must additionally develop skills specific to running temporary, unique work.

## 2.4 Essential Interpersonal and Managerial Skills

A project manager must be a people manager above all, and must therefore have strong soft skills in negotiation, communication, and interpersonal handling of the team. The essential interpersonal and managerial skills expected of a project manager are as follows.

- **Being energized and an initiator** means the project manager must not lack energy or fitness, and must be able to work continuously under pressure while taking the initiative needed to produce a positive result.
- **Communication** means the project manager must be able to express ideas clearly in both written and oral form, keeping messages simple and ensuring good feedback.
- **Influencing** is the ability to get people to act in a way they would not otherwise have chosen to act.
- **Leadership** means imparting a vision, gaining consensus on goals, establishing direction, and inspiring and motivating the team with self-assurance.
- **Being a motivator** means energizing people to reach a high level of performance and overcome barriers to change.
- **Negotiation** is the ability to resolve conflicts, reach agreements, and understand the best solution to a problem.
- **Being a problem solver** means having a problem-solving attitude along with the ability to analyze and deal with problems as they arise.
- **Perspective nature** is the ability to look beyond the immediate team and see how the project and the team fit into the wider organization.
- **Being result-oriented** means working to achieve the project's actual objectives rather than simply completing tasks for their own sake.
- **Global illiteracies** refers to the ability to work in a cross-cultural environment and understand cross-cultural issues.
- **Problem solving using problem trees** means being able to examine, analyze, and develop a structured picture of a problem, which is explained further below.

## 2.5 Effective vs Ineffective Project Managers

The qualities that separate a strong project manager from a weak one are frequently summarized as a direct comparison.

| S.N. | Effective Project Managers | Ineffective Project Managers |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Lead by example. | Set bad examples. |
| 2 | Are visionaries. | Appear confused. |
| 3 | Have strong technical skills. | Lack technical expertise. |
| 4 | Make decisions quickly. | Are indecisive. |
| 5 | Are good communicators. | Are poor communicators. |
| 6 | Are good motivators. | Are poor motivators or demotivate the team. |
| 7 | Stand up to top-level management when needed. | Complain about top-level management. |
| 8 | Support their team members. | Lack team spirit. |
| 9 | Encourage new ideas. | Are conservative and resist new ideas. |

## 2.6 Problem Solving Using Problem Trees

A **problem tree** helps analyze an existing situation by identifying the major problems within it and mapping out their causal relationships. Building a problem tree follows a defined set of steps.

1. Identify the major problems existing within the stated problem area.
2. Analyze the interrelationship and common issues among these problems to determine the core problem.
3. Write down the causes of the major problems.
4. Write down the effects caused by the core problem.
5. Form a diagram of the problem tree showing the causes, the core problem, and the effects.
6. Review the diagram as a whole to verify its validity and completeness, repeating from step 2 if required.

**Brainstorming** is often used alongside the problem tree, and is a method of generating ideas and sharing knowledge to solve a commercial or technical problem, where participants are encouraged to think and contribute without interruption.

## 2.7 Expert Judgment

**Expert judgment** is a way of making a decision based on the expertise, skill, or specialized knowledge of a person in a specific area, gained either through training or career experience. Anyone who has worked on a large, complex project understands the importance of expert judgment in making good decisions, and project managers are expected to consult experts on matters such as which methodology to follow, which programming language to use, or which training approach to adopt.
