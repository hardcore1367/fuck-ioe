# 4. Project Management

**Project management** is the use of methods, techniques, skills, knowledge, and experience to direct the activities of a planned project toward its objectives, and it mainly involves project planning and project monitoring. This chapter looks at how PMI frames the phases a project moves through, how a project's life cycle compares with a product's life cycle, the common methodologies used to develop IT systems, and the key roles that make up a project team.

## 4.1 Project Management and Its Advantages

Project management brings a structured, planned approach to carrying out a project rather than leaving it to run informally, and this structure produces several concrete advantages.

- Increased control of financial, physical, and human resources.
- Improved customer relations and higher quality outcomes.
- Enhanced reliability of the solutions delivered.
- Increased profit margins and improved productivity at work.
- Better internal coordination and higher work morale.
- Shorter development time and lower overall costs.

## 4.2 Project Management Context as per PMI

As per **PMI**, project management is subdivided into distinct phases, namely initiating, planning, monitoring, and closing, and organizing the work this way helps decrease uncertainty as the project moves forward. Each phase is marked by the completion of one or more deliverables before the project moves on to the next phase.

## 4.3 Project Life Cycle

A **project life cycle** is a collection of project phases that defines what work will be performed in each phase, what deliverables will be produced and when, who is involved in each phase, and how management will control and approve the work produced in each phase. A **deliverable** is a product or service produced or provided as part of a project. The project life cycle is generally described through four phases.

![Project Life Cycle](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/feca9947-d9ca-4d05-805d-48433a34b743)


- The **initiation phase** defines the processes required to start a new project and determines what the project should accomplish. It mainly involves developing a project charter and identifying stakeholders, and once the charter is approved, the project becomes officially authorized.
- The **planning phase** covers about half of the whole project process and determines the project's scope and objectives. It begins with the outputs of the initiation phase, and its own outputs then feed into the execution phase; planning should not be finalized before initial planning is complete, and plans should keep being revised right up until execution begins.
- The **execution phase** consists of carrying out the activities defined in the project management plan, including managing stakeholder expectations, coordinating people and resources, and performing the work needed to produce the project deliverables.
- The **monitoring, controlling, and closing phase** tracks, reviews, and regulates progress to ensure the built product matches the project management plan, focusing on identifying any changes from that plan and determining the appropriate corrective action.

### 4.3.1 Characteristics of Project Life Cycle

- A project must pass through each of its defined phases in sequence.
- Each phase is concluded with a review of its primary outputs and the project's performance so far.
- Cost and staffing levels are typically low at the start, rise higher toward the end, and then drop rapidly as the project closes.
- Risk and uncertainty are highest at the start of the project and reduce as it progresses.
- The ability of stakeholders to influence the final characteristics and final cost of the project's product is highest at the start and becomes progressively lower as the project continues.

### 4.3.2 Representative Project Life Cycles

Different fields structure their project life cycles differently even though the underlying logic of moving from initiation to closure stays the same.

- **Construction** projects typically move through feasibility study, planning and design, construction, and turnover and startup.
- **Pharmaceutical** projects move through discovery and screening, preclinical development, registration workup, and post-submission activity.
- **Government office** and **defense** projects move through feasibility study or strategic planning, concept and technology development, system development and demonstration, production and deployment, and support.
- **Software development** projects following a spiral approach move through a proof-of-concept cycle, a first build cycle, a second build cycle, and a final cycle, while software projects following a waterfall approach move through concept, analysis, requirements, design and development, programming, testing, verification, and maintenance.

## 4.4 Product Life Cycle vs Project Life Cycle

The **product life cycle** describes the stages a product passes through from conception to retirement, and these stages are development, introduction, growth, maturity, and decline; they occur sequentially and do not overlap. The **project life cycle**, by contrast, can be a part of one or more phases of the product life cycle, since a single product may need several projects across its lifetime.

| S.N. | Product Life Cycle | Project Life Cycle |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Is always longer than a project life cycle. | Is shorter than the product life cycle. |
| 2 | Acts as a superset that can contain project life cycles. | Can be a subset of the product life cycle. |
| 3 | May or may not have a definite end. | Has a definite end. |
| 4 | Has the phases: development, introduction, growth, maturity, and decline. | Has the phases: initiation, planning, executing, monitoring and controlling, and closing. |
| 5 | Phases are always sequential and never overlap. | Phases usually overlap with one another. |
| 6 | Can contain more than one project life cycle within it. | Represents a single project within the larger product life cycle. |

## 4.5 IT Product Development Life Cycle (SDLC)

The **IT Product Development Life Cycle** is more commonly called the **Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC)**, a process used by the software industry to design, develop, and test high-quality software that meets or exceeds customer expectations while staying within time and cost estimates. SDLC is generally described through six stages.

![SDLC](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/b0c8628f-204d-4f3e-8bbe-27769c5afc85)


1. **Planning and requirement analysis**, which is the most important stage, where the basic project approach is planned and a feasibility study is conducted covering economic, operational, and technical areas.
2. **Defining requirements**, where the product requirements are clearly documented and approved by the customer, typically through a Software Requirement Specification (SRS) document.
3. **Designing product architecture**, where the design approach and architecture models of the product are prepared based on the SRS.
4. **Developing the product**, where actual development begins and developers write the code for each module.
5. **Testing the product**, where the completed build is tested, defects are reported, tracked, fixed, and retested until the product meets the quality standards defined in the SRS.
6. **Deployment**, where the tested product is released into the market or the customer environment, feedback is collected, and ongoing maintenance is provided for the existing customer base.

## 4.6 System Development Methodologies

**SDLC** is a framework describing the phases involved in developing and maintaining an information system, while a **methodology** is the formalized approach used to actually implement that framework; common examples include the Waterfall model, Iterative and Incremental model, Agile model, and Lean model, and the right one is chosen based on the project's requirements and technology.

### 4.6.1 Waterfall Model

The **Waterfall model** is a sequential, linear model that divides software development into pre-defined phases, where each phase must be completed before the next one begins with no overlap between them. Its principal stages are requirements analysis and definition, system and software design, implementation and unit testing, integration and system testing, deployment, and maintenance. It is simple to understand, easy to manage due to its rigid structure, and works well for smaller projects with well-understood requirements, but it is not suitable for projects with changing requirements or large projects, and errors can only be fixed within the phase they occur in.

![Waterfall Model](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/ed47569a-0e6e-403c-915a-a3db6fb96edb)


### 4.6.2 Iterative and Incremental Model

The **Iterative and Incremental model** follows an evolutionary approach that combines the iterative design method with an incremental build model. The project starts with a comparatively small task or component, and increments are added in each cycle of iteration until the desired product is reached. This approach delivers the initial product faster, lowers initial delivery cost, and allows customers to give feedback on each increment, but it requires more resources, effective planning of each iteration, and greater management attention, making it less suitable for small projects.

![Incremental Model](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/a88fe5ba-7e93-455e-a30c-7a77129154a8)


### 4.6.3 Agile Model

The **Agile model** is a combination of the iterative and incremental process models, where the product is broken into small incremental builds delivered through iterations that typically last one to three weeks. Each iteration involves cross-functional teams working simultaneously on planning, requirements analysis, design, coding, unit testing, and acceptance testing, and a working product is shown to the customer and stakeholders at the end of every iteration. Agile requires minimal resource planning, suits both fixed and changing requirements, and gives developers flexibility, but the shortage of formal documentation can create confusion, complicate later maintenance, and make the model unsuitable for handling complex dependencies, especially since it depends heavily on clear customer interaction.

![Agile Model](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/2cb5df02-d8a1-4a7f-b120-32d18f6b12b0)


### 4.6.4 Lean Model

The **Lean model** is a systematic approach to identifying and eliminating waste through continuous improvement, based on the "just in time production" principle, and it aims to increase the speed of software development while decreasing its cost. Its core principles are eliminating waste, building in quality, amplifying learning across the team, deciding as late as possible, delivering as fast as possible, motivating the team, and optimizing the whole development process from start to finish. Lean improves customer satisfaction, productivity, and the ability to react to changes, but it can involve a high implementation cost and requires maintaining a high level of team cohesion.

## 4.7 Roles and Responsibilities of Key Project Members

A **project team** is a group of people from various skilled domains who work together to accomplish a common project goal, and it must have the right combination of skills, abilities, and personalities to succeed.

- The **Functional Manager** provides necessary support services to the project, including purchases, and manages the HR and administration of the performing organization.
- The **Project Sponsor** assures the availability of essential project resources, approves the budget, leads the project board, and holds the power and authority to make decisions and settle disputes or conflicts; this role is typically filled by an executive director or director of finance.
- The **Project Manager** manages project deliveries against the plan, develops and maintains the detailed project plan, coordinates partners and working groups, recruits project staff, records and manages project issues, resolves cross-functional issues, manages project scope, and monitors overall project progress and performance.
- The **System Administrator** handles data migration, interfaces with other systems, sets up and maintains security, manages reporting configuration and deployment, and contributes to technical strategy, policy, and procedures.
- The **Project Champion** helps focus attention on the project from a technical perspective and is usually someone with deep technical expertise and industry knowledge, such as a technical manager or CTO.
