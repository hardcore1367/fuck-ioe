# Chapter 1 — Introduction

A **project** is a set of organized activities carried out to achieve a specific objective within a defined time, cost, and quality boundary. Unlike everyday operational work, a project is unique, has a clear beginning and end, and exists to produce a specific outcome rather than to repeat an ongoing process.

## 1.1 What is a Project

**Cleland and King** define a project as a combination of human and non-human resources pulled together in a temporary organization to achieve a specific purpose. The **Project Management Institute (PMI)** defines a project more simply as a temporary effort undertaken to create a unique product or service.

## 1.2 Characteristics of a Project

Every project shares a common set of characteristics regardless of its size or domain.

- A project has a **specific objective**, and the project is considered successful only once that objective is achieved.
- A project is **temporary**, meaning it has a clear beginning and end rather than continuing indefinitely.
- A project is **non-routine and non-repetitive**, since it is carried out only once for a particular purpose.
- A project always operates within **constraints** of time, cost, and quality, which limit how it can be executed.
- A project is **unique**, because no two projects are exactly alike even if they appear similar on the surface.
- A project needs to be **flexible**, since risks and changes are unavoidable and the project must be able to adjust to them.
- A project requires **resource integration**, meaning that manpower, machines, money, and time must all be coordinated efficiently.
- A project depends on **teamwork**, where a manager leads the team toward the shared goal.
- A project requires **planning and control**, so that the work is completed efficiently and within the defined constraints.

## 1.3 Project vs Operational Work

A project is often confused with regular business operations, but the two are fundamentally different in nature and purpose.

| S.N. | Project | Operational Work |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Creates a special, temporary product or outcome. | Involves the ongoing execution of activities that produce the same repetitive result or service. |
| 2 | Temporary in nature, with a defined start and end. | Permanent in nature, with no fixed end date. |
| 3 | Has a clearly defined budget. | Does not have a separately defined budget. |
| 4 | Produces a unique product each time. | Produces the same product repeatedly to keep the system running. |
| 5 | Carries more risk since it is done for the first time. | Carries less risk since the product has already been made before. |
| 6 | Managed through **Project Management**. | Managed through **Business Process Management**. |

## 1.4 Triple Constraints of a Project

The **triple constraint**, also called the project management triangle, represents the three core limitations that every project must be managed within: **time**, **cost**, and **scope** (sometimes shown as quality). The time constraint refers to the project's schedule for completion, including deadlines for each phase of the work. The scope constraint refers to the specific tasks, deliverables, features, and functions required to fulfill the project's goals. The cost constraint refers to the project's budget, meaning the financial resources needed to cover materials, labor, vendors, and quality control. These three constraints are tightly interrelated, so a change in one almost always forces a change in the others. Reducing the project's scope can shorten its duration when the schedule is falling behind, and reducing scope also lowers cost because fewer tasks need to be executed. Similarly, time and cost are often directly related, since resources such as rented equipment or extra labor cost more the longer they are needed.

## 1.5 Classification of Projects

Projects can be classified on several different bases.

- By **sponsorship**: customer, organization, contractor, government, or donor.
- By **nature**: individual, staff, special, or complex.
- By **orientation**: product-oriented or process-oriented.
- By **speed**: normal, crash, or disaster.
- By **funding source**: private sector, government sector, grant, or loan projects.
- By **size**: mega (5–10 years), major (3–5 years), medium (1–3 years), or small (short duration).
- By **technique**: labor-intensive or capital-intensive.
- By **number of key purposes**: single-purpose or multi-purpose.
- By **type of relationship**: independent, dependent, or mutually exclusive.

## 1.6 Types of IT Projects

IT projects specifically can be grouped into a few common categories, each with a typical example.

- **System integration** projects bring together a range of diverse ICT products and services, such as building a nationwide WAN.
- **Facilities management** projects take full responsibility for operating a customer's IT facility without owning it, such as establishing an automated security and disaster control center.
- **Software application development** projects involve building specific software products, such as developing an ERP application.
- **Services consulting** projects deliver a specific consulting service to a customer, such as counseling and training on ICT issues, and may or may not result in an actual IT product being delivered.

## 1.7 Project Development Life Cycle

The **project life cycle** refers to the logical sequence of phases a project passes through from its beginning to its end. It is generally divided into four phases, and each stage builds on the one before it.

![Project Development Life Cycle](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/7d956cba-ac51-4b36-86f7-ed803122a128)


- The **project formulation phase** is where creative ideas are identified based on internal and external resources, and the project's objectives and constraints are determined. This phase includes **project identification**, where an idea is first identified, and **project evaluation**, where a project proposal is developed after a pre-feasibility study.
- The **project planning phase** is where a detailed plan is prepared covering time, cost, and quality. This phase includes the **feasibility study**, which measures how beneficial the project will be in practical terms, and **project appraisal/estimation**, where the project's expected success is evaluated by comparing it with similar past projects and developing implementation plans.
- The **implementation phase** is where the project activities are actually carried out and controlled. A proper project team is formed, project managers monitor and supervise the work, and inspection, testing, and periodic review of progress take place throughout this phase.
- The **termination phase** begins once the team has completed the project work. This phase includes **project operation and evaluation**, where the outcome is reviewed and the project's success is assessed, and **project handover**, where the finished product is delivered to the client.

## 1.8 Feasibility Study

A **feasibility study** measures how beneficial a proposed project or product will be for the organization from a practical point of view, and it is carried out during the project planning phase before major resources are committed. It is usually broken down into four types.

- **Technical feasibility** analyzes the current hardware, software, and required technology, along with the technical skills and capabilities of the team.
- **Economic feasibility** analyzes the cost and benefit of the project to determine whether it will be financially worthwhile for the organization.
- **Operational feasibility** analyzes how well the product will serve its intended requirements and how easy it will be to operate and maintain after deployment.
- **Legal feasibility** analyzes the project from a legality standpoint, covering issues such as copyright, licensing, and other legal implementation requirements.

## 1.9 Project Life Cycle vs Product Life Cycle

While the **project life cycle** covers the phases a single project passes through (formulation, planning, implementation, and termination), the **product life cycle** covers the much longer journey of a product from its conception to its retirement, through the stages of development, introduction, growth, maturity, and decline. A product life cycle is always longer than a project life cycle and can contain more than one project within it, whereas a single project has one definite, shorter life cycle with a clear end point. This comparison is covered in more depth in Chapter 4 alongside the IT Product Development Life Cycle (SDLC), since SDLC is essentially the software industry's specific version of a product development life cycle used to design, develop, and test high-quality software within time and cost estimates.
