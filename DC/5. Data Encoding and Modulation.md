# Data Encoding and Modulation

A message signal generated from an information source is called baseband signal.

## 1. Modulation
Modulation is the process of transformation of the message signal (baseband) into a form suitable for transmission over the channel with acceptable degradation.

### Need for Modulation

#### 1. Reduction in Antenna Height
Antenna length must be at least λ/4 of the transmitted signal. Audio frequencies
(20 Hz–20 kHz) would require antennas thousands of meters tall. Modulating onto a
high-frequency carrier shortens the wavelength, making antenna size practical.

#### 2. Avoids Mixing of Signals
All audio signals occupy the same 20 Hz–20 kHz range. Without modulation, every
transmitter would overlap and interfere. Modulation shifts each signal to a unique
carrier frequency so they can coexist without interference.

#### 3. Increases Range of Communication
Low-frequency signals attenuate rapidly and cannot travel long distances. Modulating
them onto a high-frequency carrier allows the signal to propagate farther.

### 4. Multiplexing is Possible
Multiple signals can be transmitted simultaneously over the same medium by assigning
each a different carrier frequency (FDM). This makes efficient use of available
bandwidth.

### 5. Improved Noise Immunity
Techniques like FM, encode information in frequency rather than amplitude, so
amplitude-based noise has little effect — resulting in better signal quality at
the receiver.


## 2. Analog Modulation
**Analog modulation** is the process of modifying an analog carrier signal to carry analog information by varying its amplitude, frequency, or phase.

### 1. Amplitude Modulation
AM is a modulation technique where the **amplitude of the carrier signal** is varied
in proportion to the message signal, while its frequency and phase remain constant.

<img width="607" height="695" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/1ba74689-eff9-4eb1-a10e-f2096751e2ad" />

This is AM's biggest drawback: most of the transmitted power is wasted on the carrier. Only a fraction goes to the sidebands that actually carry the message.
The bandwidth of an AM signal is twice the message frequency, which means higher message frequencies require more channel bandwidth.

#### Signals

- **Message signal:** `m(t) = Am sin(ωm t)`
- **Carrier signal:** `c(t) = Ac sin(ωc t)`

where `ωm = 2πfm` and `ωc = 2πfc` are angular frequencies.

#### Derivation

In AM, the amplitude of the carrier varies with the message. So the instantaneous
amplitude becomes `Ac + m(t)`, giving:

```
y(t) = [Ac + m(t)] sin(ωc t)

y(t) = [Ac + Am sin(ωm t)] sin(ωc t)
```

Taking `Ac` common:

```
y(t) = Ac [1 + (Am/Ac) sin(ωm t)] sin(ωc t)
```

Define **modulation index** `μ = Am / Ac`:

```
y(t) = Ac [1 + μ sin(ωm t)] sin(ωc t)
```

Expanding using `sin A · sin B = ½[cos(A−B) − cos(A+B)]`:

```
y(t) = Ac sin(ωc t)  +  Ac μ sin(ωm t) sin(ωc t)

y(t) = Ac sin(ωc t)
     + (μAc/2) cos((ωc − ωm)t)
     − (μAc/2) cos((ωc + ωm)t)
```

Three frequency components: **carrier (ωc)**, **LSB (ωc − ωm)**, **USB (ωc + ωm)**.
The sidebands carry the information; the carrier carries none.

#### Bandwidth

```
BW = 2fm  =  ωm / π
```

#### Double-Tone AM

Double-tone AM uses two message frequencies simultaneously, better representing
real-world signals where a message has multiple frequency components.

#### Signals

- **Message signal:** `m(t) = Am1 sin(ωm1 t) + Am2 sin(ωm2 t)`
- **Carrier signal:** `c(t) = Ac sin(ωc t)`

Each tone independently modulates the carrier, producing its own pair of sidebands. So instead of 3 components (single-tone), double-tone AM produces 5 frequency components — one carrier, two LSBs, and two USBs.

> **LSB (Lower Sideband)** — the frequency component at `fc − fm`, produced below the carrier frequency.
>
> **USB (Upper Sideband)** — the frequency component at `fc + fm`, produced above the carrier frequency.
>
>Both are created during modulation. Together they are called **sidebands** and they carry the actual message
> information. The carrier sits in the middle, with LSB on the left and USB on the right in the frequency spectrum.


## 2. Frequency Modulation (FM)

FM is a type of analog modulation where the **frequency of the carrier signal**
is varied in proportion to the instantaneous value of the message signal, while
amplitude and phase remain constant. Unlike AM where noise corrupts the amplitude
directly, FM encodes information in frequency — making it far more resistant to
noise and interference.

The amount by which the carrier frequency deviates from its rest value is called
**frequency deviation (Δf)**. The faster the message changes, the faster the
carrier frequency swings around $f_c$.

<img width="590" height="567" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/3aeaac76-9b86-467a-88ec-50ba25116971" />


- **Message signal:** $m(t) = A_m \sin(\omega_m t)$
- **Carrier signal:** $c(t) = A_c \sin(\omega_c t)$

#### Modulation Index

The **modulation index (β)** for FM is:

$$\beta = \frac{\Delta f}{f_m} = \frac{k_f A_m}{f_m}$$

where $k_f$ is the frequency sensitivity of the modulator.  
Unlike AM, β can be greater than 1 without causing distortion.


In FM, the instantaneous frequency varies with the message:

$$f_i(t) = f_c + k_f \cdot m(t) = f_c + k_f A_m \sin(\omega_m t)$$

The instantaneous phase is the integral of instantaneous frequency:

$$\phi(t) = \int \omega_i(t)\, dt = \omega_c t - \frac{k_f A_m}{f_m} \cos(\omega_m t)$$

$$\phi(t) = \omega_c t - \beta \cos(\omega_m t)$$

Substituting into the carrier:

$$\boxed{y(t) = A_c \sin(\omega_c t - \beta \cos(\omega_m t))}$$

This is the **final FM expression**. Unlike AM, it cannot be expanded into
a simple 3-component form — it produces **infinite sidebands** described by
**Bessel functions**:

$$y(t) = A_c \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} J_n(\beta)\, \sin((\omega_c + n\omega_m)t)$$

where $J_n(\beta)$ is the Bessel function of the first kind of order $n$.
In practice, only sidebands with significant amplitude are considered.

#### Bandwidth — Carson's Rule

$$BW = 2(\Delta f + f_m) = 2 f_m(\beta + 1)$$

FM bandwidth is much wider than AM, which is the tradeoff for better noise immunity.

#### Advantages of FM

- Much better **noise immunity** than AM — noise affects amplitude, not frequency.
- Higher **audio fidelity** — used in FM radio and audio broadcasting.
- **No over-modulation distortion** — β can exceed 1 without signal distortion.
- More **efficient power use** — all transmitted power carries information
  (no wasted carrier power like in AM).

#### Disadvantages of FM

- Requires **much larger bandwidth** than AM (governed by Carson's Rule).
- **Circuits are more complex** — modulation and demodulation hardware is harder
  to build than AM.
- **Shorter range** — FM uses VHF band which follows line-of-sight propagation,
  unlike AM which can reflect off the ionosphere.


## 3. Phase Modulation 
**Phase Modulation (PM)** is a type of analog modulation where the **phase of the carrier signal** is varied in proportion to the instantaneous value of the message signal, while amplitude and frequency remain constant.

<img width="1024" height="768" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/829139e5-77e2-4e41-84f9-91ef256eb816" />


- When the message signal is high, the phase of the carrier shifts forward. When it is low, the phase shifts back.
- PM and FM are closely related — in both cases the carrier wave appears to change frequency. Because of this similarity, both are grouped together as **angle modulation**.
- The key difference from FM is that in FM, the frequency deviation depends on both the amplitude and frequency of the message. In PM, the phase deviation depends only on the **amplitude** of the message, not its frequency.
- PM is less commonly used in analog communication but is the foundation of modern digital modulation schemes like **PSK (Phase Shift Keying)** used in Wi-Fi and mobile networks.

## 4. Pulse Modulation

**Pulse modulation** is a technique where a continuous analog signal is sampled at regular intervals and the samples are used to modulate some characteristic of a pulse train. It is the foundation of converting analog signals into a form suitable for digital transmission.

## PAM (Pulse Amplitude Modulation)
In **PAM**, the amplitude of each pulse is varied according to the instantaneous value of the analog signal at the time of sampling. The pulse width and position remain constant — only the height changes.

<img width="557" height="528" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/e0464c3d-77e6-4fbd-82af-72109d00ef63" />


**Advantages:**
- Simplest form of pulse modulation — easy to generate and implement.
- Forms the basis of PCM, making it an important intermediate step.

**Disadvantages:**
- Highly susceptible to noise since amplitude is the most easily corrupted characteristic.
- Requires more bandwidth than PWM or PPM for the same signal quality.

## PWM (Pulse Width Modulation)
In **PWM**, the width (duration) of each pulse is varied according to the amplitude of the analog signal, while the amplitude and position remain constant. A stronger signal produces a wider pulse.

<img width="1024" height="768" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/f934e921-6464-45f5-953f-1a4566c4b2b2" />


**Advantages:**
- More noise-resistant than PAM since noise affects amplitude, not width.
- Widely used and easy to implement in hardware.

**Disadvantages:**
- Variable pulse width means variable bandwidth usage, which is inefficient.
- Synchronization between sender and receiver is required.

## PPM (Pulse Position Modulation)
In **PPM**, the position (timing) of each pulse is shifted relative to a reference based on the amplitude of the analog signal. Amplitude and width remain constant.

<img width="557" height="520" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/bbac13b7-31ca-437c-8753-bb3bd432cc7d" />


**Advantages:**
- Best noise immunity among the three since neither amplitude nor width carries the information.
- More power efficient than PAM and PWM.

**Disadvantages:**
- Most complex to implement — requires precise timing and synchronization.
- Sensitive to timing errors and jitter.

## Comparison

| | PAM | PWM | PPM |
|---|---|---|---|
| **What varies** | Amplitude of pulse | Width of pulse | Position of pulse |
| **What stays fixed** | Width and position | Amplitude and position | Amplitude and width |
| **Noise immunity** | Low | High | Highest |
| **Complexity** | Simple | Moderate | Complex |
| **Bandwidth** | Moderate | Variable | Constant |
| **Main use** | Intermediate step in PCM | Motor control, power electronics | Telemetry, optical communication |


## Pulse Code Modulation
**Pulse Code Modulation (PCM)** is a method of converting a continuous analog signal into a digital signal. It is the most widely used technique for digital audio — telephone networks, CDs, and VoIP all rely on PCM.

![Uploading Screenshot_20260509_175316.png…]()


## Steps in PCM

### 1. Sampling
The analog signal is measured at regular intervals of time. According to the **Nyquist sampling theorem**, the sampling rate must be at least twice the highest frequency present in the signal — otherwise the original signal cannot be accurately reconstructed. This process produces a series of discrete samples that still carry analog amplitude values.

### 2. Quantization
Each sample's amplitude is rounded to the nearest value from a fixed set of discrete levels. The more levels available, the more accurately the sample is represented. The difference between the actual amplitude and the rounded value is called **quantization error** or **quantization noise** — it is unavoidable but can be reduced by using more levels.

### 3. Encoding
Each quantized level is assigned a unique binary code. This converts the quantized samples into a stream of bits ready for digital transmission or storage.

## Advantages
- Highly resistant to noise since the signal is fully digital after encoding.
- Repeaters can regenerate the signal exactly without accumulating noise over distance.
- Can be easily encrypted, compressed, and multiplexed with other digital signals.

## Disadvantages
- Requires significantly more bandwidth than the original analog signal.
- Quantization noise is always present — it can be reduced but never fully eliminated.
- Requires more complex circuitry than analog modulation systems.

## 5. Line Coding Schemes

Line coding converts digital data (bits) into a digital signal for transmission.
Each scheme defines how 1s and 0s are represented using voltage levels over time.
The choice affects bandwidth, synchronization, and noise immunity.


### 1. Unipolar

Uses only one polarity — signal never goes negative.
**Voltage levels: +V and 0**

#### NRZ (Non-Return to Zero)
- Bit 1 → +V, Bit 0 → 0V for the entire bit duration.
- Simple but suffers from DC component buildup and poor sync on long same-bit runs.

**How to draw:** Draw a high line (+V) for every 1, flat line (0V) for every 0.
No transitions in the middle of a bit.

#### RZ (Return to Zero)
- Bit 1 → +V for the first half, then drops to 0 for the second half.
- Bit 0 → stays at 0V throughout.
- Better sync than NRZ but requires double the bandwidth.

**How to draw:** For each 1, draw a pulse that goes up then comes back to 0 at
mid-bit. For 0, stay flat at 0V the whole bit.


### 2. Polar

Uses both positive and negative voltages.
**Voltage levels: +V and −V**

### NRZ-L (Non-Return to Zero Level)
- Bit 0 → +V, Bit 1 → −V. Voltage holds for the full bit duration.
- Simple but loses sync on long runs of the same bit.

**How to draw:** +V for 0, −V for 1. Change level only when the bit changes.

#### NRZ-I (Non-Return to Zero Inversion)
- Bit 1 → **transition** at the beginning of the bit (flip whatever level you are on).
- Bit 0 → **no transition**, stays at same level.
- Better than NRZ-L for runs of 1s since each 1 forces a change.

**How to draw:** Start at any level. For each 1, flip the level at the bit boundary.
For each 0, continue at the same level.

#### RZ
- Bit 0 → +V for first half, returns to 0 for second half.
- Bit 1 → −V for first half, returns to 0 for second half.
- Every bit returns to 0 in the middle — excellent sync, but high bandwidth.

**How to draw:** For 1, draw a -ve half-pulse then drop to 0. For 0, draw a
+ve half-pulse then return to 0.


### 3. Bipolar

Uses three voltage levels. 0 bits are always 0V. 1 bits alternate between +V and −V.
**Voltage levels: +V, 0, −V** (NRZ)

#### AMI (Alternate Mark Inversion)
- Bit 0 → 0V always.
- Bit 1 → alternates between +V and −V with each successive 1.
- No DC component. If two consecutive 1s have the same sign, it means an error
  occurred — good error detection.

**How to draw:** Keep 0V for all 0 bits. For the first 1 use +V, next 1 use −V,
next 1 use +V again, and so on — strictly alternating.

### Pseudo-Ternary
- Exact opposite of AMI.
- Bit 1 → 0V always.
- Bit 0 → alternates between +V and −V with each successive 0.

**How to draw:** Same as AMI but swap the roles of 0 and 1.


### 4. Biphase

A transition **always occurs in the middle of every bit** — guarantees
synchronization. This mid-bit transition is the defining feature of all
biphase schemes.
**Voltage levels: +V and −V**

#### Manchester
- Bit 0 → transition from **+V to −V** at mid-bit.
- Bit 1 → transition from **−V to +V** at mid-bit.
- Used in **Ethernet (IEEE 802.3)**. Excellent synchronization.

**How to draw:** Every bit is split into two halves. For 0: first half +V, second
half −V. For 1: first half −V, second half +V. There may also be a transition at
the bit boundary if adjacent bits require it.

#### Differential Manchester
- **Mid-bit transition always present** (for clocking — same as Manchester).
- Bit 0 → **additional transition at the START** of the bit.
- Bit 1 → **no transition at the start** of the bit.
- More noise-resistant since logic depends on presence/absence of transition,
  not its direction. Used in **Token Ring (IEEE 802.5)**.

**How to draw:** Always draw a mid-bit transition for every bit. Then, at the
start of each bit — add a transition for 0, do nothing for 1.


### Voltage Level Summary

| Scheme | Voltage Levels |
|---|---|
| Unipolar NRZ / RZ | 0, +V |
| Polar NRZ-L / NRZ-I | +V, −V |
| Polar RZ | +V, 0, −V |
| AMI / Pseudo-Ternary | +V, 0, −V |
| Manchester | +V, −V |
| Differential Manchester | +V, −V |


## 6. Block Coding 

Block coding is a technique used to improve synchronization and error detection
in digital transmission. It works by replacing every block of **m bits** with a
block of **n bits** (where n > m), written as **mB/nB encoding**. The extra bits
provide redundancy that helps detect errors and ensure enough transitions in the
signal for clock synchronization.

For example, **4B/5B** replaces every 4 data bits with a 5-bit code, ensuring no
more than one leading zero and no more than two trailing zeros — preventing long
runs of 0s that would kill synchronization.

### Why Block Coding?

- Long runs of 0s or 1s cause loss of synchronization at the receiver.
- Block coding guarantees enough transitions in the signal.
- The receiver can detect errors if an invalid code word is received.


## 7. Scrambling (AMI)

Scrambling is an alternative to block coding that **replaces problematic sequences**
(long runs of 0s) with special sequences that still contain enough transitions for
sync — without adding extra bits or increasing bandwidth. The receiver knows the
substitution rule and reverses it to recover the original data.

Two most common scrambling techniques are **B8ZS** and **HDB3**.

### 1. B8ZS (Bipolar with 8-Zero Substitution)

B8ZS is used with **AMI** encoding. In AMI, long runs of 0s are a problem because
0V has no transitions, causing loss of sync. B8ZS fixes this by replacing every
sequence of **8 consecutive 0s** with a special 8-symbol code that contains
**two AMI violations** (two consecutive pulses of the same sign, which is illegal
in normal AMI).

#### Substitution Rule

If the last pulse before the 8 zeros was **+V**, replace the 8 zeros with:
$$0\ 0\ 0\ +V\ -V\ 0\ -V\ +V$$

If the last pulse was **-V**, replace with:
$$0\ 0\ 0\ -V\ +V\ 0\ +V\ -V$$

The two violations (positions 4 & 5, and positions 7 & 8) are intentional and
detectable — the receiver sees them, knows it is a substitution, and replaces
them back with 8 zeros. Used in **North America** (T1 lines).

### 2. HDB3 (High Density Bipolar 3)

HDB3 works similarly to B8ZS but replaces every sequence of **4 consecutive 0s**
instead of 8. It is used with AMI encoding and also introduces deliberate violations
to maintain sync. Used in **Europe** (E1 lines).

#### Substitution Rule

The substitution depends on two things:
1. The **polarity of the last pulse** (was it +V or −V?)
2. The **number of 1s since the last substitution** (odd or even?)

| Last Pulse | 1s since last sub | Substitution |
|---|---|---|
| +V | Odd | 0 0 0 − |
| −V | Odd | 0 0 0 + |
| +V | Even | + 0 0 + |
| −V | Even | − 0 0 − |

The last symbol in the substitution is always a **violation** (same sign as the
last pulse). When the count of 1s is even, an extra pulse is added at position 1
to maintain the DC balance — this is called a **balancing pulse**.

The receiver detects any AMI violation and knows it signals the end of a 4-zero
substitution, then restores the original 0s.

### B8ZS vs HDB3

| B8ZS | HDB3 |
|---|---|
| Replaces 8 consecutive 0s | Replaces 4 consecutive 0s |
| Used in North America (T1) | Used in Europe (E1) |
| 2 violations per substitution | 1 violation per substitution |
| Simpler substitution rule | Slightly more complex (depends on count of 1s) |

## 8. Digital modulation

**Digital modulation** is the process of mapping digital data (0s and 1s) onto an analog carrier signal for transmission over a channel. The carrier signal has three properties that can be varied — **amplitude**, **frequency**, and **phase** — giving rise to the three fundamental digital modulation schemes.

## ASK (Amplitude Shift Keying)
In **ASK**, the amplitude of the carrier signal is varied to represent binary data while frequency and phase remain constant. Typically, a high amplitude represents binary 1 and a zero or low amplitude represents binary 0. ASK is the simplest digital modulation scheme and is easy to implement. However, it is highly susceptible to noise because noise directly corrupts the amplitude of the signal, making it difficult for the receiver to distinguish between levels. It is commonly used in optical fiber communication and RFID systems where noise levels are controlled.

## FSK (Frequency Shift Keying)
In **FSK**, the frequency of the carrier is switched between two distinct values to represent binary data while amplitude and phase remain constant. A higher frequency represents binary 1 and a lower frequency represents binary 0. Since noise primarily affects amplitude rather than frequency, FSK is significantly more noise-resistant than ASK. The main drawback is that it requires more bandwidth because two separate frequencies must be allocated. FSK is widely used in radio communication, caller ID systems, and older telephone modems.

## PSK (Phase Shift Keying)
In **PSK**, the phase of the carrier signal is shifted to represent binary data while amplitude and frequency remain constant. Rather than changing the shape or frequency of the wave, the signal is abruptly shifted in phase at each bit boundary. PSK is the most bandwidth-efficient and noise-resistant of the three basic schemes because phase is harder to corrupt than amplitude or frequency.

### BPSK (Binary Phase Shift Keying)
**BPSK** is the simplest form of PSK where exactly two phase values are used — 0° represents binary 1 and 180° represents binary 0. Each symbol carries exactly one bit, making it the most robust PSK variant since the two phases are maximally separated and hardest to confuse. The downside is low spectral efficiency — only one bit is transmitted per symbol. Higher-order variants increase efficiency by using more phase values per symbol — **QPSK** uses 4 phases (2 bits/symbol), **8-PSK** uses 8 phases (3 bits/symbol), and so on, at the cost of increased complexity and sensitivity to noise.

## Comparison

| | ASK | FSK | PSK (BPSK) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **What varies** | Amplitude | Frequency | Phase |
| **What stays fixed** | Frequency and phase | Amplitude and phase | Amplitude and frequency |
| **Noise immunity** | Low | Moderate | High |
| **Bandwidth efficiency** | Low | Low (needs two frequencies) | High |
| **Complexity** | Simple | Moderate | Moderate to complex |
| **Bits per symbol** | 1 (on/off) | 1 (two frequencies) | 1 (BPSK), 2 (QPSK), 3 (8-PSK) |
| **Use case** | Optical fiber, RFID | Radio, modems, caller ID | Wi-Fi, satellite, mobile networks |

## 9. Quadrature Amplitude Modulation (QAM)

**Quadrature Amplitude Modulation (QAM)** combines both amplitude and phase variation simultaneously to represent data, unlike ASK or PSK which vary only one property. Two carrier waves of the same frequency but 90° out of phase — called the **in-phase (I)** and **quadrature (Q)** components — are independently modulated and combined into a single signal. The receiver separates the two and reads both amplitude and phase together to determine which symbol was sent. This allows multiple bits to be packed into each symbol, making QAM the most spectrally efficient of all basic modulation schemes. A **constellation diagram** is used to visualize QAM — each point represents a unique symbol, and more points mean more bits per symbol but require a higher SNR.

## 8-QAM
**8-QAM** has 8 constellation points, each carrying 3 bits per symbol. The points are arranged using 2 amplitude levels and 4 phase values. It is rarely used in practice because 16-QAM provides a much better efficiency jump for only a moderate increase in SNR requirement.

## 16-QAM
**16-QAM** has 16 constellation points arranged in a 4×4 grid, each carrying 4 bits per symbol. Both amplitude and phase vary across all 16 points. It strikes the most practical balance between spectral efficiency and noise tolerance and is used in **LTE**, **DVB-T**, and cable modems.

## 32-QAM
**32-QAM** has 32 constellation points, each carrying 5 bits per symbol. The constellation is arranged in a cross pattern rather than a square grid — a perfect square would need 36 points, so the 4 corners are dropped to maintain equal average power. It offers higher throughput than 16-QAM but demands a cleaner channel and is used in **DVB-T2** and microwave links.

## Comparison

| | 8-QAM | 16-QAM | 32-QAM |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Constellation points** | 8 | 16 | 32 |
| **Bits per symbol** | 3 | 4 | 5 |
| **SNR requirement** | Moderate | Moderate-high | High |
| **Use case** | Rarely used alone | LTE, DVB-T, cable modems | DVB-T2, microwave links |
