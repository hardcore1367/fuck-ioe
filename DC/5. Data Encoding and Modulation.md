# Data Encoding and Modulation

A message signal generated from an information source is called baseband signal.

## 5.1 Modulation
Modulation is the process of transformation of the message signal (baseband) into a form suitable for transmission over the channel with acceptable degradation.

### Need for Modulation

#### 1. Reduction in Antenna Height
Antenna length must be at least λ/4 of the transmitted signal. Audio frequencies
(20 Hz–20 kHz) would require antennas thousands of meters tall. Modulating onto a
high-frequency carrier shortens the wavelength, making antenna size practical.

#### 2. Avoids Mixing of Signals
All audio signals occupy the same 20 Hz–20 kHz range. Without modulation, every
transmitter would overlap and interfere. Modulation shifts each signal to a unique
carrier frequency so they can coexist without interference.

#### 3. Increases Range of Communication
Low-frequency signals attenuate rapidly and cannot travel long distances. Modulating
them onto a high-frequency carrier allows the signal to propagate farther.

### 4. Multiplexing is Possible
Multiple signals can be transmitted simultaneously over the same medium by assigning
each a different carrier frequency (FDM). This makes efficient use of available
bandwidth.

### 5. Improved Noise Immunity
Techniques like FM, encode information in frequency rather than amplitude, so
amplitude-based noise has little effect — resulting in better signal quality at
the receiver.


## 5.2 Analog Modulation
**Analog modulation** is the process of modifying an analog carrier signal to carry analog information by varying its amplitude, frequency, or phase.

### 1. Amplitude Modulation
AM is a modulation technique where the **amplitude of the carrier signal** is varied
in proportion to the message signal, while its frequency and phase remain constant.

This is AM's biggest drawback: most of the transmitted power is wasted on the carrier. Only a fraction goes to the sidebands that actually carry the message.
The bandwidth of an AM signal is twice the message frequency, which means higher message frequencies require more channel bandwidth.

#### Signals

- **Message signal:** `m(t) = Am sin(ωm t)`
- **Carrier signal:** `c(t) = Ac sin(ωc t)`

where `ωm = 2πfm` and `ωc = 2πfc` are angular frequencies.

#### Derivation

In AM, the amplitude of the carrier varies with the message. So the instantaneous
amplitude becomes `Ac + m(t)`, giving:

```
y(t) = [Ac + m(t)] sin(ωc t)

y(t) = [Ac + Am sin(ωm t)] sin(ωc t)
```

Taking `Ac` common:

```
y(t) = Ac [1 + (Am/Ac) sin(ωm t)] sin(ωc t)
```

Define **modulation index** `μ = Am / Ac`:

```
y(t) = Ac [1 + μ sin(ωm t)] sin(ωc t)
```

Expanding using `sin A · sin B = ½[cos(A−B) − cos(A+B)]`:

```
y(t) = Ac sin(ωc t)  +  Ac μ sin(ωm t) sin(ωc t)

y(t) = Ac sin(ωc t)
     + (μAc/2) cos((ωc − ωm)t)
     − (μAc/2) cos((ωc + ωm)t)
```

Three frequency components: **carrier (ωc)**, **LSB (ωc − ωm)**, **USB (ωc + ωm)**.
The sidebands carry the information; the carrier carries none.

#### Bandwidth

```
BW = 2fm  =  ωm / π
```

#### Double-Tone AM

Double-tone AM uses two message frequencies simultaneously, better representing
real-world signals where a message has multiple frequency components.

#### Signals

- **Message signal:** `m(t) = Am1 sin(ωm1 t) + Am2 sin(ωm2 t)`
- **Carrier signal:** `c(t) = Ac sin(ωc t)`

Each tone independently modulates the carrier, producing its own pair of sidebands. So instead of 3 components (single-tone), double-tone AM produces 5 frequency components — one carrier, two LSBs, and two USBs.

> **LSB (Lower Sideband)** — the frequency component at `fc − fm`, produced below the carrier frequency.
>
> **USB (Upper Sideband)** — the frequency component at `fc + fm`, produced above the carrier frequency.
>
>Both are created during modulation. Together they are called **sidebands** and they carry the actual message
> information. The carrier sits in the middle, with LSB on the left and USB on the right in the frequency spectrum.


## 2. Frequency Modulation (FM)

FM is a type of analog modulation where the **frequency of the carrier signal**
is varied in proportion to the instantaneous value of the message signal, while
amplitude and phase remain constant. Unlike AM where noise corrupts the amplitude
directly, FM encodes information in frequency — making it far more resistant to
noise and interference.

The amount by which the carrier frequency deviates from its rest value is called
**frequency deviation (Δf)**. The faster the message changes, the faster the
carrier frequency swings around $f_c$.

<img width="590" height="567" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/3aeaac76-9b86-467a-88ec-50ba25116971" />


- **Message signal:** $m(t) = A_m \sin(\omega_m t)$
- **Carrier signal:** $c(t) = A_c \sin(\omega_c t)$

#### Modulation Index

The **modulation index (β)** for FM is:

$$\beta = \frac{\Delta f}{f_m} = \frac{k_f A_m}{f_m}$$

where $k_f$ is the frequency sensitivity of the modulator.  
Unlike AM, β can be greater than 1 without causing distortion.


In FM, the instantaneous frequency varies with the message:

$$f_i(t) = f_c + k_f \cdot m(t) = f_c + k_f A_m \sin(\omega_m t)$$

The instantaneous phase is the integral of instantaneous frequency:

$$\phi(t) = \int \omega_i(t)\, dt = \omega_c t - \frac{k_f A_m}{f_m} \cos(\omega_m t)$$

$$\phi(t) = \omega_c t - \beta \cos(\omega_m t)$$

Substituting into the carrier:

$$\boxed{y(t) = A_c \sin(\omega_c t - \beta \cos(\omega_m t))}$$

This is the **final FM expression**. Unlike AM, it cannot be expanded into
a simple 3-component form — it produces **infinite sidebands** described by
**Bessel functions**:

$$y(t) = A_c \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} J_n(\beta)\, \sin((\omega_c + n\omega_m)t)$$

where $J_n(\beta)$ is the Bessel function of the first kind of order $n$.
In practice, only sidebands with significant amplitude are considered.

#### Bandwidth — Carson's Rule

$$BW = 2(\Delta f + f_m) = 2 f_m(\beta + 1)$$

FM bandwidth is much wider than AM, which is the tradeoff for better noise immunity.

#### Advantages of FM

- Much better **noise immunity** than AM — noise affects amplitude, not frequency.
- Higher **audio fidelity** — used in FM radio and audio broadcasting.
- **No over-modulation distortion** — β can exceed 1 without signal distortion.
- More **efficient power use** — all transmitted power carries information
  (no wasted carrier power like in AM).

#### Disadvantages of FM

- Requires **much larger bandwidth** than AM (governed by Carson's Rule).
- **Circuits are more complex** — modulation and demodulation hardware is harder
  to build than AM.
- **Shorter range** — FM uses VHF band which follows line-of-sight propagation,
  unlike AM which can reflect off the ionosphere.


### 3. Phase Modulation 
**Phase Modulation (PM)** is a type of analog modulation where the **phase of the carrier signal** is varied in proportion to the instantaneous value of the message signal, while amplitude and frequency remain constant.

- When the message signal is high, the phase of the carrier shifts forward. When it is low, the phase shifts back.
- PM and FM are closely related — in both cases the carrier wave appears to change frequency. Because of this similarity, both are grouped together as **angle modulation**.
- The key difference from FM is that in FM, the frequency deviation depends on both the amplitude and frequency of the message. In PM, the phase deviation depends only on the **amplitude** of the message, not its frequency.
- PM is less commonly used in analog communication but is the foundation of modern digital modulation schemes like **PSK (Phase Shift Keying)** used in Wi-Fi and mobile networks.

## 4. Line Coding Schemes

Line coding converts digital data (bits) into a digital signal for transmission.
Each scheme defines how 1s and 0s are represented using voltage levels over time.
The choice affects bandwidth, synchronization, and noise immunity.



### 1. Unipolar

Uses only one polarity — signal never goes negative.
**Voltage levels: +V and 0**

#### NRZ (Non-Return to Zero)
- Bit 1 → +V, Bit 0 → 0V for the entire bit duration.
- Simple but suffers from DC component buildup and poor sync on long same-bit runs.

**How to draw:** Draw a high line (+V) for every 1, flat line (0V) for every 0.
No transitions in the middle of a bit.

#### RZ (Return to Zero)
- Bit 1 → +V for the first half, then drops to 0 for the second half.
- Bit 0 → stays at 0V throughout.
- Better sync than NRZ but requires double the bandwidth.

**How to draw:** For each 1, draw a pulse that goes up then comes back to 0 at
mid-bit. For 0, stay flat at 0V the whole bit.


### 2. Polar

Uses both positive and negative voltages.
**Voltage levels: +V and −V**

### NRZ-L (Non-Return to Zero Level)
- Bit 1 → +V, Bit 0 → −V. Voltage holds for the full bit duration.
- Simple but loses sync on long runs of the same bit.

**How to draw:** +V for 1, −V for 0. Change level only when the bit changes.

#### NRZ-I (Non-Return to Zero Inversion)
- Bit 1 → **transition** at the beginning of the bit (flip whatever level you are on).
- Bit 0 → **no transition**, stays at same level.
- Better than NRZ-L for runs of 1s since each 1 forces a change.

**How to draw:** Start at any level. For each 1, flip the level at the bit boundary.
For each 0, continue at the same level.

#### RZ (Polar)
- Bit 1 → +V for first half, returns to 0 for second half.
- Bit 0 → −V for first half, returns to 0 for second half.
- Every bit returns to 0 in the middle — excellent sync, but high bandwidth.

**Voltage levels: +V, 0, −V**

**How to draw:** For 1, draw a positive half-pulse then drop to 0. For 0, draw a
negative half-pulse then return to 0.


### 3. Bipolar

Uses three voltage levels. 0 bits are always 0V. 1 bits alternate between +V and −V.
**Voltage levels: +V, 0, −V** (NRZ)

#### AMI (Alternate Mark Inversion)
- Bit 0 → 0V always.
- Bit 1 → alternates between +V and −V with each successive 1.
- No DC component. If two consecutive 1s have the same sign, it means an error
  occurred — good error detection.

**How to draw:** Keep 0V for all 0 bits. For the first 1 use +V, next 1 use −V,
next 1 use +V again, and so on — strictly alternating.

### Pseudo-Ternary
- Exact opposite of AMI.
- Bit 1 → 0V always.
- Bit 0 → alternates between +V and −V with each successive 0.

**How to draw:** Same as AMI but swap the roles of 0 and 1.


### 4. Biphase

A transition **always occurs in the middle of every bit** — guarantees
synchronization. This mid-bit transition is the defining feature of all
biphase schemes.
**Voltage levels: +V and −V**

#### Manchester
- Bit 1 → transition from **+V to −V** at mid-bit.
- Bit 0 → transition from **−V to +V** at mid-bit.
- Used in **Ethernet (IEEE 802.3)**. Excellent synchronization.

**How to draw:** Every bit is split into two halves. For 1: first half +V, second
half −V. For 0: first half −V, second half +V. There may also be a transition at
the bit boundary if adjacent bits require it.

#### Differential Manchester
- **Mid-bit transition always present** (for clocking — same as Manchester).
- Bit 0 → **additional transition at the START** of the bit.
- Bit 1 → **no transition at the start** of the bit.
- More noise-resistant since logic depends on presence/absence of transition,
  not its direction. Used in **Token Ring (IEEE 802.5)**.

**How to draw:** Always draw a mid-bit transition for every bit. Then, at the
start of each bit — add a transition for 0, do nothing for 1.


### Voltage Level Summary

| Scheme | Voltage Levels |
|---|---|
| Unipolar NRZ / RZ | 0, +V |
| Polar NRZ-L / NRZ-I | +V, −V |
| Polar RZ | +V, 0, −V |
| AMI / Pseudo-Ternary | +V, 0, −V |
| Manchester | +V, −V |
| Differential Manchester | +V, −V |
