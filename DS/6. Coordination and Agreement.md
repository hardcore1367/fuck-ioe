# 6. Coordination and Agreement

**Coordination** organizes the actions of distributed processes, while **agreement** makes them choose the same result through message exchange.

## 1. Mutual Exclusion in Distributed Systems

**Distributed mutual exclusion** ensures that only one process at a time executes the **critical section (CS)** that accesses a shared resource. Since distributed processes have no shared memory or common semaphore, they must coordinate entry by exchanging messages.

### 1.1 Fundamental Requirements

1. **Mutual exclusion:** At most one process can execute the critical section at any instant.
2. **No deadlock:** When processes request the critical section, at least one requester must eventually be allowed to enter.
3. **No starvation:** Every valid requester must eventually get an opportunity to enter.
4. **Fair ordering:** Competing requests should be served according to an agreed order, such as Lamport timestamp order.
5. **Fault handling:** Failure of a process, coordinator, link, or token should not permanently stop the system or permit two processes to enter together.

### 1.2 Assumptions and Performance Criteria

The basic mutual-exclusion algorithms normally assume an asynchronous system, reliable message delivery, and no process failure during one execution. A fault-tolerant implementation must add failure detection and recovery without creating two coordinators or duplicate tokens.

Algorithms are commonly evaluated using:

1. **Message cost or bandwidth:** The number of messages required for one critical-section entry and exit.
2. **Client and synchronization delay:** The time from requesting access until entry, and the delay between one process leaving and the next process entering.
3. **Throughput:** The number of critical-section executions completed per unit time when several processes compete.

### 1.3 Token-Based and Non-Token-Based Mutual Exclusion

| S.N. | Token-based mutual exclusion | Non-token-based mutual exclusion |
|---:|---|---|
| 1 | A unique logical token gives its holder permission to enter the critical section. | A process receives permission messages or a grant before entering the critical section. |
| 2 | The requesting process must wait until it possesses the token. | The requesting process must satisfy the permission rule used by the algorithm. |
| 3 | Request order depends on token movement or request information stored with the token. | Request order usually depends on a coordinator queue or logical timestamps. |
| 4 | Token transfer may require fewer messages when several processes are competing. | Permission-based algorithms exchange request and reply messages for each entry. |
| 5 | A circulating token may create traffic even when no process needs the resource. | Most non-token algorithms create no traffic when there is no request. |
| 6 | Loss, duplication, or holder failure is the main danger because exactly one token must exist. | Coordinator failure or a missing permission reply can block progress. |
| 7 | Ricart–Agrawala's token-based algorithm and Token Ring are common examples. | Centralized Coordinator and Ricart–Agrawala's permission-based algorithm are common examples. |

## 2. Algorithms for Mutual Exclusion

### 2.1 Centralized Coordinator Algorithm

The **Centralized Coordinator algorithm** selects one process as coordinator. The coordinator records whether the critical section is free and maintains a queue of waiting processes.

#### 2.1.1 Algorithmic Steps

1. A process sends **REQUEST** to the coordinator.
2. If the critical section is free, the coordinator marks it busy and sends **GRANT**.
3. If it is busy, the coordinator places the requester in a FIFO queue.
4. The process enters the critical section only after receiving **GRANT**.
5. On exit, the process sends **RELEASE** to the coordinator.
6. The coordinator grants access to the next queued process or marks the resource free.

It requires three messages for each use of the critical section: **REQUEST**, **GRANT**, and **RELEASE**.

**Advantages**

- It is simple to understand and implement.
- Its FIFO queue provides fair service.
- Its message cost is low.

**Limitations**

- The coordinator is a single point of failure.
- It may become a performance bottleneck.
- After a failure, election of a new coordinator must avoid two active coordinators.

### 2.2 Ricart–Agrawala Permission-Based Algorithm

The first **Ricart–Agrawala algorithm** is a non-token and fully distributed algorithm. A requester must receive permission from every other process before entering the critical section.

Every process uses a Lamport clock. Requests are ordered by **(timestamp, process ID)**. The smaller timestamp gets priority; if timestamps are equal, the smaller process ID gets priority.

#### 2.2.1 Algorithmic Steps

**When process $P_i$ requests the critical section:**

1. It changes its state to **WANTED**, increments its clock, and records timestamp $T_i$.
2. It sends **REQUEST($T_i$, $i$)** to all other $n-1$ processes.
3. It waits for a **REPLY** from every other process.
4. After receiving all replies, it changes its state to **HELD** and enters the critical section.

**When process $P_j$ receives the request:**

1. If $P_j$ is **RELEASED**, it replies immediately.
2. If $P_j$ is **HELD**, it defers the reply until it exits.
3. If $P_j$ is also **WANTED**, it compares the two requests:
   - It defers the reply when its own request has the smaller **(timestamp, ID)** pair.
   - Otherwise, it replies immediately.

**When $P_i$ exits:**

1. It changes its state to **RELEASED**.
2. It sends **REPLY** to every process whose request it deferred.

For example, requests $(5,1)$ and $(5,2)$ have the same timestamp. Process $P_1$ receives priority because its ID is smaller, so $P_2$ can enter only after $P_1$ exits and sends its deferred reply.

#### 2.2.2 Correctness and Cost

- **Mutual exclusion:** Two competing processes cannot both collect all replies because the lower-priority requester must wait for the higher-priority requester.
- **Fairness:** Timestamp and process ID create a deterministic total order.
- **Message cost:** Each entry needs $n-1$ requests and $n-1$ replies:

$$
2(n-1)\text{ messages}
$$

- **Main advantage:** It removes the central coordinator.
- **Main limitation:** One crashed or unreachable process can block every requester because permission is required from all processes.

### 2.3 Ricart–Agrawala Token-Based Algorithm

The second **Ricart–Agrawala algorithm** uses exactly one token. Only the token holder may enter the critical section, and request messages tell the holder which processes are waiting.

Each process $P_i$ records the latest request received from $P_j$ in `request_i[j]`. The token carries `token[j]`, the timestamp of the latest request from $P_j$ that has already been served. Therefore,

$$
\mathrm{request}_i[j] > \mathrm{token}[j]
$$

means that $P_j$ has a pending request.

#### 2.3.1 Token States

Each process is in one of three states:

1. **NO-TOKEN:** The process does not own the token. If it needs the critical section, it broadcasts a request and enters only after receiving the token.
2. **TOKEN-PRESENT:** The process owns the token but is not using the critical section. It can enter immediately for a local request or pass the token to a pending remote requester.
3. **TOKEN-HELD:** The process owns the token and is executing the critical section. After leaving, it changes to TOKEN-PRESENT before deciding whether to keep or transfer the token.

The main transitions are **NO-TOKEN → TOKEN-HELD** after a requester receives the token, **TOKEN-PRESENT → TOKEN-HELD** for a local request, **TOKEN-HELD → TOKEN-PRESENT** on exit, and **TOKEN-PRESENT → NO-TOKEN** when the token is transferred.

#### 2.3.2 Algorithmic Steps

1. Initially, all request and token values are zero, and one process owns the token.
2. A process that already owns the idle token enters the critical section directly.
3. A process without the token timestamps its request and multicasts it to the other $n-1$ processes.
4. Every receiver records the latest request value for that process.
5. An idle token holder transfers the token to a pending requester. A holder already inside the critical section transfers it only after exit.
6. On exit, process $P_i$ sets `token[i] = request_i[i]` to record that its latest request has been served.
7. It searches the processes in cyclic order and gives the token to the first $P_j$ for which `request_i[j] > token[j]`. If none is pending, it keeps the idle token.

#### 2.3.3 Evaluation

- A requester that already has the idle token enters without sending a message. Otherwise, it uses $n-1$ request messages and one token-transfer message, giving $n$ messages.
- Failure of a non-holder need not block progress after the failure is detected and its stale request is excluded. Without this recovery, the token could be sent to a failed process.
- The idle token need not circulate continuously.
- Loss of the token or failure of its holder stops progress. Recovery must create exactly one replacement token.

### 2.4 Token-Ring Algorithm

The **Token-Ring algorithm** arranges all processes in a logical ring. Each process knows its clockwise successor, and a unique token continuously follows the ring.

#### 2.4.1 Algorithmic Steps

1. The system creates one token and gives it to one process.
2. A process that does not need the critical section immediately forwards the token to its successor.
3. A requester waits until the token arrives.
4. The requester keeps the token, executes the critical section once, and then passes the token to its successor.

For the ring $P_1 \rightarrow P_2 \rightarrow P_3 \rightarrow P_4 \rightarrow P_1$, if $P_3$ is waiting while $P_1$ has the token, the token passes through $P_2$ to $P_3$.

#### 2.4.2 Evaluation

**Advantages**

- The unique token makes mutual exclusion simple.
- Every process receives the token in order, so the algorithm is fair and starvation-free in a failure-free ring.
- It needs neither timestamp comparisons nor permission from all processes.

**Limitations**

- The token may circulate even when no process needs the resource.
- A requester may wait for up to $n-1$ token transfers.
- A process or link failure can break the ring.
- Token loss requires safe regeneration, while token duplication violates mutual exclusion.

## 3. Distributed Elections

A **distributed election algorithm** selects exactly one active process as coordinator and makes all correct processes agree on that coordinator. Election is needed when the coordinator fails, the system starts without a leader, or a higher-priority process recovers.

Both common algorithms assume unique process IDs and select the active process with the highest ID.

An election has two main phases: first select the highest-priority active process, and then inform every correct process about the winner.

### 3.1 Bully Election Algorithm

In the **Bully algorithm**, every process knows all other processes. A process can become coordinator only after checking whether any higher-ID process is active.

#### 3.1.1 Algorithmic Steps

1. Process $P_i$ detects coordinator failure and sends **ELECTION** to every process with a higher ID.
2. If no higher process replies before timeout, $P_i$ declares itself coordinator and sends **COORDINATOR** to the other processes.
3. If a higher process sends **OK**, $P_i$ stops competing and waits for the coordinator announcement. If that announcement does not arrive before timeout, it starts a new election because the higher process may also have failed.
4. Each higher process that receives **ELECTION** replies **OK** and starts its own election.
5. Eventually, the highest active process receives no reply from above and announces itself as coordinator.
6. A recovered high-ID process starts another election and may replace the current coordinator.

**Example:** If $P_5$ has failed and $P_2$ starts an election, active processes $P_3$ and $P_4$ reply and conduct higher elections. Since no process above $P_4$ replies, $P_4$ becomes coordinator.

#### 3.1.2 Evaluation

- It always selects the highest active process.
- Its best case is efficient when a high-ID process starts the election.
- Its worst case uses $O(n^2)$ messages when a low-ID process starts.
- Every process must know the complete membership, and repeated failures can create heavy election traffic.

### 3.2 Ring-Based Election Algorithm

The **Ring-based election algorithm** arranges processes in a logical ring. Each process sends election messages through its active successor.

#### 3.2.1 Algorithmic Steps

1. A process detecting coordinator failure sends **ELECTION(its ID)** to its successor.
2. A receiver forwards the larger of its own ID and the candidate ID in the message.
3. When an election message returns to the process whose ID it carries, that process knows it has the highest active ID.
4. The winner sends **ELECTED(winner ID)** around the ring.
5. Each process records the coordinator and forwards the announcement until it returns to the winner.

**Example:** In the ring $P_4 \rightarrow P_1 \rightarrow P_7 \rightarrow P_3 \rightarrow P_4$, an election eventually carries ID $7$. When it returns to $P_7$, that process announces itself as coordinator.

#### 3.2.2 Evaluation

- With one initiator in a failure-free ring, an election and its announcement use $O(n)$ messages. Concurrent initiators may create additional election traffic.
- Each process mainly needs to know its successor rather than every member.
- The election may be slow because messages traverse the ring.
- A failed member must be bypassed or the ring must be repaired.

### 3.3 Bully and Ring Election Comparison

| S.N. | Bully algorithm | Ring-based algorithm |
|---:|---|---|
| 1 | Every process knows all other participating processes. | Each process mainly needs to know its active successor. |
| 2 | Election messages are sent directly to higher-ID processes. | Election messages travel through a logical ring. |
| 3 | The highest active process that receives no reply announces itself. | The highest ID completes a full ring and its owner announces itself. |
| 4 | The worst case requires $O(n^2)$ messages. | One election normally requires $O(n)$ messages. |
| 5 | The winner broadcasts the coordinator announcement. | The elected message travels once around the ring. |
| 6 | Its main weaknesses are complete membership knowledge and high election traffic. | Its main weaknesses are election delay and the need to repair a broken ring. |

## 4. Multicast Communication

**Multicast communication** sends one logical message from a sender to a selected group of processes, whereas **broadcast** sends to every process in the system. A single group operation lets the implementation distribute the message more efficiently than independent unicasts to every member.

### 4.1 Reliable Multicast Properties

1. **Efficiency:** The implementation can use a distribution tree or network multicast support so the same message is not sent repeatedly over each communication link.
2. **Reliable delivery:** A valid multicast from a correct sender should reach every correct group member.
3. **Integrity and agreement:** A process should not deliver a duplicate or invented message, and correct members should agree on which messages are delivered.
4. **Delivery order:** The service may preserve sender order, causal order, or one common total order, depending on the application.
5. **Group membership:** The system must know which processes belong to the group for each multicast operation.

Message identifiers, sequence numbers, acknowledgements, and retransmission can be used to detect duplicates and recover missing messages.

Reliable multicast is mainly used by replicated servers, group notifications, and replicated state machines.

## 5. Consensus in Distributed Systems

**Consensus** makes a group of processes choose one common value from the values they propose.

### 5.1 Consensus Requirements

1. **Agreement:** No two correct processes decide different values.
2. **Validity:** The decided value must have been proposed by some process.
3. **Termination:** Every correct process eventually decides when the algorithm's assumptions hold.
4. **Integrity:** A process decides at most once and cannot later change its decision.

Agreement, validity, and integrity are **safety properties**, while termination is a **liveness property**.

### 5.2 Need and Applications

Consensus is required whenever several processes must maintain one common decision despite executing independently. Important applications are:

1. keeping replicated state machines in the same state;
2. electing one leader or coordinator;
3. creating one globally ordered fault-tolerant log;
4. agreeing on group membership or system configuration; and
5. deciding whether a distributed transaction commits or aborts.

### 5.3 Simple Coordinator-Based Agreement

A simple failure-free approach works as follows:

1. Every process sends its proposal or vote to a coordinator.
2. The coordinator applies the agreed decision rule, such as selecting one valid proposal or choosing commit only when every participant votes to commit.
3. The coordinator sends the decision to all participants.
4. Each participant records the same decision and does not decide again.

Leader election and two-phase commit illustrate this form of agreement. It works only while the coordinator and participants are alive and can communicate; additional fault-tolerant protocols are required when these assumptions do not hold.

### 5.4 Main Challenges

- A timeout cannot prove whether a process has crashed or is only slow.
- A process or coordinator may fail after only some participants receive a proposed decision.
- Communication failures or network partitions can prevent processes from learning one common result.
- Consensus requires clearly stated assumptions about failures, timing, and which processes remain reachable; without suitable assumptions, progress cannot always be guaranteed.
