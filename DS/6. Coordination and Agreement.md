# 6. Coordination and Agreement

**Coordination** allows processes on different machines to organize their actions, while **agreement** allows them to reach the same decision even though they communicate only through messages. These ideas are needed when processes share a resource, select a coordinator, communicate as a group, or maintain the same distributed state.

## 1. Mutual Exclusion in Distributed Systems

**Distributed mutual exclusion** ensures that at most one process at a time executes the **critical section (CS)** that accesses a shared resource. Examples include updating a shared file, allocating a printer, or changing a replicated account record.

Unlike a single computer, a distributed system has no shared semaphore, common memory, or perfectly synchronized global clock. Therefore, processes coordinate entry to the critical section by exchanging messages.

A process normally uses the following interface:

```text
enter()            // request permission; wait if necessary
resourceAccess()   // execute the critical section
exit()             // release permission for another process
```

### 1.1 Fundamental Requirements

1. **Mutual exclusion (safety):** At most one process may be inside the critical section at any instant.
2. **No deadlock (progress):** If one or more processes request the critical section, some requesting process must eventually be allowed to enter it. Processes must not wait forever for messages that will never arrive.
3. **No starvation:** Every valid requester must eventually get a chance to enter the critical section, even when other processes repeatedly request it.
4. **Fairness:** Requests should be served in an agreed order, commonly the order defined by logical timestamps. A process must not continuously overtake an older request.
5. **Fault tolerance and recovery:** A process, communication link, coordinator, or token failure should be detected and handled so that the system can continue without permitting two processes to enter together.

Basic mutual-exclusion algorithms usually assume that processes do not fail while the algorithm is running, process identifiers are unique, and messages are delivered reliably. Practical systems add timeouts, failure detectors, elections, and recovery rules because a timeout alone cannot prove whether a remote process has failed or is only slow.

### 1.2 Performance Criteria

Let `n` be the number of participating processes.

- **Message complexity:** The number of messages required for one critical-section entry and exit.
- **Client delay:** The time between sending an entry request and receiving permission when the critical section is free.
- **Synchronization delay:** The time between one process leaving the critical section and the next process entering it.
- **Throughput:** The number of critical-section executions completed per unit time. Lower communication and synchronization delays normally give higher throughput.

### 1.3 Token-Based and Non-Token-Based Approaches

| Basis | Token-based mutual exclusion | Non-token-based mutual exclusion |
|---|---|---|
| Proof of permission | A unique logical **token** gives the right to enter the critical section. | A process collects permission messages or receives a grant from a coordinator. |
| Entry condition | The requesting process must possess the token. | The requesting process must satisfy the permission rule of the algorithm. |
| Request ordering | Determined by token movement or by pending-request information stored with the token. | Usually determined by a coordinator queue or Lamport timestamp order. |
| Messages under contention | Token transfer can require few messages once requests are known. | Permission-based algorithms normally exchange request and reply messages for every entry. |
| Traffic while idle | A circulating-token algorithm may continue sending the token even when no process needs the resource. | No request means no mutual-exclusion traffic in most non-token algorithms. |
| Main failure risk | Loss or duplication of the token, or failure of the token holder, can stop the system or violate safety. | Failure of a coordinator or of a process whose reply is required can block progress. |
| Recovery | Must locate or regenerate exactly one token; generating two tokens violates mutual exclusion. | Must elect a new coordinator or stop waiting for failed members without creating conflicting grants. |
| Examples | Ricart–Agrawala second algorithm and Token-Ring algorithm. | Centralized Coordinator and Ricart–Agrawala permission-based algorithm. |

## 2. Algorithms for Mutual Exclusion

### 2.1 Centralized Coordinator Algorithm

The **Centralized Coordinator algorithm** selects one process as the coordinator. The coordinator stores the resource state and a queue of waiting requests, and it alone grants permission to enter the critical section.

#### 2.1.1 Algorithmic Steps

1. A process `P_i` sends `REQUEST` to the coordinator.
2. If the critical section is free, the coordinator marks it busy and sends `GRANT` to `P_i`.
3. If it is busy, the coordinator places `P_i` in a request queue. It does not send a grant yet.
4. After receiving `GRANT`, `P_i` enters and executes the critical section.
5. On leaving, `P_i` sends `RELEASE` to the coordinator.
6. The coordinator removes the next process from the queue and sends it `GRANT`; if the queue is empty, it marks the critical section free.

For example, if `P_1` is using the resource and `P_2` and `P_3` request it, the coordinator queues them. After `P_1` releases the resource, it grants access to `P_2`, followed by `P_3`.

> Insert a diagram of the **Centralized Coordinator mutual-exclusion algorithm** here. It should show requesting processes, the coordinator and its FIFO queue, and the `REQUEST`, `GRANT`, and `RELEASE` messages.

#### 2.1.2 Evaluation

**Advantages**

- It is simple to understand and implement because all decisions are made at one place.
- It needs only **three messages per critical-section use**: `REQUEST`, `GRANT`, and `RELEASE`.
- A FIFO request queue provides fair service and prevents starvation.

**Limitations**

- The coordinator is a **single point of failure**. If it crashes, no new request can be granted until another coordinator is elected.
- It can become a communication and performance bottleneck when many processes request the resource.
- Recovery must prevent **split brain**, in which two processes both believe they are the coordinator and issue conflicting grants.

### 2.2 Ricart–Agrawala Permission-Based Algorithm

The first **Ricart–Agrawala algorithm** is a non-token, fully distributed mutual-exclusion algorithm. A process obtains permission from every other participating process before entering the critical section. There is no central coordinator.

Every process maintains a Lamport logical clock. Requests are placed in the total order `(timestamp, process ID)`, where the lower timestamp has priority and the lower process ID breaks a timestamp tie:

$$
(T_i,i)<(T_j,j)
\iff
T_i<T_j\;\text{or}\;(T_i=T_j\;\text{and}\;i<j)
$$

Each process is in one of three states:

| State | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `RELEASED` | The process is not requesting or executing the critical section. |
| `WANTED` | It has requested the critical section and is waiting for replies. |
| `HELD` | It is executing the critical section. |

#### 2.2.1 Algorithmic Steps

**When process `P_i` requests the critical section:**

1. It changes its state to `WANTED` and increments its Lamport clock.
2. It saves the request timestamp `T_i` and multicasts `REQUEST(T_i, i)` to all other `n-1` processes.
3. It waits until a `REPLY` has arrived from every other process.
4. It changes its state to `HELD` and enters the critical section.

**When process `P_j` receives `REQUEST(T_i, i)`:**

1. It updates its Lamport clock from the received timestamp.
2. If its state is `RELEASED`, it sends `REPLY` immediately.
3. If its state is `HELD`, it defers the reply and places `P_i` in a deferred-reply set.
4. If its state is `WANTED`, it compares its own request `(T_j,j)` with `(T_i,i)`:
   - If `(T_j,j)<(T_i,i)`, its own request has priority, so it defers the reply to `P_i`.
   - Otherwise, `P_i` has priority, so it sends `REPLY` immediately.

**When `P_i` leaves the critical section:**

1. It changes its state to `RELEASED`.
2. It sends `REPLY` to every process in its deferred-reply set and then clears the set.

If `P_1` and `P_2` both request at timestamp `5`, the pairs are `(5,1)` and `(5,2)`. Therefore, `P_1` has priority. `P_2` replies to `P_1`, while `P_1` defers its reply to `P_2`. `P_2` can enter only after `P_1` exits and sends the deferred reply.

> [!NOTE]
> Insert a message-sequence diagram of the **Ricart–Agrawala permission-based algorithm** here. It should show two simultaneous requests, Lamport timestamps, immediate and deferred `REPLY` messages, critical-section entry, and the reply sent on release.

#### 2.2.2 Correctness and Cost

- **Mutual exclusion:** For two competing processes, the request with lower `(timestamp, ID)` gets priority. The lower-priority process cannot collect all replies until the higher-priority process releases the critical section, so both cannot enter together.
- **Fairness:** Lamport timestamps and process IDs give every request a deterministic total order.
- **Message complexity:** Each entry uses `n-1` request messages and `n-1` replies, for a total of:

$$
2(n-1)\text{ messages}
$$

- **Advantages:** It removes the central coordinator and needs fewer messages than Lamport's queue-based distributed mutual-exclusion algorithm.
- **Limitations:** A requester must receive a reply from every other process. Therefore, one crashed or unreachable process can block all progress unless membership and failure recovery are added. It also generates high traffic in a large system.

### 2.3 Ricart–Agrawala Token-Based Algorithm

The second **Ricart–Agrawala algorithm** is token-based. Exactly one token exists, and only its owner can enter the critical section. Requests are still multicast so that the token holder can discover waiting processes.

Each process records the latest request timestamp seen from every process in `request[j]`. The token carries an array `served[j]`, which records the timestamp of the latest request of `P_j` that has already used the token. Thus:

$$
request[j]>served[j]
$$

means that `P_j` has a pending request.

The token state of a process can be represented as follows:

| State | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `NO_TOKEN` | The process does not possess the token. |
| `TOKEN_PRESENT` | It possesses the token but is not using the critical section. |
| `TOKEN_HELD` | It possesses the token and is executing the critical section. |

#### 2.3.1 Algorithmic Steps

1. **Initialization:** Set every `request[j]` and `served[j]` to `0`. Give the only token to one process, whose state is `TOKEN_PRESENT`; all other processes start in `NO_TOKEN`.
2. **Request:** When `P_i` needs the critical section, it first checks its token state:
   - If its state is `TOKEN_PRESENT`, it changes to `TOKEN_HELD` and enters immediately without sending request messages.
   - If its state is `NO_TOKEN`, it increments its logical clock, records its request timestamp, multicasts `REQUEST(T_i,i)` to the other `n-1` processes, and waits for the token.
3. **Record a request:** On receiving `REQUEST(T_j,j)`, a process updates `request[j]` to the larger timestamp. If it owns the token and is not in the critical section, it transfers the token to a pending requester. If it is in the critical section, it keeps the token until it exits.
4. **Enter:** On receiving the token, the requester changes its state to `TOKEN_HELD` and enters the critical section.
5. **Release:** On exit, `P_i` changes its state to `TOKEN_PRESENT` and sets `served[i]=request[i]` in the token.
6. **Select the next owner:** It searches processes in cyclic order `i+1, i+2, ..., n, 1, ..., i-1`. The first `P_j` for which `request[j]>served[j]` receives the token. If there is no pending request, `P_i` retains the idle token.

#### 2.3.2 Evaluation

- If the requester does not already own the token, one entry normally requires `n-1` request messages and one token-transfer message, or **`n` messages**.
- A failed process that does not hold the token does not prevent other processes from progressing because replies from every process are not required.
- The token holder can retain an idle token, so the algorithm avoids continuous token circulation.
- Failure of the token holder or loss of the token stops progress. Recovery must create exactly one replacement token; otherwise, duplicate tokens can violate mutual exclusion.

### 2.4 Token-Ring Algorithm

The **Token-Ring mutual-exclusion algorithm** arranges all processes in a logical ring. Each process knows its clockwise successor. The logical ring does not have to match the physical network topology.

#### 2.4.1 Algorithmic Steps

1. The system creates one token and gives it to one process.
2. Every process passes the token to its clockwise successor.
3. A process that does not need the critical section forwards the token immediately.
4. A process that needs the critical section waits until the token arrives, keeps it, and enters the critical section.
5. After leaving the critical section, it sends the token to its successor. It may enter only once during a token visit, which gives other processes a fair chance.

For example, in the ring `P_1 → P_2 → P_3 → P_4 → P_1`, if `P_3` requests the resource while `P_1` has the token, the token moves through `P_2` to `P_3`. `P_3` enters the critical section and then passes it to `P_4`.

> [!NOTE]
> Insert a diagram of the **Token-Ring mutual-exclusion algorithm** here. It should show a logical clockwise ring of processes, the unique token, its direction of movement, and one process retaining it while executing the critical section.

#### 2.4.2 Evaluation

**Advantages**

- Mutual exclusion is simple because possession of the unique token is the only permission required.
- It is fair and starvation-free in a failure-free ring because the token visits every process in order.
- It does not need timestamp comparisons or replies from all processes.

**Limitations**

- Even when no process needs the resource, the token may continue to consume network bandwidth.
- A requester may wait for up to `n-1` token transfers; under uniformly distributed positions, the average is about half a ring.
- A process or communication-link failure can break the ring until the failed member is bypassed.
- Loss of the token requires an election and safe regeneration, while accidental token duplication destroys mutual exclusion.

### 2.5 Comparison of Mutual-Exclusion Algorithms

| Algorithm | Control | Messages for an entry | Main strength | Main failure problem |
|---|---|---:|---|---|
| Centralized Coordinator | One coordinator grants access | `3` | Simple and low message cost | Coordinator failure or bottleneck |
| Ricart–Agrawala permission-based | Permission from all other processes | `2(n-1)` | No coordinator or token | Any missing reply can block progress |
| Ricart–Agrawala token-based | Unique token plus request records | About `n` when token transfer is needed | No reply from every process; idle token may be retained | Token-holder failure or token loss |
| Token Ring | Token circulates around a logical ring | `0` to `n-1` token transfers | Simple, fair order | Broken ring, lost token, or duplicate token |

## 3. Distributed Elections

A **distributed election algorithm** selects exactly one active process to perform a special role, usually the coordinator or leader. All correct processes must agree on the same winner.

An election is needed when:

- the current coordinator fails or becomes unreachable;
- the process holding an important centralized role or token fails;
- the system starts without a known coordinator; or
- a higher-priority process recovers and the policy requires it to become coordinator.

A correct election algorithm provides:

- **Safety:** At the end of one election, correct processes do not accept two different coordinators.
- **Liveness:** If enough processes can communicate, the election eventually terminates.
- **Agreement:** Every correct process learns the identity of the same coordinator.
- **Validity:** The selected coordinator is an active eligible process; Bully and Ring algorithms select the active process with the highest identifier.

Both algorithms below assume unique process identifiers. Their use of timeouts also assumes that a non-responding process can eventually be suspected; unreliable failure detection can start unnecessary elections.

### 3.1 Bully Election Algorithm

The **Bully algorithm** lets the active process with the highest identifier become coordinator. A recovering high-ID process can start a new election and replace, or “bully,” a lower-ID coordinator.

Each process knows the identifiers and addresses of all other processes.

#### 3.1.1 Algorithmic Steps

1. When `P_i` suspects that the coordinator has failed, it sends `ELECTION` to every process with an identifier greater than `i`.
2. If no higher process replies before a timeout, `P_i` declares itself coordinator and sends `COORDINATOR(i)` to all lower-ID processes.
3. If any higher process sends `OK`, `P_i` stops competing and waits for a `COORDINATOR` message.
4. Every higher process that receives `ELECTION` sends `OK` to the sender and starts its own election, unless it is already holding one.
5. This continues until the highest active process receives no reply from a higher process. It announces itself as coordinator.
6. If a process received `OK` but no coordinator announcement arrives before timeout, it starts another election because the expected winner may also have failed.
7. A recovered process starts an election. If its identifier is higher than the current coordinator's identifier, it eventually becomes the new coordinator.

**Example:** Processes `P_1` to `P_5` use their numbers as identifiers, and coordinator `P_5` crashes. If `P_2` detects the failure, it sends `ELECTION` to `P_3`, `P_4`, and `P_5`. `P_3` and `P_4` reply `OK` and start their own elections. `P_4` receives no response from `P_5`, so it announces `COORDINATOR(4)`. Every surviving process then accepts `P_4`.

> [!NOTE]
> Insert a message-sequence diagram of the **Bully election algorithm** here. It should show a failed highest-ID coordinator, `ELECTION` messages sent upward, `OK` replies, timeouts, and the highest surviving process broadcasting `COORDINATOR`.

#### 3.1.2 Evaluation

- **Advantage:** It normally selects a powerful or preferred process when higher identifiers represent higher priority.
- **Best case:** When the second-highest process detects failure of the highest process, only a linear number of election and announcement messages is needed.
- **Worst case:** When the lowest process starts the election, many higher processes start further elections, producing `O(n^2)` messages.
- **Limitations:** Every process must know all other members. Repeated failures, recoveries, or false suspicions can produce heavy election traffic.

### 3.2 Ring-Based Election Algorithm

The **Ring-based election algorithm** arranges processes in a unidirectional logical ring. Each process knows only its active successor. An election message circulates around the ring, and the highest active identifier becomes coordinator.

Each process uses a `PARTICIPANT` flag to prevent unnecessary duplicate messages.

#### 3.2.1 Algorithmic Steps

1. A process `P_i` that detects coordinator failure sets `PARTICIPANT = true` and sends `ELECTION(i)` to its successor.
2. When `P_j` receives `ELECTION(k)`:
   - If `k > j`, it forwards `ELECTION(k)` and marks itself as a participant.
   - If `k < j` and it is not already a participant, it replaces the identifier with its own and sends `ELECTION(j)`.
   - If `k < j` and it is already a participant, it discards that lower-priority message.
   - If `k = j`, its own identifier has completed a full ring, so `P_j` is the highest active process and wins.
3. The winner sends `ELECTED(j)` around the ring.
4. Each process records `P_j` as coordinator, clears its participant flag, and forwards the announcement.
5. When `ELECTED(j)` returns to the winner, the election is complete.

**Example:** Suppose the logical order is `P_4 → P_1 → P_7 → P_3 → P_4`. If `P_1` starts an election, `P_7` replaces identifier `1` with `7`. Lower identifiers then forward `7`. When the message returns to `P_7`, it announces itself as the coordinator.

> [!NOTE]
> Insert a diagram of the **Ring-based election algorithm** here. It should show the logical successor links, an `ELECTION` message whose candidate ID is replaced by a higher ID, the maximum-ID winner, and the returning `ELECTED` announcement.

#### 3.2.2 Evaluation

- A single election completes using a linear number of messages, although concurrent initiators can increase the cost to `O(n^2)`.
- Each process needs to know only its successor, so it stores less membership information than in the Bully algorithm.
- The election message must travel around the ring, which may make election slower in a large system.
- A failed process breaks the path until the ring is repaired or the next active successor is found.

### 3.3 Bully and Ring Election Comparison

| Basis | Bully algorithm | Ring-based algorithm |
|---|---|---|
| Membership knowledge | Every process knows all other processes. | A process mainly needs to know its successor. |
| Communication pattern | Messages are sent directly to all higher-ID processes. | Messages move through a logical ring. |
| Winner | Highest active identifier. | Highest active identifier. |
| Typical message cost | Low if a high-ID process starts; worst case `O(n^2)`. | One election is `O(n)`; concurrent elections can approach `O(n^2)`. |
| Announcement | Winner broadcasts to lower processes. | `ELECTED` message completes one ring. |
| Main weakness | Many messages and complete membership knowledge. | Ring repair is required after a member failure. |

## 4. Multicast Communication

**Multicast communication** sends one logical message from a sender to a selected group of processes. The sender performs one multicast operation instead of issuing a separate send operation for every receiver. Sending to every process in the complete system is called **broadcast**.

| Communication type | Destination |
|---|---|
| Unicast | One sender to one receiver |
| Multicast | One sender to the members of a selected group |
| Broadcast | One sender to every process in the system or network scope |

### 4.1 Why Group Communication Is Useful

- **Efficiency:** The implementation knows that the same data is required by many receivers. It can use hardware multicast or a distribution tree so that a communication link carries the message no more than necessary.
- **Lower delivery time:** Different branches of a distribution tree can deliver the message in parallel instead of the sender contacting every receiver serially.
- **Common delivery guarantees:** A group communication service can coordinate whether and in what order members deliver messages. Independent unicast operations cannot easily provide a single guarantee for the whole group if the sender crashes halfway.
- **Common applications:** Replicated servers, distributed notifications, collaborative applications, cluster membership, and replicated state machines use multicast.

### 4.2 Reliable Multicast Properties

Network multicast, such as basic IP multicast, may lose, duplicate, or reorder packets. **Reliable multicast** adds protocol rules so that group members obtain a consistent delivery result despite message loss and crash failures.

Its main properties are:

1. **Validity:** If a correct process multicasts a message, it eventually delivers that message itself.
2. **Integrity:** A process delivers a message at most once and only if that message was actually multicast by its stated sender.
3. **Agreement:** If one correct group member delivers a message, every correct member of the same group eventually delivers it.
4. **Uniform agreement:** If any process delivers a message, including a process that later fails, every correct member eventually delivers that message.

Reliability does not itself define the order of different messages. A multicast service may additionally provide:

- **FIFO order:** Messages from the same sender are delivered in sending order.
- **Causal order:** If sending message `m_1` could have influenced the sending of `m_2`, all members deliver `m_1` before `m_2`.
- **Total order:** All members deliver all messages in exactly the same order. Reliable total-order multicast is commonly called **atomic multicast**.

### 4.3 Working of Reliable Group Communication

A reliable multicast implementation commonly uses the following mechanisms:

1. The sender attaches a unique identifier and sequence number to each message.
2. A distribution tree, hardware multicast, or point-to-point sends carry the message to group members.
3. Receivers record delivered identifiers so that duplicate copies are discarded.
4. Acknowledgements confirm receipt, or negative acknowledgements request missing sequence numbers.
5. The sender or another member retransmits a missing message. Forwarding by receivers is useful if the original sender crashes after reaching only part of the group.
6. A message is considered stable when the required members have received it; only then may stored retransmission copies be deleted.
7. A group-membership service defines a **view** of current members. Join, leave, and failure events install a new view so that delivery guarantees refer to a clearly defined membership set.

A simple **eager reliable multicast** forwards a newly seen message to every group member before delivering it and discards later duplicates. Thus, if the sender reaches one correct process before crashing, that process continues dissemination. Its disadvantage is up to `O(n^2)` message traffic, so practical systems use trees, acknowledgements, or gossip to reduce cost.

## 5. Consensus in Distributed Systems

**Consensus** is the problem of making a group of processes choose one common value from the values they propose. Each process starts with a proposal, communicates with others, and performs an irreversible `decide(value)` operation.

The value may be binary, such as `commit` or `abort`, or it may represent a leader, configuration, log entry, or command. Consensus is easy when every process and link works perfectly, but failures and uncertain message delays make it difficult.

### 5.1 Consensus Requirements

1. **Agreement:** No two correct processes decide different values.
2. **Validity:** The decided value must be a value proposed by some process; the algorithm cannot invent an unrelated value.
3. **Termination:** Every correct process eventually decides if the assumptions required by the algorithm hold.
4. **Integrity:** A process decides at most once and cannot later change its decision.

Agreement and validity are **safety properties**: they prevent an incorrect decision. Termination is a **liveness property**: it requires the system eventually to make progress.

### 5.2 Why Consensus Is Needed

Consensus is used for:

- choosing one leader or coordinator;
- keeping replicated state machines in the same state;
- agreeing on the next entry in a fault-tolerant distributed log;
- installing a common group-membership view;
- deciding whether to commit or abort a distributed transaction; and
- agreeing on which process may perform a unique system-wide action.

Election is therefore a special agreement problem whose value is a process identifier. However, election alone does not solve repeated agreement on application commands or replicated log entries.

### 5.3 General Leader-and-Quorum Working

A common crash-tolerant consensus design uses numbered rounds, a leader, and intersecting majority quorums:

1. **Propose:** Processes submit proposed values for a numbered consensus instance.
2. **Choose a round leader:** An election selects a leader for the current round. A leader coordinates communication but cannot decide alone.
3. **Prepare:** The leader asks a majority for their previously accepted proposals. If any response reports an accepted proposal, the leader must choose the value from the reported proposal with the highest round number. It may select a new valid proposal only when none of the responses contains a previously accepted proposal.
4. **Accept:** The leader sends the selected proposal with its round number. A process persistently accepts it if it has not promised to a higher round.
5. **Decide:** After a majority accepts the same proposal, the leader announces the decision. All correct processes learn and apply the same value.
6. **Recover:** If the leader fails, a higher-numbered round elects another leader. The new leader reads accepted state from a majority before proposing, so it cannot replace an already chosen value.

Any two majority quorums intersect in at least one process. This intersection, together with persistent round information, preserves agreement across retries and leader failures. Progress requires a communicating majority and eventually a leader that remains reachable long enough to complete a round.

**Example:** Five replicas must agree on the next log command. If three accept command `X`, `X` is chosen. A new leader must contact at least three replicas; its majority overlaps the earlier majority, discovers `X`, and cannot safely decide a conflicting command `Y` for that same log position.

### 5.4 Failure and Timing Challenges

- A timeout cannot tell with certainty whether a process crashed, a link failed, or a reply is only delayed.
- A network partition may create separated groups. Allowing both sides to decide independently can violate agreement, so only a side with the required quorum may progress.
- A leader may fail after sending a proposal to only some processes. Round numbers and persistent accepted state allow the next leader to recover safely.
- In a completely asynchronous message-passing system, deterministic consensus cannot guarantee termination if even one process may crash. This is the **FLP impossibility result**. Practical protocols obtain liveness by assuming partial synchrony, using failure detectors, or using randomized techniques, while still preserving safety during delays.
- Byzantine processes may send conflicting or dishonest messages and require stronger consensus protocols than ordinary crash-failure consensus.

### 5.5 Coordination Problems Compared

| Problem | Common decision | Main goal |
|---|---|---|
| Mutual exclusion | Which process may enter the critical section now | Prevent simultaneous access to one shared resource |
| Election | Which active process is the coordinator | Select exactly one leader and inform all processes |
| Reliable multicast | Which messages the group delivers and in what order | Give group-wide delivery guarantees |
| Consensus | Which proposed value all correct processes decide | Maintain one safe decision despite concurrency and failures |
