# 9. Fault Tolerance

**Fault tolerance** is the ability of a distributed system to continue providing an acceptable service even when some components fail. It uses detection, redundancy, recovery, and agreement to prevent local problems from stopping the complete system.

## 1. Faults, Errors, and Failures

### 1.1 Fault–Error–Failure Chain

- A **fault** is the underlying defect or cause, such as damaged hardware, a software bug, or a broken communication link.
- An **error** is an incorrect internal system state produced when a fault becomes active, such as a corrupted memory value.
- A **failure** occurs when the error reaches the service boundary and the system's actual behavior differs from its specified behavior.

The relationship is:

$$
\text{Fault} \rightarrow \text{Error} \rightarrow \text{Failure}
$$

For example, a programming bug is a fault. When the faulty instruction creates a wrong account balance, it produces an error. When a client receives that wrong balance, the service has failed.

Not every fault causes a failure. Redundancy or validation may detect and remove the error before it becomes externally visible.

### 1.2 Types of Faults

**According to duration**

- **Transient fault:** It occurs once and then disappears, such as a temporary network disturbance.
- **Intermittent fault:** It appears repeatedly but unpredictably, such as a loose connection.
- **Permanent fault:** It remains until the faulty component is repaired or replaced.

**According to system behavior**

- **Crash fault:** A process stops and performs no further action.
- **Omission fault:** A process or channel fails to send or receive a required message.
- **Timing fault:** A response occurs outside its permitted time interval.
- **Response fault:** A process returns an incorrect value or performs an incorrect state transition.
- **Arbitrary or Byzantine fault:** A component behaves unpredictably and may send different false information to different processes.

### 1.3 Fault Detection and Handling

Faults are detected using:

- **Timeouts and heartbeats** to suspect crashed or unreachable processes.
- **Checksums and error-detecting codes** to detect corrupted messages or stored data.
- **Assertions and acceptance tests** to identify invalid program states or results.
- **Replication and voting** to compare independently produced results and detect an arbitrary output.
- **Sequence numbers and acknowledgements** to detect missing, duplicate, or delayed communication.

After detection, the system may:

1. **Contain** the error so it does not spread.
2. **Mask** it using a correct replica or majority result.
3. **Reconfigure** the system to exclude the faulty component.
4. **Recover** a correct state by forward or backward recovery.
5. **Repair and reintegrate** the component after its state is synchronized.

### 1.4 $k$-Fault-Tolerant Systems

A system is **$k$-fault tolerant** if it continues satisfying its specification while at most $k$ components fail according to its stated failure model.

The required redundancy depends on the fault type:

- At least $k+1$ replicas are needed to keep one replica available after $k$ crash failures.
- At least $2k+1$ independently computed results are needed to outvote $k$ incorrect results by simple majority.
- Byzantine agreement with unauthenticated oral messages requires at least $3k+1$ processes to tolerate $k$ Byzantine processes.

The fault model must always be stated because a system that tolerates crash faults may not tolerate arbitrary behavior.

### 1.5 Triple Modular Redundancy

**Triple Modular Redundancy (TMR)** runs three identical modules on the same input and sends their outputs to a majority voter. If one module produces an incorrect result, the other two matching outputs determine the service result.

TMR masks one faulty module because it is a $2k+1$ voting system with $k=1$. The voter itself must be highly reliable or replicated because one faulty voter can become a single point of failure.

## 2. Process Resilience

**Process resilience** protects a service by replicating its computation across a process group. If one member fails, another member continues the work.

### 2.1 Flat and Hierarchical Process Groups

| S.N. | Flat process group | Hierarchical process group |
|---:|---|---|
| 1 | Every group member has an equal role. | One coordinator controls several worker processes. |
| 2 | Decisions are distributed and may require voting among members. | The coordinator can make decisions without consulting every worker. |
| 3 | The group has no single coordinator whose crash stops all control. | Coordinator failure can stop the group until a new coordinator is selected. |
| 4 | Communication and decision making create more overhead. | Communication is simpler because workers mainly interact through the coordinator. |
| 5 | It provides stronger resilience but is more complex to implement. | It is easier to implement and scale but depends on coordinator recovery. |

Process groups require membership management, failure detection, request ordering, and state transfer so that a replacement member has the correct state.

## 3. Reliable Client–Server Communication

Client–server communication may fail in several places:

1. The client cannot locate the server.
2. The request is lost before reaching the server.
3. The server crashes before or after executing the request.
4. The reply is lost before reaching the client.
5. The client crashes after sending a request, leaving an **orphan computation** at the server.

A timeout tells the client that no reply arrived, but it cannot determine whether the request was lost, the server crashed, or the operation completed and only the reply was lost.

### 3.1 Techniques for Reliable Calls

- Give every request a unique client ID and sequence number.
- Retransmit a request after timeout.
- Let the server reject duplicate request IDs.
- Cache a completed request's reply so a duplicate receives the earlier result without re-execution.
- Store request and reply information on stable storage when it must survive a crash.
- Use idempotent operations where repeating the operation has the same effect as performing it once.

### 3.2 Call Semantics

| S.N. | At-least-once semantics | At-most-once semantics |
|---:|---|---|
| 1 | The client retries until the operation executes one or more times. | The server uses duplicate filtering so the operation executes zero or one time. |
| 2 | It is simple and suitable for idempotent operations. | It is safer for non-idempotent operations such as transferring money. |
| 3 | A lost reply may cause the operation to execute again. | A lost reply may leave the client uncertain, but the operation is not repeated. |

True **exactly-once** execution is difficult when clients, servers, and networks can fail. Transactional logging can provide an exactly-once effect within stated recovery assumptions.

## 4. Distributed Commit During Recovery

An atomic commit protocol makes every participant in a distributed transaction reach the same commit or abort result. **Two-Phase Commit (2PC)** records protocol states on stable storage so the decision can survive crashes.

### 4.1 Recovery Rules in 2PC

- A recovering coordinator that finds **GLOBAL-COMMIT** or **GLOBAL-ABORT** in its log resends that decision.
- A recovering participant with a commit or abort record repeats that final action if necessary.
- A participant that failed before voting commit may abort locally.
- A participant whose log contains **READY** has promised to commit if instructed. It must ask the coordinator or another participant for the global decision.
- If every process that knows the decision is unavailable, a READY participant remains blocked.

Thus, stable logging preserves atomicity, but basic 2PC cannot guarantee non-blocking progress after coordinator failure.

## 5. Distributed Recovery

**Recovery** restores correct service after an error or failure.

### 5.1 Forward and Backward Recovery

| S.N. | Forward recovery | Backward recovery |
|---:|---|---|
| 1 | The system moves from the erroneous state to a new correct state. | The system returns to an earlier saved correct state. |
| 2 | It applies error-specific correction, compensation, or redundancy. | It restores a checkpoint and may replay logged operations. |
| 3 | It does not repeat all work performed after an old checkpoint. | It loses or repeats work performed after the selected checkpoint. |
| 4 | It requires knowledge of how to correct the particular error. | It can handle unexpected errors when a valid recovery point exists. |
| 5 | Examples include correcting a damaged record or switching to a redundant result. | Examples include transaction rollback and checkpoint restart. |

### 5.2 Independent Checkpointing

In **independent checkpointing**, every process saves its local state without coordinating with other processes.

During recovery, the system must find a set of local checkpoints that forms a consistent global state. Rolling one process back may create an orphan message and force another process to roll back. This can continue repeatedly and cause the **domino effect**, where recovery returns to a very old state.

### 5.3 Coordinated Checkpointing

In **coordinated checkpointing**, processes cooperate to record one consistent global checkpoint.

#### 5.3.1 Basic Procedure

1. A coordinator asks all participating processes to take a tentative checkpoint.
2. Each process records its local state and controls application-message exchange so the resulting global state remains consistent.
3. Every process reports success or failure to the coordinator.
4. If all succeed, the coordinator tells them to make the tentative checkpoints permanent.
5. If any process fails, the coordinator tells all processes to discard the tentative checkpoint.
6. After a failure, every process rolls back to the same committed recovery line.

The method may briefly pause computation and exchange extra control messages, but it prevents the domino effect and simplifies recovery.

### 5.4 Checkpointing Comparison

| S.N. | Independent checkpointing | Coordinated checkpointing |
|---:|---|---|
| 1 | Each process chooses its checkpoint time independently. | Processes cooperate to create one consistent checkpoint. |
| 2 | Normal execution requires little checkpoint coordination. | Checkpoint creation requires control messages and possible process pausing. |
| 3 | Recovery must search for a consistent set of checkpoints. | The latest committed global checkpoint is already consistent. |
| 4 | Rollback propagation and the domino effect are possible. | All processes roll back to one recovery line, so the domino effect is avoided. |
| 5 | It may store several checkpoints per process. | It normally needs fewer retained checkpoint generations. |

### 5.5 Message Logging

With **message logging**, each process periodically checkpoints its state and records received messages. After a crash, the process restores its checkpoint and replays the logged messages to reconstruct the state it had before failure.

Logging reduces lost work, but the recovered state must remain consistent with surviving processes and must not create orphan processes whose states depend on an unrecoverable event.

## 6. Byzantine Faults and Agreement

A **Byzantine fault** is an arbitrary fault in which a process may lie, send different values to different recipients, omit messages, or cooperate with other faulty processes.

### 6.1 Byzantine Generals Problem

Several generals must agree on one action by exchanging messages. Some generals may be traitors who send conflicting orders. The loyal generals must satisfy:

1. **Agreement:** All loyal processes decide the same value.
2. **Validity:** If the commander is loyal, every loyal process decides the commander's value.
3. **Termination:** Every loyal process eventually decides.

### 6.2 Agreement with One Byzantine Fault

To tolerate one Byzantine process with unauthenticated messages, at least four processes are required:

1. The commander sends its value to each lieutenant.
2. Every lieutenant forwards the value it received to every other lieutenant.
3. Each loyal lieutenant collects the commander's value and the forwarded reports.
4. It applies the same majority rule, using a fixed default when no majority exists.

If the commander is loyal, all loyal lieutenants receive and decide its value. If the commander is faulty, the forwarding step exposes its inconsistent reports and all loyal lieutenants still apply the same decision rule.

In general, agreement against $k$ Byzantine processes using unauthenticated messages requires:

$$
n \geq 3k+1
$$

This means more than two-thirds of the processes must be correct. Authentication with unforgeable digital signatures can reduce the replication requirement under different assumptions.
