# 5. Time and State in Distributed Systems

## 1. Time and Physical Clocks

### 1.1 Importance of Time in Distributed Systems

A distributed system has no single clock shared by every computer. Each machine observes events using its own local clock, while messages take an unpredictable amount of time to travel through the network. Therefore, two machines may assign different times or different orders to the same set of events.

Time and event ordering are needed to:

- **Timestamp transactions and logs:** The events at different servers must be placed in a meaningful order for auditing and debugging.
- **Maintain replicated data:** Updates must be ordered so that replicas can resolve conflicts and remain consistent.
- **Coordinate activities:** Processes use timeouts, leases, elections, and schedules to coordinate their work.
- **Check security information:** Authentication protocols use timestamps to reject expired or replayed requests.
- **Detect duplicate or delayed operations:** A server can use time or ordering information to decide whether a request is new.

The main difficulty is that a local clock can drift, network delay is variable, and there is no instantaneous way to read all clocks at the same time.

### 1.2 Physical Clocks

A **physical clock** measures real or wall-clock time. A computer normally uses a quartz crystal oscillator whose ticks are counted and converted into seconds. The operating system maintains a clock value from these ticks and may synchronize it with an external standard such as **Coordinated Universal Time (UTC)**.

Two important clock errors are:

- **Clock skew:** The difference between the readings of two clocks at the same real instant.
- **Clock drift:** The difference between the rates at which two clocks run. A clock that gains or loses time gradually increases skew.

If the drift rate of a clock is bounded by \(\rho\), its rate satisfies

\[
1-\rho \leq \frac{dC(t)}{dt} \leq 1+\rho
\]

where \(C(t)\) is the clock reading at real time \(t\). Thus, clocks must be synchronized periodically because their readings separate again after every correction.

### 1.3 Goals and Types of Physical Clock Synchronization

**Clock synchronization** adjusts distributed clocks so that their readings remain within an acceptable error bound.

- **External synchronization:** Each clock is kept close to an external reference such as UTC. If \(|C_i(t)-UTC(t)|<D\), clock \(C_i\) is externally synchronized within bound \(D\).
- **Internal synchronization:** The clocks are kept close to one another, even if none of them is exactly equal to UTC. If \(|C_i(t)-C_j(t)|<D\), clocks \(C_i\) and \(C_j\) are internally synchronized within bound \(D\).

A correction should normally preserve **monotonicity**: clock time must not move backward. If a clock is ahead, the system can slow it temporarily, called **clock slewing**, instead of immediately setting it to an earlier value.

### 1.4 Cristian's Algorithm

**Cristian's algorithm** externally synchronizes a client with a time server that has access to an accurate UTC source. It works best when the round-trip network delay is small and approximately symmetric.

**Algorithm:**

1. At local time \(T_0\), the client sends a time request to the server.
2. The server reads its clock and returns timestamp \(S\), taken as close as possible to sending the reply.
3. At local time \(T_1\), the client receives the reply and calculates the round-trip time:

   \[
   RTT=T_1-T_0
   \]

4. Assuming equal delay in both directions, the client estimates that \(RTT/2\) elapsed after timestamp \(S\) and sets its clock to

   \[
   C_{new}=S+\frac{RTT}{2}
   \]

5. The client repeats the request periodically. A sample with the smallest \(RTT\) is usually preferred because it is likely to contain the least queueing delay.

If the minimum possible one-way transmission time is \(d_{min}\), the uncertainty of the estimate is at most

\[
\pm\left(\frac{RTT}{2}-d_{min}\right)
\]

**Advantages:**

- It is simple and gives a client UTC-based time.
- Its error can be estimated from the measured round-trip time.

**Limitations:**

- Unequal request and reply delays make the \(RTT/2\) estimate inaccurate.
- A single time server is a performance bottleneck and a single point of failure.
- A faulty server may return an incorrect time unless several trusted servers are consulted.
- Long or highly variable Internet delays reduce accuracy.

### 1.5 Berkeley Algorithm

The **Berkeley algorithm** provides internal synchronization when no machine must be synchronized directly to UTC. One machine acts as a **master (time daemon)** and periodically brings all clocks, including its own, close to the group average.

**Algorithm:**

1. A master is selected, usually by an election algorithm.
2. The master polls every slave for its current clock value.
3. It estimates each slave's reading while allowing for half of the measured round-trip time.
4. It discards clocks that are faulty or far outside an acceptable range.
5. It computes the average offset of the remaining clocks, including its own clock.
6. Instead of sending an absolute time, the master sends each machine the positive or negative adjustment it must make. This avoids additional error caused by reply delay.
7. The machines slew their clocks by the requested amounts, and the procedure is repeated periodically.

**Example:** Suppose the master reads 3:00 and two slaves read 2:50 and 3:25. Their offsets from the master are \(0\), \(-10\), and \(+25\) minutes. The average offset is

\[
\frac{0-10+25}{3}=+5\text{ minutes}
\]

The target group time is therefore 3:05. The master advances by 5 minutes, the slow slave advances by 15 minutes, and the fast slave slows or moves toward a correction of \(-20\) minutes.

**Advantages:**

- No external UTC receiver is required.
- Averaging reduces the effect of ordinary fast and slow clocks.
- Outlier readings can be ignored before calculating the average.

**Limitations:**

- The result is internally consistent but may differ from UTC.
- Failure of the master requires another election.
- Network-delay estimates and a faulty master can reduce accuracy.

### 1.6 Network Time Protocol (NTP)

The **Network Time Protocol (NTP)** is an Internet-scale protocol that synchronizes computer clocks with UTC. Unlike a scheme using only one server, NTP uses a hierarchy of redundant time servers and selects reliable, low-delay observations.

**NTP hierarchy:**

| Level | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Stratum 0** | Highly accurate reference devices such as atomic, radio, or GPS clocks; they are not ordinary network servers. |
| **Stratum 1** | Primary time servers directly connected to stratum-0 references. |
| **Stratum 2 and above** | Secondary servers synchronized with lower-stratum servers. A larger stratum number is farther from the reference. |

This hierarchy forms a **synchronization subnet**. A client normally consults several servers so that it can reject faulty sources and continue operating when a server or network path fails.

**Communication modes:**

- **Client-server mode:** A client requests timestamps from a server and calculates delay and offset.
- **Symmetric peer mode:** Two servers exchange timestamps and synchronize one another; this is useful among higher-level servers.
- **Broadcast or multicast mode:** A server periodically sends time to many clients. It has low overhead but gives less accurate delay estimation.

**Offset and delay calculation:** Let client \(A\) send a request at \(T_1\), server \(B\) receive it at \(T_2\), server \(B\) send the response at \(T_3\), and client \(A\) receive it at \(T_4\). NTP estimates the clock offset of the server relative to the client as

\[
\theta=\frac{(T_2-T_1)+(T_3-T_4)}{2}
\]

and the total network delay as

\[
\delta=(T_4-T_1)-(T_3-T_2)
\]

The client adjusts its clock by approximately \(\theta\). NTP keeps multiple samples, prefers samples with smaller delay, filters noisy measurements, and usually slews the clock to avoid moving time backward.

**Advantages:**

- It provides accurate UTC synchronization across large networks.
- Redundant servers and multiple strata improve availability and fault tolerance.
- Filtering several timestamp exchanges reduces the effect of variable delay.

**Limitations:**

- Accuracy is limited by asymmetric routes, congestion, and variable network delay.
- Misconfigured or malicious servers can supply false time unless sources are authenticated and checked.
- A server far from an accurate reference normally provides less accurate time.

## 2. Logical Time

### 2.1 Logical Clocks and the Happened-Before Relation

A **logical clock** is a software counter that represents the causal order of events rather than real time. It is useful when the system only needs to know whether one event could have influenced another.

Lamport defined the **happened-before relation**, written \(a\rightarrow b\), using three rules:

1. **Process order:** If \(a\) and \(b\) occur in the same process and \(a\) occurs first, then \(a\rightarrow b\).
2. **Message order:** If \(a\) is the sending of a message and \(b\) is the receipt of that message, then \(a\rightarrow b\).
3. **Transitivity:** If \(a\rightarrow b\) and \(b\rightarrow c\), then \(a\rightarrow c\).

If neither \(a\rightarrow b\) nor \(b\rightarrow a\), the events are **concurrent**, written \(a\parallel b\). Concurrent events have no known causal relationship even if one happens to receive a smaller timestamp.

### 2.2 Physical Clock versus Logical Clock

| Basis | Physical clock | Logical clock |
|---|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Measures approximate real or wall-clock time. | Represents event order and causality. |
| **Implementation** | Hardware oscillator plus an operating-system clock. | Software counter or vector updated at events. |
| **Reference** | May be synchronized to UTC or another physical clock. | Uses process order and message exchange; UTC is unnecessary. |
| **Main error** | Suffers from drift, skew, and network-delay uncertainty. | Does not measure elapsed real time; its order may be incomplete or arbitrary. |
| **Meaning of timestamps** | A timestamp estimates when an event occurred. | A timestamp shows an event's logical position or causal history. |
| **Examples** | Cristian's algorithm, Berkeley algorithm, and NTP. | Lamport scalar clock and vector clock. |

### 2.3 Lamport's Logical Clock

Lamport's logical clock assigns one increasing integer timestamp to every event. Each process \(P_i\) maintains a scalar counter \(L_i\).

**Implementation rules:**

1. Initially, set \(L_i=0\) at every process.
2. Before each internal or send event at \(P_i\), increment the clock:

   \[
   L_i=L_i+1
   \]

3. When \(P_i\) sends message \(m\), it includes timestamp \(t=L_i\) in the message.
4. When \(P_j\) receives a message carrying timestamp \(t\), it updates its clock before timestamping the receive event:

   \[
   L_j=\max(L_j,t)+1
   \]

These rules satisfy the **clock condition**:

\[
a\rightarrow b \implies L(a)<L(b)
\]

To obtain a total order, two events can be ordered lexicographically by the pair

\[
(L(e),\text{process ID})
\]

The process ID breaks a tie when timestamps are equal. This total order is consistent with causality, but it also imposes an arbitrary order on concurrent events.

**Example:**

| Step | Event | Lamport-clock calculation | Timestamp |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | \(P_1\) performs internal event \(a\) | \(0+1\) | \(L(a)=1\) |
| 2 | \(P_1\) sends \(m_1\) at \(b\) | \(1+1\) | \(L(b)=2\) |
| 3 | \(P_2\) independently performs \(c\) | \(0+1\) | \(L(c)=1\) |
| 4 | \(P_2\) receives \(m_1(2)\) at \(d\) | \(\max(1,2)+1\) | \(L(d)=3\) |
| 5 | \(P_2\) sends reply \(m_2\) at \(e\) | \(3+1\) | \(L(e)=4\) |
| 6 | \(P_1\) receives \(m_2(4)\) at \(f\) | \(\max(2,4)+1\) | \(L(f)=5\) |

The timestamps correctly show \(b\rightarrow d\rightarrow e\rightarrow f\). Events \(b\) and \(c\) are concurrent, even though \(L(c)<L(b)\).

**Advantages:**

- The algorithm is simple and requires only one integer per process and message.
- It produces an ordering that never contradicts the happened-before relation.
- A total order can be produced by adding a process-ID tie-breaker.

**Limitations:**

- The converse of the clock condition is false: \(L(a)<L(b)\) does not prove \(a\rightarrow b\).
- It cannot determine whether two events are causally related or concurrent from timestamps alone.
- It gives no real time, elapsed duration, or physical distance between events.
- Its total ordering unnecessarily orders concurrent events and therefore loses concurrency information.

### 2.4 Vector Clocks

A **vector clock** extends a Lamport clock so that causality and concurrency can be determined from timestamps. In a system of \(N\) processes, each process \(P_i\) maintains a vector

\[
V_i[1\ldots N]
\]

where \(V_i[k]\) is the number of events of \(P_k\) known to \(P_i\). In particular, \(V_i[i]\) counts \(P_i\)'s own events.

**Implementation rules:**

1. Initially, every component is zero: \(V_i[k]=0\) for all \(i,k\).
2. Before an internal or send event at \(P_i\), increment its own component:

   \[
   V_i[i]=V_i[i]+1
   \]

3. Attach a copy of \(V_i\) to every message sent by \(P_i\).
4. When \(P_i\) receives a message with vector \(T\), first merge every component:

   \[
   V_i[k]=\max(V_i[k],T[k]) \quad \text{for } k=1,\ldots,N
   \]

5. Increment the receiver's own component to timestamp the receive event:

   \[
   V_i[i]=V_i[i]+1
   \]

**Comparing vector timestamps:** For vectors \(X\) and \(Y\), define

\[
X<Y \iff (\forall k,\ X[k]\leq Y[k])\ \land\ (\exists k,\ X[k]<Y[k])
\]

Then:

- \(a\rightarrow b\) if and only if \(V(a)<V(b)\).
- \(a\parallel b\) if neither \(V(a)<V(b)\) nor \(V(b)<V(a)\). Their vectors are **incomparable**.

**Example using two processes:**

| Event | Operation | Vector timestamp |
|---|---|---|
| \(a\) at \(P_1\) | Internal event | \([1,0]\) |
| \(b\) at \(P_1\) | Send \(m_1\) | \([2,0]\) |
| \(c\) at \(P_2\) | Independent internal event | \([0,1]\) |
| \(d\) at \(P_2\) | Receive \(m_1[2,0]\); merge, then increment | \([2,2]\) |
| \(e\) at \(P_2\) | Send reply \(m_2\) | \([2,3]\) |
| \(f\) at \(P_1\) | Receive \(m_2[2,3]\); merge, then increment | \([3,3]\) |

Vectors \([2,0]\) for \(b\) and \([0,1]\) for \(c\) are incomparable, so \(b\parallel c\). However, \([2,0]<[2,2]\), so \(b\rightarrow d\).

### 2.5 Lamport Clock versus Vector Clock

| Basis | Lamport clock | Vector clock |
|---|---|---|
| **Timestamp** | One scalar integer. | An array of \(N\) integers. |
| **Guarantee** | \(a\rightarrow b\) implies \(L(a)<L(b)\). | \(a\rightarrow b\) if and only if \(V(a)<V(b)\). |
| **Concurrency detection** | Cannot detect concurrency. | Detects concurrency using incomparable vectors. |
| **Ordering** | Easily gives a total order with a process-ID tie-breaker. | Naturally gives a causal partial order. |
| **Message overhead** | \(O(1)\). | \(O(N)\). |
| **Best use** | Simple ordering, mutual exclusion, and totally ordered logs. | Causal multicast, version tracking, conflict detection, and debugging. |

The main benefit of a vector clock is that it solves Lamport clock's inability to infer causality from timestamps. Its main limitation is that each process and message needs an entry for every process, so storage and communication costs grow with \(N\), and changing group membership is harder to manage.

## 3. Causal Ordering of Messages

### 3.1 Causal Message Order

Messages have **causal order** when every process delivers causally related messages in an order consistent with the happened-before relation. Formally, if

\[
send(m_1)\rightarrow send(m_2)
\]

then every process that delivers both messages must deliver \(m_1\) before \(m_2\). Concurrent messages may be delivered in either order.

**Example:** \(P_1\) multicasts update \(m_1\). After receiving \(m_1\), process \(P_2\) multicasts dependent update \(m_2\). If \(P_3\) receives \(m_2\) first because of network delay, it must buffer \(m_2\), deliver \(m_1\), and then deliver \(m_2\).

### 3.2 Vector-Clock Implementation of Causal Delivery

For causal multicast, vector entry \(V_i[k]\) can count how many messages from \(P_k\) process \(P_i\) has delivered.

1. Before \(P_i\) multicasts a new message, it increments \(V_i[i]\) and attaches the resulting vector \(T\).
2. When \(P_j\) receives a message from \(P_i\), it delivers the message only when both conditions hold:

   \[
   T[i]=V_j[i]+1
   \]

   \[
   T[k]\leq V_j[k] \quad \text{for every } k\neq i
   \]

3. The first condition ensures that no earlier message from the same sender is missing. The second ensures that all messages on which this message causally depends have already been delivered.
4. If either condition is false, \(P_j\) holds the message in a queue and checks it again after delivering other messages.
5. After delivery, \(P_j\) updates its delivered-message vector component-wise:

   \[
   V_j[k]=\max(V_j[k],T[k]) \quad \text{for every } k
   \]

| Ordering guarantee | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **FIFO order** | Preserves the send order of messages from the same sender only. |
| **Causal order** | Preserves the order of all causally related messages, even from different senders. |
| **Total order** | Every receiver delivers all messages in the same order, including concurrent messages. |

## 4. Global State and State Recording

### 4.1 Global State

The **global state** of a distributed system consists of:

1. the local state of every process, and
2. the state of every communication channel, represented by the messages in transit.

For processes \(P_1,\ldots,P_n\) and channels \(C_{ij}\), it can be written as

\[
G=(LS_1,LS_2,\ldots,LS_n,CS_{12},CS_{13},\ldots)
\]

A global state is useful for detecting termination or deadlock, making checkpoints, recovering after failure, evaluating global predicates, and debugging. Simply asking each process for its current state does not guarantee a valid global state because computation and message exchange continue while replies are collected.

### 4.2 Cuts of a Distributed Computation

A **cut** is a boundary drawn across every process timeline. Events to the left of the boundary form the recorded past, while events to the right have not been included. The cut chooses one local state from each process and therefore represents a possible global state.

| Type of cut | Condition | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Consistent cut** | If event \(b\) is included and \(a\rightarrow b\), then \(a\) is also included. | It contains every cause of every recorded effect. A send may be before the cut and its receive after the cut; that message is in transit. |
| **Inconsistent cut** | It includes a receive event but excludes the corresponding send event. | It records an effect without its cause and cannot represent a possible execution state. In a left-to-right spacetime diagram, such a message crosses the cut from right to left. |
| **Strongly consistent cut** | It is consistent and **transitless**: no application message crosses the cut in either direction. | Every recorded send has its receive recorded, so all channel states are empty at the cut. |

For a consistent cut \(C\), the set of included events is closed under happened-before:

\[
b\in C\ \land\ a\rightarrow b \implies a\in C
\]

**How to classify a cut:**

1. Inspect every message arrow that crosses the cut.
2. If its receive is before the cut but its send is after the cut, the cut is inconsistent.
3. If only send-before/receive-after arrows cross, the cut is consistent and those messages form the recorded channel state.
4. If no message arrow crosses and the cut is consistent, it is strongly consistent.

### 4.3 Distributed Snapshot

A **distributed snapshot** records a consistent global state while the system continues to execute. The snapshot contains each process's local state and all messages that were in transit on communication channels at the represented cut. The recorded state may not have existed at one common physical instant, but it represents a state possible under the causal ordering of the execution.

### 4.4 Chandy-Lamport Snapshot Algorithm

The **Chandy-Lamport algorithm** records a consistent snapshot of an asynchronous distributed system by using special **marker messages**. It does not require synchronized physical clocks or stop normal computation.

**Assumptions:**

- Processes do not fail while the snapshot is being recorded.
- Communication channels are reliable, unidirectional, and FIFO ordered.
- Every sent message arrives exactly once.
- There is a directed communication path between every pair of participating processes, so marker information can reach all of them.
- A process sends a marker on an outgoing channel before sending any later application message on that channel.

**Marker-sending rule:**

1. The initiator records its local state.
2. It sends a marker on every outgoing channel before sending any further application message on those channels.
3. It starts recording application messages arriving on each incoming channel until a marker is received on that channel.

**Marker-receiving rule at process \(P_i\):**

- **If this is the first marker received by \(P_i\):**
  1. Record the local state of \(P_i\) before processing later application messages.
  2. Record the state of the incoming channel carrying this first marker as empty. FIFO ordering guarantees that no pre-snapshot message can be behind the marker on that channel.
  3. Send a marker on every outgoing channel before any later application message.
  4. Start recording messages arriving on every other incoming channel.
- **If \(P_i\) has already recorded its local state:**
  1. Stop recording the incoming channel on which the marker arrived.
  2. Record that channel's state as all application messages received after \(P_i\) recorded its local state and before this marker arrived.

**Completion:** A process finishes its part after it has received a marker on every incoming channel. The recorded process states and channel states are then collected, or distributed among processes, to form the global snapshot.

**Why the snapshot is consistent:** A marker separates messages sent before a sender recorded its state from messages sent afterward. FIFO channels preserve this separation. Therefore, any receive included in the snapshot has its send included, while a message sent before the cut but received after the receiver's local-state recording is correctly stored as an in-transit channel message.

**Small example:** Suppose \(P_1\) initiates a snapshot and its marker reaches \(P_2\). Process \(P_2\) records its local state and begins recording messages on channel \(P_3\rightarrow P_2\). If application message \(x\) arrives on that channel before \(P_3\)'s marker, \(x\) is recorded as in transit. When the marker arrives, \(P_2\) stops recording that channel.

## 5. Distributed Debugging

### 5.1 Meaning and Difficulties

**Distributed debugging** is the process of finding faults by observing and reconstructing the states and event relationships of several communicating processes. It is harder than debugging a sequential program because there is no single global clock or complete execution history.

Major difficulties include:

- **Nondeterminism:** Different process schedules and message delays can make the same fault appear differently or disappear on the next run.
- **Clock skew:** Physical timestamps from different machines may give a misleading event order.
- **Partial order:** Concurrent events have no unique correct order.
- **No instantaneous global state:** Reading processes at different times can produce an inconsistent view.
- **Observer effect:** Logging, breakpoints, and network tracing change timing and may hide a race condition.
- **Large event volume:** Many machines can generate more trace data than a debugger can easily inspect.

### 5.2 Distributed Debugging with Logical Time and Snapshots

A practical debugging procedure is:

1. Each process records important local events, message identifiers, send/receive events, and a Lamport or vector timestamp.
2. The logs are collected by a debugger without assuming that raw physical timestamps give the true order.
3. The debugger reconstructs the happened-before partial order. Vector timestamps reveal both causal and concurrent events.
4. Chandy-Lamport snapshots or consistent cuts are used to examine only global states that could have occurred.
5. The debugger evaluates a **global predicate**, such as "all processes are waiting" for deadlock or "two replicas contain different versions" for inconsistency.
6. The causal chain leading to the faulty state is traced backward through message and process dependencies.

A **distributed breakpoint** is a predicate over the states of several processes rather than a single source-code location. The debugger should evaluate it on a consistent cut; otherwise, it may report a condition formed from mutually incompatible local states.

Global predicates may be:

- **Stable predicates:** Once true, they remain true, such as completed termination or permanent deadlock. They are easier to detect from a later consistent snapshot.
- **Transient predicates:** They may become true and then false, such as a temporary safety violation. Detecting them may require retaining event histories and searching several consistent cuts.
