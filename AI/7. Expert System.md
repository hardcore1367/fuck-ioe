## 1. Expert System

An expert system is an **AI program that mimics the decision-making ability of a human expert** in a specific domain. It uses a collection of knowledge (rules, facts) to solve complex problems that would otherwise require human expertise.

For example, a medical expert system can diagnose diseases by applying stored medical knowledge — just like a doctor would.

## 2. Expert System Shell

An expert system shell is a **ready-made framework for building expert systems** — it provides all the structural components of an expert system but **without any domain knowledge filled in**.

Think of it as an empty expert system — the inference engine, user interface, and knowledge base structure are already built. You just need to **fill in the domain-specific rules and facts** to create a working expert system for any field.

For example, the same shell can be used to build a medical diagnosis system or a legal advisory system — only the knowledge base content changes.

## 3. Examples of Expert Systems

**1. DENDRAL**
One of the first expert systems, designed to **identify chemical compounds** from mass spectrometry data. It applied chemistry rules to determine the molecular structure of unknown substances — a task that required expert chemist knowledge.

**2. MYCIN**
Designed for **medical diagnosis** — specifically to diagnose bacterial blood infections and recommend antibiotics. It used around 600 if-then rules and could perform at the level of human specialists. It also explained its reasoning to the user.


## 4. Architecture of Expert System

An expert system is designed to replicate the decision-making ability of a human expert. Its architecture consists of the following six key components:

### 1. Domain Expert
The domain expert is the **human specialist** whose knowledge forms the foundation of the entire system. They possess years of experience in a specific field and the quality of the expert system depends entirely on the quality of knowledge this expert provides.
*Example: An experienced doctor who diagnoses diseases based on symptoms.*

### 2. Knowledge Acquisition
This is the process of **extracting and collecting knowledge from the domain expert**. A knowledge engineer conducts interviews, studies manuals, and observes the expert solving problems, then converts that knowledge into a form the system can use. This is the most difficult step because experts often struggle to express their reasoning explicitly as rules.
*Example: Knowledge engineer asks — "What symptoms make you suspect flu?" and records it as a rule.*

### 3. Knowledge Base
The knowledge base is the **storage of all domain knowledge** collected through acquisition. It contains:
- **Facts** — static truths about the domain ("Flu is caused by a virus")
- **Rules** — conditional logic in if-then form ("IF fever > 102 AND cough = yes THEN diagnosis = flu")

It is the core component the inference engine works with — without a well-built knowledge base the system cannot function.

### 4. Inference Engine
The inference engine is the **reasoning and processing unit** of the expert system. It applies rules from the knowledge base to current inputs and derives conclusions using two strategies:
- **Forward chaining** — begins with known facts and applies rules until a conclusion is reached. Used when the goal is unknown.
- **Backward chaining** — begins with a hypothesis and works backward to verify supporting facts. Used when the goal is already defined.

*Example: Patient has fever and cough → inference engine matches these against rules → concludes "diagnosis = flu."*

### 5. User Interface
The user interface is the **communication layer between the system and the end user**. It allows users to input their problem, receive conclusions, and ask for explanations — all without understanding the internal rules or inference process.
*Example: A patient enters symptoms through a form and receives a diagnosis on screen.*

### 6. Non-Expert User
The non-expert user is the **end user of the system** — a person with no specialist knowledge who needs expert-level answers. They interact through the user interface by describing their problem and receiving conclusions without knowing anything about the rules or reasoning behind it.
*Example: A patient with no medical knowledge gets a diagnosis by simply entering their symptoms.*

### How They Work Together
The domain expert provides knowledge → acquired and encoded into the knowledge base → inference engine applies those rules to user inputs → result is delivered to the non-expert user through the interface. Each component depends on the others — a weakness in any one affects the entire system.


## 5. Steps in Expert System Development

### 1. Problem Identification and Feasibility Study
The first step is to identify the problem the expert system will solve and check whether it is suitable — the domain must be narrow, rule-based, and have an accessible human expert. A feasibility study checks if building the system is technically and economically justified.

### 2. Knowledge Acquisition
A **knowledge engineer** interviews domain experts, studies manuals and case studies to extract relevant knowledge — facts, rules, and heuristics the expert uses to solve problems. This is the most critical and difficult step as experts often struggle to express their knowledge explicitly.

### 3. Knowledge Representation
The acquired knowledge is encoded into a structured form the system can process. Common representations include:
- **If-Then rules** — "IF symptom is fever THEN disease is flu"
- **Semantic networks**
- **Frames and objects**

### 4. Knowledge Inference
The **inference engine** is built to apply rules from the knowledge base to given inputs and reach conclusions. Two strategies are used:
- **Forward chaining** — starts from known facts, derives conclusion
- **Backward chaining** — starts from goal, works backward to verify facts

### 5. Knowledge Transfer
The knowledge extracted from experts is **transferred into the knowledge base** by the knowledge engineer. This involves converting informal expert knowledge into formal rules and facts the system can use. It bridges the gap between human expertise and machine representation.

### 6. User Interface Development
A **user-friendly interface** is built so non-expert users can interact with the system — input data, ask questions, and receive explanations without understanding the internal logic.

### 7. Testing and Validation
The system is tested with real cases whose answers are already known. Outputs are compared against expert decisions to check accuracy and errors are corrected by refining the knowledge base.

### 8. Maintenance and Updation
After deployment, the system is **regularly updated** as domain knowledge evolves. New rules are added and outdated ones removed to keep the system accurate and relevant.


## 6. Expert System vs Human Expert

### 1. Availability
**Human Expert** — available only during working hours, can be on leave, sick, or unavailable during emergencies.
**Expert System** — available 24/7 without breaks.
*Example: A medical diagnosis system can assist a patient at 3AM when no doctor is available.*

### 2. Consistency
**Human Expert** — performance varies with mood, fatigue, stress, and emotional state.
**Expert System** — gives the exact same output for the same input every time.
*Example: A tax expert may make calculation errors on a tiring day — a tax expert system will not.*

### 3. Speed
**Human Expert** — takes time to analyze, recall knowledge, and deliberate before deciding.
**Expert System** — processes rules and reaches conclusions almost instantly.
*Example: A fault detection system in a power plant identifies the fault in milliseconds, while an engineer would take minutes.*

### 4. Knowledge Preservation
**Human Expert** — knowledge is lost when the expert retires, resigns, or passes away.
**Expert System** — knowledge is permanently stored and never lost.
*Example: A senior geologist's 30 years of mineral identification experience can be encoded and preserved forever.*

### 5. Cost
**Human Expert** — hiring and consulting a specialist is expensive, especially repeatedly.
**Expert System** — high initial development cost but very low cost per use afterward.
*Example: Consulting a legal expert for every contract review is costly — a legal expert system does it at near zero recurring cost.*

### 6. Ability to Learn and Adapt
**Human Expert** — learns continuously from new experiences, reads new research, and adapts over time.
**Expert System** — cannot learn on its own; must be manually updated by a knowledge engineer.
*Example: A doctor updates their knowledge from new medical journals automatically — the system cannot.*

### 7. Handling Uncertainty
**Human Expert** — can make reasonable judgments even with incomplete or ambiguous information using intuition and experience.
**Expert System** — struggles with uncertainty; relies on fixed crisp rules and may fail when input is incomplete.
*Example: A doctor can suspect appendicitis even with partial symptoms — a rule-based system may fail to reach any conclusion.*

### 8. Common Sense and Creativity
**Human Expert** — possesses common sense, can think creatively, and handle unexpected situations outside their domain.
**Expert System** — has no common sense; completely confined to its programmed rules.
*Example: A human engineer notices something "feels off" about a machine and investigates — the expert system only responds to what its sensors and rules define.*

## 7. Applications of Expert Systems

**1. Medical Diagnosis**
Used to diagnose diseases and recommend treatment based on symptoms and patient data. Example: MYCIN diagnosed bacterial infections.

**2. Fault Diagnosis**
Used in engineering to detect and identify faults in machines, circuits, or power systems automatically.

**3. Financial and Tax Advisory**
Used by banks and financial institutions for loan approval, fraud detection, and tax calculation based on rules and regulations.

**4. Geological Exploration**
Used to analyze soil and rock data to predict locations of mineral or oil deposits. Example: PROSPECTOR.

**5. Legal Advisory**
Assists in interpreting laws, checking contract compliance, and advising on legal matters within a defined legal domain.



## 8.Advantages / Features

**1. Consistent Performance**
Unlike human experts, it never gets tired, emotional, or distracted — gives the **same quality decision every time**.

**2. Preserves Expert Knowledge**
Human experts retire or leave — an expert system **permanently stores** their knowledge for future use.

**3. Explains Reasoning**
It can show **how it reached a conclusion** via inference chain, making it transparent and trustworthy.

**4. Cost Effective in Long Run**
Once built, it reduces the need to hire expensive human experts repeatedly for routine decisions.

**5. Fast Decision Making**
Processes rules and reaches conclusions much faster than a human expert in complex rule-heavy domains.

## 9. Disadvantages / Limitations

**1. Narrow Domain**
Works only within its specific area — cannot handle problems outside its programmed scope.

**2. Difficult Knowledge Acquisition**
Converting human expertise into if-then rules is **time-consuming and expensive** — experts often struggle to express their knowledge explicitly.

**3. No Learning Ability**
Cannot update itself — knowledge base must be **manually maintained** as domain evolves.

**4. If Expert is Wrong, System is Wrong**
The system is only as good as the knowledge provided. If the human expert makes errors during knowledge encoding, the system will **consistently reproduce those errors**.

**5. Lacks Common Sense**
Cannot reason beyond its rules — fails in situations requiring context, judgment, or real-world understanding.

**6. High Maintenance Cost**
As domain knowledge evolves, rules must be constantly reviewed and updated by a knowledge engineer.



## 10. Characteristics of Problems Solved by Expert Systems

**1. Requires Specialized Knowledge**
The problem needs domain-specific expertise not common to everyone — the kind where you would normally consult a human expert.
Example: Diagnosing a rare disease requires medical expertise.

**2. Well-Defined Domain**
The problem must be limited to a specific area with clear boundaries so that knowledge can be encoded into rules effectively.
Example: Tax calculation follows specific, bounded rules and laws.

**3. Solvable Using Rules and Facts**
The solution process must be expressible as **if-then rules**.
Example: "IF patient has fever AND cough THEN possible diagnosis is flu."

**4. Does Not Require Common Sense**
Works well on **technical, structured problems** but cannot handle situations requiring human judgment.
Example: Fault diagnosis in a machine follows strict technical rules.

**5. Solution is Verifiable**
The output must be checkable against known correct answers so the system can be validated.
Example: Loan approval decisions can be verified against bank policies.

### In Short
Problems that are **narrow, rule-based, knowledge-intensive, and verifiable** are best suited for expert systems.


## 11. Declarative vs Procedural Knowledge

| Declarative Knowledge | Procedural Knowledge |
|---|---|
| Represents **what** something is — facts and truths about the domain | Represents **how** to do something — steps and actions to solve a problem |
| Static in nature — does not change during reasoning | Dynamic in nature — actively used during the reasoning process |
| Stored as facts, assertions, and relationships in the knowledge base | Stored as if-then rules and procedures used by the inference engine |
| Does not trigger any action on its own | Directly guides the system toward a conclusion or action |
| Example: "Flu is a viral disease caused by influenza virus" | Example: "IF fever > 102 AND cough = yes THEN diagnose flu AND prescribe antiviral" |
