## 1. MARR

**MARR (Minimum Attractive Rate of Return)** is the minimum rate of return a company or investor is willing to accept on an investment before it is considered worthwhile. Any project with a return below MARR is rejected.

**Determining Factors of MARR:**
- **Cost of capital** — the rate at which the firm borrows or raises funds (debt + equity)
- **Risk involved** — higher project risk demands a higher MARR
- **Opportunity cost** — returns available from alternative investments
- **Inflation rate** — MARR must account for the erosion of purchasing power
- **Market interest rates** — prevailing rates in the financial market set a baseline
- **Availability of capital** — scarce capital raises the threshold
- **Tax considerations** — after-tax returns influence the effective MARR
- **Organizational policy** — management may set a corporate-wide MARR floor

## 2. Advantages & Limitations of the Payback Period Method

The **Payback Period** is the time required for cumulative cash inflows to recover the initial investment.

**Advantages:**
- Simple and easy to calculate and understand
- Useful as a quick screening tool for risky or short-lived projects
- Preferred when liquidity is a priority (recover money fast)
- Requires minimal data — suitable for early-stage analysis

**Limitations:**
- Ignores the **time value of money** (unless discounted payback is used)
- Ignores all cash flows **after** the payback period — profitability is overlooked
- Does not measure overall project worth or rate of return
- Biased toward **short-term** projects; may reject good long-term investments
- Gives no information about the **magnitude** of returns

## 3. Absolute vs Relative Measures of Economic Analysis

| Feature | Absolute Measures | Relative Measures |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | Express project worth in monetary units | Express project worth as a ratio or percentage |
| **Examples** | NPV, Net Annual Worth, Net Future Worth | IRR, BCR (Benefit-Cost Ratio), ROI |
| **What it shows** | *How much* value is created (in $) | *How efficiently* capital is used |
| **Best used when** | Comparing projects of similar scale | Comparing projects of different sizes/budgets |
| **Limitation** | Ignores scale of investment | Can be misleading for mutually exclusive projects |

> **Key Rule:** For mutually exclusive projects, always rely on **absolute measures** (e.g., NPV). Relative measures like IRR can give conflicting rankings.

## 4. Differentiate Financial vs Economic Analysis

| Basis | Financial Analysis | Economic Analysis |
|---|---|---|
| **Perspective** | Private entity (firm/investor) | Society / nation as a whole |
| **Prices used** | Market prices | Shadow prices / economic prices |
| **Goal** | Maximize profit / financial return | Maximize social welfare / net benefit |
| **Taxes & subsidies** | Included as real costs/benefits | Excluded (treated as transfers) |
| **Externalities** | Generally ignored | Included (pollution, employment, etc.) |
| **Stakeholders** | Shareholders, lenders | General public, government |
| **Used by** | Private companies, banks | Governments, development agencies (World Bank, ADB) |
| **Discount rate** | MARR or cost of capital | Social discount rate |

> Financial analysis asks *"Is it profitable for me?"* Economic analysis asks *"Is it good for society?"*


## 5. Introduction to Lifecycle Costing (LCC)

**Lifecycle Costing** is an economic method that considers **all costs** associated with an asset or project from its conception to disposal — not just the initial purchase price.

**Phases of a Lifecycle:**
1. **Acquisition/Initial cost** — design, procurement, installation
2. **Operation costs** — energy, labor, consumables
3. **Maintenance costs** — scheduled and unscheduled repairs
4. **Downtime costs** — lost production during failures
5. **End-of-life costs** — disposal, decommissioning, salvage value

**Why LCC matters:**
- Initial cost is often a small fraction of total lifecycle cost
- Helps in comparing alternatives that have different cost profiles over time
- Promotes better long-term decision-making
- Common in public infrastructure, defense, and engineering projects
