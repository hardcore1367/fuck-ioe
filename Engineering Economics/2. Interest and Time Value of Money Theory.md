| Nominal Rate | Effective Rate |
|---|---|
| The **advertised** annual interest rate given by banks or contracts | The **actual** interest rate earned or paid after accounting for compounding |
| Does **not** consider how frequently interest is compounded within the year | Fully accounts for **compounding frequency** within the period |
| Remains the same regardless of how often compounding occurs | **Increases** as compounding frequency increases |
| Always **less than or equal to** the effective rate | Always **greater than or equal to** the nominal rate |
| Cannot be directly used in time value calculations | Must be used in all **actual financial calculations** |

## Time Value of Money

The time value of money is a financial concept that states that a sum of money is worth more now than the same amount in the future due to its potential earning capacity.

---

| Simple Interest | Compound Interest |
|---|---|
| Interest is calculated on the **original principal only** | Interest is calculated on **principal + previously accumulated interest** |
| Interest amount stays **constant** every period | Interest amount **grows** every period |
| Does **not** reinvest earned interest | **Reinvests** earned interest back into the principal |
| Growth is **linear** over time | Growth is **exponential** over time |

## Factors to consider in calculating time value of money
- **P** (Present Value) — the initial amount of money at time zero
- **F** (Future Value) — the amount to be received or paid at a future date
- **A** (Annual/Uniform Amount) — equal payments occurring every period
- **i** (Interest Rate) — rate at which money grows per compounding period
- **n** (Number of Periods) — total time periods (years, months, etc.)
- **Compounding Frequency** — how often interest is applied (annually, monthly, continuously)
- **Inflation** — reduces the real value of money over time
- **Risk** — uncertain future cash flows must be discounted more heavily
> Tips: Remember the PW, AW, FW etc formula.

## Continuous compounding
**Continuous compounding** is a situation where interest is compounded **infinitely many times** per year. In normal compounding, interest is added at fixed intervals — monthly, daily, etc. But in continuous compounding, we imagine interest being added at **every instant**, which gives the maximum possible return for a given nominal rate.

As compounding frequency m increases in the formula $i = (1 + r/m)^m - 1$, when m → ∞, this expression converges to:

$$i_{eff} = e^r - 1$$

The future and present value formulas become:

$$F = P \cdot e^{rn} \qquad P = F \cdot e^{-rn}$$

where r = nominal rate, n = number of years, and e = 2.71828.

**Example:** Rs.1000 invested at 10% nominal rate for 1 year under continuous compounding gives $F = 1000 \cdot e^{0.10} = \textbf{Rs.1105.17}$, compared to Rs.1100 under simple annual compounding — a small but real difference that becomes significant over long periods.
